Ratio Analysis — Interpretation & Industry Identification

Using ratios to identify industries, benchmark against peers, and understand the limitations that every analyst must navigate.

T1 Chapters 16-17 (Maheswari)Comprehensive Exam~40 min read
Real-World Problem

Can you tell a bank from an FMCG company just by looking at financial ratios?

A company with a 1% net margin and 15x equity multiplier. Another with 20% net margin and 1.5x equity multiplier. Which is HDFC Bank and which is Hindustan Unilever? The ratios themselves tell you the industry — this is the power of ratio-based industry identification.

Understanding how ratios cluster by industry is a powerful analytical skill tested in BITS exams and used daily by equity analysts, credit officers, and management consultants.

Exam Tip: BITS loves this type of question — giving you a table of 5-6 unnamed companies' ratios and asking you to match them to industries. Focus on: (1) Equity multiplier (banks are extreme), (2) Net margin (IT/pharma are high; banks/retail are low), (3) Asset turnover (retail is high; infra is low), (4) Inventory (zero for services/banking).

2 Trend Analysis & Benchmarking Intermediate

A ratio in isolation is meaningless. It needs context through:

Trend Analysis (Time Series)

  • Compare a company's ratios across 3-5 years
  • Is ROE improving or declining?
  • Is D/E going up (more risky) or down?
  • Base year = 100, index subsequent years

Benchmarking (Cross-Section)

  • Compare vs direct competitors
  • Compare vs industry average
  • Use screener.in / Bloomberg for Indian data
  • Same accounting policies needed for fair comparison

3 Limitations of Ratio Analysis Intermediate

LimitationExplanationExample
Historical dataRatios reflect the past, not the futureJet Airways had decent ratios in 2017 but collapsed by 2019
Inflation distortionHistorical cost accounting understates current valuesLand bought in 1990 at ₹1 crore vs current value ₹500 crore
Window dressingYear-end transactions designed to flatter ratiosSelling inventory on 31 March and buying back on 1 April
Accounting policy differencesDifferent depreciation methods, inventory valuation, etc.FIFO vs weighted average changes inventory turnover ratio
Single ratio misleadingMust interpret ratios in groupsHigh current ratio may mean excess idle inventory (bad, not good)
Trending Example (2024)

Startup IPOs in India (Paytm, Nykaa, Zomato) challenged traditional ratio analysis. These companies had negative ROE, no dividends, and sky-high P/E ratios — yet received massive valuations based on growth metrics (GMV, user base, revenue CAGR) that traditional ratios do not capture. This is a real limitation of classical ratio analysis for new-age businesses.

Session 9 — Key Takeaways

  • Industry identification from ratios is a key analytical skill
  • Banks = high leverage, thin margin; FMCG = high turnover, negative WC; IT = high margin, asset-light
  • Trend analysis (time-series) and benchmarking (cross-section) provide context
  • 5 limitations: historical, inflation, window-dressing, policy differences, single-ratio trap
  • Always use multiple ratios together; never rely on one number
