GAAP & Accounting Principles

Every financial statement follows a philosophy — a set of rules that determine how transactions are recorded. Master these rules and you will never be fooled by a company's numbers.

Real-World Problem

Satyam Computers: How accounting principles failed — and why they matter

In 2009, Satyam's chairman Ramalinga Raju confessed to inflating cash balances by ₹5,040 crore and overstating revenues for years. The fraud exploited gaps in how accounting rules were applied:

  • The entity concept was violated — personal expenses were charged to the company.
  • Revenue recognition was manipulated — fake invoices created sales that never happened.
  • The conservatism convention was ignored — known losses were hidden.
  • Full disclosure failed — material information was suppressed from auditors.
Accounting principles are not abstract philosophy. They are the guardrails that prevent fraud, ensure comparability, and protect investors. Understanding them helps you spot red flags before they become headlines.

1 Accounting Concepts (The "Why") Beginner

Concepts are the fundamental assumptions on which the entire accounting framework rests. There are 9 key concepts:

#ConceptWhat It MeansExample
1Business EntityThe business is separate from its owner. Owner's personal transactions are NOT the business's transactions.Mukesh Ambani buys a painting for ₹50 crore — it does NOT go on Reliance's books.
2Money MeasurementOnly transactions that can be expressed in money are recorded.A company's amazing culture or brilliant CEO cannot be recorded as an asset.
3Going ConcernThe business will continue to operate indefinitely (not shutting down tomorrow).This justifies recording assets at historical cost, not liquidation (fire-sale) value.
4Cost (Historical)Assets are recorded at acquisition cost, not current market value.Tata bought Jaguar Land Rover for $2.3B in 2008 — on B/S at cost, not today's value.
5Dual AspectEvery transaction has two effects: a debit and a credit. Foundation of double-entry.Buy goods for ₹10,000 cash: Stock +₹10K (Dr), Cash -₹10K (Cr).
6Accounting PeriodFinancial life is divided into regular intervals (usually April–March in India).Even if a building lasts 50 years, we report its impact year by year via depreciation.
7RealisationRevenue is recognised when earned (goods delivered/service rendered), not when cash received.Infosys bills a client ₹5 crore for consulting done in March — recognised in March, even if paid in May.
8MatchingExpenses must be matched with the revenue they helped generate in the same period.If Maruti sells a car in March, the steel and labour cost of that car must also hit March's P&L.
9AccrualRecord events when they occur, not when cash flows.Rent due for March but paid in April — record the expense in March.
Daily Life Example

Entity Concept: You and your wallet are different entities. If you lend ₹5,000 to your friend from personal savings, your freelance "business" should NOT show this as an expense.

Matching Concept: You spend ₹2,000 on ingredients today to cater a party tomorrow for ₹8,000. The ₹2,000 expense should be matched against the ₹8,000 revenue in the same period.

The Accounting Equation (from Dual Aspect): Assets = Liabilities + Equity

2 Accounting Conventions (The "How") Beginner

While concepts are the assumptions, conventions are the practical customs that guide application:

Consistency

Use the same accounting methods across periods.

  • If you use Straight-Line Depreciation in Year 1, do not switch to WDV in Year 2 without disclosure
  • Enables meaningful trend analysis
  • Changes must be disclosed in notes

Conservatism (Prudence)

Anticipate all possible losses, never anticipate gains.

  • Write down inventory if NRV < cost
  • Do not record expected profit until actually earned
  • Provision for doubtful debts even if not yet bad

Full Disclosure

All material information must be disclosed in financial statements or notes.

  • Contingent liabilities (lawsuits pending)
  • Related-party transactions
  • Change in accounting policy

Materiality

Only significant items need separate treatment.

  • A ₹500 stapler for TCS (₹2.4L crore revenue) — expense immediately, do not depreciate!
  • But ₹500 crore for TCS — definitely material, needs detailed disclosure
Trending Example (2023-25)

Adani Group (2023): When the Hindenburg report alleged accounting irregularities, analysts scrutinised whether Adani had violated the full disclosure convention by not adequately disclosing related-party transactions through offshore entities. The conservatism principle was also questioned — were asset valuations overly aggressive? The stock fell 60%+ in days. This shows how accounting conventions are not just theory — they are what investors trust and markets price.

3 Why Principles Matter: Bias & Manipulation Intermediate

Accounting rules permit judgement in many areas. This creates room for management to introduce bias:

Red FlagWhich Principle is Bent?What Management Does
Aggressive Revenue RecognitionRealisation, MatchingRecord revenue before delivery; channel stuffing at quarter-end
Capitalising Operating CostsMatching, CostRecord repairs as assets instead of expenses — inflates profit
Off-Balance-Sheet FinancingFull Disclosure, EntityUse SPVs to hide debt from the balance sheet
Cookie Jar ReservesConservatism (abused)Over-provision in good years, release provisions in bad years
Frequent "One-Time" ChargesMatching, MaterialityClassify recurring costs as "exceptional" to keep operating profit clean
Exam Tip: A typical 5-10 mark question will describe a business scenario and ask you to identify which accounting concepts and conventions are being applied (or violated). Practice by reading Indian business news and mapping events to principles.

Daily Life Example

Conservatism at home: Your uncle promises to give you ₹1 lakh as a gift (not certain). Your employer says your salary will be ₹80,000 this month (certain). Under conservatism, you plan your budget assuming only the ₹80K. You do not count the uncle's promise until the money is actually in your account.

Session 2 — Key Takeaways

  • 9 Accounting Concepts: Entity, Money Measurement, Going Concern, Historical Cost, Dual Aspect, Accounting Period, Realisation, Matching, Accrual
  • 4 Conventions: Consistency, Conservatism, Full Disclosure, Materiality
  • Concepts = "Why" (fundamental assumptions); Conventions = "How" (practical customs)
  • Dual Aspect gives us the Accounting Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity
  • Accrual vs Cash: Record when event occurs, not when cash moves
  • Matching: Match expense to the revenue it generated in the same period
  • Red flags emerge when management bends these principles
