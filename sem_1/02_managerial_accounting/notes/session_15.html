CVP Analysis & Managerial Decision-Making

Break-even, contribution margin, make-or-buy, special orders, and inventory valuation — the quantitative tools for managerial decisions.

T1 Chapters 22-23 (Maheswari)Comprehensive Exam~55 min read
Real-World Problem

How many Vada Pavs must Goli Vada Pav sell per day to cover its costs?

Goli Vada Pav has fixed costs (rent, staff, equipment depreciation) of about ₹3 lakh per month per outlet. Each vada pav sells for ₹50 and costs ₹20 in variable ingredients and packaging. How many vada pavs must they sell just to break even? This is CVP analysis in action.

CVP (Cost-Volume-Profit) analysis answers the most fundamental business question: How many units must I sell to make a profit? And what happens to profit if volume, price, or costs change?

1 Contribution Margin: The Key Concept Beginner

Contribution Margin (per unit) = Selling Price - Variable Cost per Unit
Contribution Margin Ratio (P/V Ratio) = CM per unit / Selling Price x 100%

Contribution margin is what each unit "contributes" towards covering fixed costs and then generating profit.

Goli Vada Pav Example

ItemAmount (₹)
Selling Price per Vada Pav50
Variable Cost per unit20
Contribution Margin per unit30
P/V Ratio30/50 = 60%

Every vada pav sold contributes ₹30 towards paying off the ₹3 lakh monthly fixed costs.

2 Break-Even Analysis Intermediate

Break-Even (units) = Fixed Costs / Contribution Margin per unit
Break-Even (₹) = Fixed Costs / P/V Ratio

Goli Vada Pav Break-Even

CalculationResult
BE Units = 3,00,000 / 3010,000 vada pavs/month (or ~333/day)
BE Sales = 3,00,000 / 0.60₹5,00,000/month

If they sell fewer than 333 vada pavs per day, they lose money. Every vada pav beyond 333 generates ₹30 pure profit.

Margin of Safety = Actual Sales - Break-Even Sales
Margin of Safety % = (Actual Sales - BE Sales) / Actual Sales x 100%

If Goli actually sells 15,000 vada pavs/month:

MetricCalculationResult
Actual Sales15,000 x ₹50₹7,50,000
BE Sales₹5,00,000
Margin of Safety7,50,000 - 5,00,000₹2,50,000 (33.3%)
Profit(15,000 - 10,000) x ₹30₹1,50,000
Target Profit Units = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) / CM per unit

3 Decision-Making with CVP Advanced

Make-or-Buy Decision

Scenario: A company can make a component for ₹80/unit (variable ₹50 + allocated fixed ₹30). A supplier offers it at ₹65/unit. Should they buy?

OptionRelevant Cost per Unit
Make (only variable + avoidable fixed)₹50 (if fixed costs are unavoidable, they stay regardless)
Buy₹65

Decision: MAKE (₹50 < ₹65). The ₹30 fixed cost is sunk — it will be incurred whether we make or buy. Only compare relevant costs.

Special Order Decision

Scenario: A hotel offers to buy 500 cakes at ₹150 each (normal price ₹250). Variable cost is ₹120/cake. Factory has spare capacity.

FactorAnalysis
Special price₹150
Variable cost₹120
Contribution per unit₹30 (positive!)
Total extra contribution500 x ₹30 = ₹15,000

Decision: ACCEPT — as long as (1) spare capacity exists, (2) it does not affect regular customers, and (3) it is a one-time order.

Operating Leverage = Contribution / EBIT

High operating leverage means small revenue changes cause large profit swings — risky but rewarding when revenue grows.

Trending Example (2024-25)

IndiGo Airlines has very high operating leverage (high fixed costs: aircraft leases, crew salaries). When passenger loads are high (90%+), IndiGo is extremely profitable. But if load factor drops to 75%, the same fixed costs turn profits into losses rapidly. This is exactly why CVP analysis matters — airlines, hotels, and cinema halls all have high fixed costs and must obsess over their break-even load/occupancy.

Exam Tip: CVP is the most numerically tested topic in the comprehensive exam. Expect: (1) Break-even calculation, (2) Target profit, (3) Make-or-buy decision, (4) Special order pricing. Practice at least 10 problems. Also remember: LIFO is banned under IFRS/Ind-AS — this is a favourite MCQ question.

Session 15 — Key Takeaways

  • CM per unit = SP - VC; P/V Ratio = CM/SP
  • Break-even (units) = FC / CM per unit; Break-even (₹) = FC / PV Ratio
  • Margin of Safety = Actual Sales - BE Sales (higher is safer)
  • Target Profit = (FC + Target Profit) / CM per unit
  • Make-or-Buy: Compare only relevant costs (variable + avoidable fixed)
  • Special Order: Accept if price > variable cost (spare capacity assumed)
  • Operating Leverage = Contribution / EBIT (high = more risk & reward)
  • Inventory: FIFO & Weighted Avg allowed under IFRS; LIFO prohibited
