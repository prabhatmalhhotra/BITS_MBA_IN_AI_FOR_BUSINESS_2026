Trial Balance, Adjustments & Financial Statements

From raw journal entries to the final P&L and Balance Sheet — the complete journey of turning transactions into meaningful financial reports.

T1 Chapters 6-7 (Maheswari) Mid-Sem Syllabus ~50 min read
Real-World Problem

Why does Amul's annual report look different from what their daily cash register shows?

Amul sells ₹50,000+ crore worth of dairy products annually. Their daily cash register tracks cash received and paid. But their annual report shows many items that have nothing to do with cash:

  • Depreciation on dairy plants (no cash spent this year, but equipment is wearing out)
  • Outstanding expenses (electricity bill for March arrives in April — but expense belongs to March)
  • Provision for bad debts (some retailers may not pay their dues)
  • Closing stock (unsold butter and cheese sitting in cold storage)
The trial balance captures raw ledger data. Adjustments transform it into accurate financial statements that follow the matching and accrual principles we learned in Session 2.

1 Trial Balance Beginner

A Trial Balance is a list of all ledger account balances at a given date. It checks whether total debits equal total credits.

Worked Example: Priya's Bakery Trial Balance (30 April 2025)

AccountDebit (₹)Credit (₹)
Cash2,58,000
Equipment80,000
Purchases40,000
Rent Expense15,000
Electricity Expense5,000
Salary Expense12,000
Capital3,00,000
Sales1,10,000
TOTAL4,10,0004,10,000

Debits = Credits = ₹4,10,000. The trial balance "balances"!

Errors NOT Detected by Trial Balance

Error TypeWhat HappenedWhy TB Still Balances
Error of OmissionTransaction completely forgotten — not recorded at allBoth Dr and Cr missing equally
Error of CommissionCorrect amount, correct side, but wrong account (debited A instead of B)Total Dr unchanged
Error of PrincipleDebited Furniture A/c instead of Repairs A/c (capital vs revenue confusion)Total Dr unchanged
Compensating ErrorOne account overstated by ₹5K, another understated by ₹5KErrors cancel each other
Exam Tip: A classic exam question: "Trial Balance agrees but the books are still incorrect. Explain." List these 4 error types with examples. Each is worth marks.

2 Adjusting Entries Intermediate

Before preparing P&L and B/S, we must adjust for items not yet recorded. These follow the accrual and matching principles:

AdjustmentJournal EntryEffect on P&LEffect on B/S
Outstanding Expense
(Rent due but unpaid)		Rent A/c Dr
To Outstanding Rent A/c		Expense increases → Profit decreasesLiability increases (Current)
Prepaid Expense
(Insurance paid for 12 months, only 3 months used)		Prepaid Insurance A/c Dr
To Insurance A/c		Expense decreases → Profit increasesAsset increases (Current)
Accrued Income
(Interest earned but not yet received)		Accrued Interest A/c Dr
To Interest Income A/c		Income increases → Profit increasesAsset increases (Current)
Depreciation
(Equipment wearing out)		Depreciation A/c Dr
To Equipment A/c		Expense increases → Profit decreasesAsset decreases (Non-current)
Bad Debts
(Customer will never pay)		Bad Debts A/c Dr
To Debtors A/c		Expense increases → Profit decreasesAsset decreases (Current)
Closing Stock
(Unsold goods at year-end)		Closing Stock A/c Dr
To Trading A/c		Reduces COGS → Profit increasesAsset increases (Current)
Daily Life Example

Prepaid expense: You pay ₹12,000 for a yearly gym membership in January. By March, you have used 3 months = ₹3,000. The remaining ₹9,000 is a "prepaid expense" (an asset — you have 9 months of gym still owed to you). You only charge ₹3,000 as expense for the Jan-Mar quarter.

Trending Example (2024-25)

D-Mart (Avenue Supermarts) consistently reports closing stock of ₹4,000-5,000 crore on its balance sheet — that is unsold groceries, clothes, and household items sitting in their warehouses and stores. This single adjustment (closing stock) converts what would look like a massive "purchase expense" into an asset, dramatically changing the reported profit. Without it, D-Mart's COGS would be overstated and profit understated by thousands of crores.

4 Cash vs Profit — Why Both Matter Advanced

"Cash is King" — A profitable company can go bankrupt if it runs out of cash.

Profit (P&L)

  • Based on accrual accounting
  • Includes non-cash items (depreciation, provisions)
  • Revenue recognised even if cash not received
  • Can be manipulated via accounting choices
  • "Profit is opinion"

Cash (Cash Flow Statement)

  • Based on actual cash movements
  • Cannot include non-cash items
  • Only counts money actually in/out of bank
  • Much harder to manipulate
  • "Cash is fact"
Daily Life Example

Your freelance business billed ₹5 lakh in March (profitable month!). But clients have not paid yet. Your rent, food, and EMI are due April 1st. You are "profitable" but cash-strapped. This is exactly why businesses track both profit AND cash flow.

Session 4 — Key Takeaways

  • Trial Balance = list of all ledger balances; Total Dr must equal Total Cr
  • 4 errors not caught by TB: Omission, Commission, Principle, Compensating
  • Adjustments: Outstanding expenses, prepaid expenses, accrued income, depreciation, bad debts, closing stock
  • COGS = Opening Stock + Purchases - Closing Stock
  • Trading A/c gives Gross Profit; P&L A/c gives Net Profit; B/S shows financial position
  • Net Profit flows from P&L into Capital on B/S (connecting the statements)
  • "Profit is opinion, Cash is fact" — always reconcile both
