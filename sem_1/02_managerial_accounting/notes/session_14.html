Cost Accounting: Cost Classification & Cost Sheet

Understanding cost behaviour — fixed, variable, direct, indirect — and building cost sheets that reveal the true cost of products.

T1 Chapters 22-23 (Maheswari)Comprehensive Exam~45 min read
Real-World Problem

Why does a Maruti Suzuki factory run even when demand drops 20%?

In an economic slowdown, car sales may drop 20%. But Maruti keeps its factories running at reduced capacity rather than shutting them. Why? Because fixed costs (factory rent, permanent salaries, depreciation on machinery) continue whether or not a single car is produced. Understanding cost behaviour — which costs are fixed and which are variable — is the foundation of every production, pricing, and shutdown decision in business.

Cost accounting moves beyond "what happened" (financial accounting) to "what should we do?" (managerial decision-making). This is where accounting becomes truly powerful for managers.

1 Cost Classification Beginner

By Behaviour (Most Important!)

TypeTotal BehaviourPer Unit BehaviourExample
FixedConstant regardless of outputDecreases as output increasesFactory rent ₹5 lakh/month, CEO salary, insurance
VariableChanges proportionally with outputConstant per unitRaw material per car, packaging, delivery fuel
Semi-VariableHas both fixed and variable componentsMixedElectricity (base charge + per-unit), phone (rental + usage)
Step FixedFixed within a range, then jumpsDrops then jumpsNeed a new supervisor for every 20 workers

By Other Classifications

  • COST CLASSIFICATIONS
    • By FunctionProduction, Selling & Distribution, Administration, R&D
    • By IdentifiabilityDirect (traceable to product) vs Indirect (overheads)
    • By Decision RelevanceRelevant (future, differential) vs Sunk (already incurred)
    • By ElementMaterial, Labour, Overheads
Daily Life Example

Running a chai stall:
Fixed costs: Stall rent ₹5,000/month, gas cylinder connection ₹500/month
Variable costs: Tea leaves ₹3/cup, milk ₹4/cup, sugar ₹1/cup, cup ₹2/cup = ₹10/cup
Whether you sell 0 cups or 500 cups, rent stays ₹5,000. But material cost is ₹0 for 0 cups and ₹5,000 for 500 cups.

2 Cost Sheet Intermediate

Direct Material + Direct Labour + Direct Expenses
= PRIME COST
▼ + Factory Overheads
= WORKS COST (Factory Cost)
▼ + Office & Admin Overheads
= COST OF PRODUCTION
▼ + Selling & Distribution Overheads
= COST OF SALES (Total Cost)
▼ + Profit
= SALES
Prime Cost = Direct Material + Direct Labour + Direct Expenses
Conversion Cost = Direct Labour + Manufacturing Overhead
COGS = Opening Stock + Cost of Production - Closing Stock

Session 14 — Key Takeaways

  • Cost behaviour: Fixed (constant total), Variable (constant per unit), Semi-variable, Step
  • Classifications: By function, identifiability, decision relevance, element
  • Cost sheet hierarchy: Prime Cost → Works Cost → Cost of Production → Cost of Sales
  • Prime Cost = DM + DL + DE; Conversion Cost = DL + Manufacturing OH
  • Fixed vs Variable distinction is crucial for CVP analysis (next session)
