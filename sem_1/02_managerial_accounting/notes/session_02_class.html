2 Business Entity, Going Concern & Money Measurement Beginner

1. Business Entity Concept

A business is treated as a separate entity that is distinct from its owner(s) and hence a distinction should be made between:

  • (i) Personal transactions and business transactions, and
  • (ii) Transactions of one business entity and those of another business entity
Example: X owns a personal residence costing ₹6,00,000 (market value ₹9,00,000). During preparation of the business financial statements, this property was ignored and not shown. → Business Entity Concept (personal assets are separate from business assets).

2. Going Concern Concept

The enterprise is normally viewed as a going concern, i.e. as continuing operations for the foreseeable future. It is assumed that the enterprise has neither the intention nor the necessity of liquidation.

Examples from professor's slides:
• RIL — Dhirubai Ambani: The founder passed away, but Reliance Industries Limited continues to operate.
• Infosys — Narayana Murthy: The founder stepped back, but the company continues as a going concern.

3. Money Measurement Concept

Only those transactions which are capable of being expressed in terms of money are included in the accounting records.

What CANNOT be recorded (not quantifiable in money):

  • Human Capital — RCB (cricket team), Barcelona-Messi, Narayana Hrudayalaya - Dr. Devi Prasad Shetty
  • Employee loyalty
  • Customer satisfaction
  • Company reputation
  • Working conditions
  • Employee morale
These are invaluable to the business but cannot appear on the Balance Sheet because they cannot be objectively measured in monetary terms.

3 Cost, Dual Aspect & Accounting Period Beginner

4. Cost Concept

An asset is recorded in the accounting records at the price paid to acquire it at the time of its acquisition (Cost).

  • The cost becomes the basis for the accounts during subsequent accounting periods (objectively verifiable)
  • The cost of an asset is systematically reduced from year to year by charging depreciation and the asset is shown in the Balance Sheet at Book Value
  • Closing stock is always valued at cost price or market price, whichever is less (in P&L statement)

5. Dual Aspect Concept

Two-fold aspect of a transaction is called dual aspect or duality of a transaction. This duality is the basis of double entry records.

As the name implies, the entry made for each transaction is composed of two parts — one for debit and another for credit.

This is the Basis of Accounting. Every transaction affects at least two accounts. If you buy inventory for cash: Inventory (Debit) increases, Cash (Credit) decreases.

6. Accounting Period Concept

Also known as Periodicity Principle or Time Period Principle.

According to this principle, the economic life of an enterprise is artificially split into periodic intervals which are known as accounting periods (annual/quarterly), at the end of which an Income Statement and Balance Sheet are prepared to ascertain the Financial Performance and position respectively.

4 Conservatism Concept (Prudence Principle) Intermediate

7. Conservatism Concept

Also called the Prudence Principle, this concept states that the business should:

"Anticipate no profit but provide for all probable losses"

It requires that in situations of uncertainty and doubt, business transactions should be recorded in such a manner that the Profits and Assets are not overstated and the Losses and Liabilities are not understated.

Applications of Conservatism

  • Provision for Doubtful Debts and Discount on Debtors — even if we are not sure if debts will go bad, we make a provision
  • Closing stock is always valued at cost price or market price, whichever is less — this is based on the principle of Conservatism

5 Realization & Accrual Concept + IFRS 15 / Ind AS 115 Advanced

8a. Accrual Concept

Revenues and Costs are accrued, that is, recognised as they are earned or incurred (and not as money is received or paid) and recorded in the financial statements of the periods to which they relate.

For companies it is mandatory to keep accounts on accrual basis under the Companies Act, 2013.

8b. Realization Concept / Revenue Recognition Concept

We recognize the revenue from sale of goods or provision of services when such goods are delivered or the services are rendered to customers (transfer of property in goods), not when cash is received.

Revenue = recognised when goods delivered / services rendered, NOT when cash is received

IFRS 15 / Ind AS 115 — Revenue Recognition Rules

According to IFRS rules for revenue recognition:

  1. Identify the customer contract — Contract exists if parties have approved it (written / oral / implied)
  2. Identify the obligations in the customer contract
  3. When control of the promised good or service transfers to the customer (legal title / physical possession / risk and reward / acceptance)
  4. Over time vs point-in-time recognition

Over Time vs Point-in-Time Recognition

Revenue is recognised over time if ANY one of the following is met:

  • Customer simultaneously receives and consumes benefits (house cleaning / internet)
  • Customer controls the asset as it is created or enhanced (house construction)
  • Asset has no alternative use AND entity has enforceable right to payment (customized products)

Otherwise — point in time recognition (e.g., sale of goods).

Exam Tip: IFRS 15 / Ind AS 115 revenue recognition is a high-weightage topic. Remember the 4 steps and the 3 conditions for over-time recognition. The professor specifically emphasized the distinction between over-time (services, construction) and point-in-time (sale of goods).

6 Matching Concept Intermediate

9. Matching Concept

The expenses incurred in an accounting period should be matched with the revenues recognised in that period. It means that, if revenue is recognised on all goods sold during a period, cost of those goods sold should also be charged to that period.

It is wrong to recognise revenue on all sales, but charge expenses only on such sales as are collected in cash till that period.

This concept is based on accrual concept since it disregards the timing and the amount of actual cash inflow or cash outflow and concentrates on the occurrence (i.e. accrual) of revenue and expenses.

7 Consistency Concept Beginner

10. Consistency Concept

It is assumed that accounting policies are consistent from one period to another. Whatever accounting practices are selected for a given category of transactions, they should be followed on a horizontal basis from one accounting period to another to achieve comparability.

Examples from Slides

  • If the inventory is valued on LIFO basis, this basis should be followed year after year
  • If a particular asset is depreciated according to WDV (Written Down Value) method, this method should be followed year after year

8 Full Disclosure & Materiality Concepts Intermediate

11. Full Disclosure Concept

The financial statements must disclose all the relevant and reliable information which they purport to represent, so that the information may be useful for the user and they can make a correct assessment about the financial performance and position of the enterprise.

12. Materiality Concept

All relatively relevant items, the knowledge of which might influence the decision of the user of the financial statements, should be disclosed in the financial statements.

  • Which information is more relevant than other is largely a matter of judgement
  • Materiality depends upon the amount of item, the size of business, level and nature of information

Relationship: Full Disclosure vs Materiality

Materiality is basically an exception to the Full Disclosure Principle.

The full disclosure principle requires that all facts necessary to ensure that the financial statements are not misleading must be disclosed, whereas the materiality principle requires that items or events having an insignificant economic effect or not being relevant to the user's need need not be disclosed.

Q1. X owns a personal residence that cost ₹6,00,000 but has a market value of ₹9,00,000. During preparation of the financial statement for the business, the entire value of property was ignored and was not shown in the financial statements. Which accounting concept was followed?
Answer: Business Entity Concept
Q2. Appending notes to the financial statements.
Answer: Full Disclosure Concept
Q3. Accounting of a small calculator as an expense and not as an asset.
Answer: Materiality Concept
Q4. Following the WDV method of depreciating a particular asset year after year.
Answer: Consistency Concept
Q5. Valuation of stock at lower of cost or net realisable value.
Answer: Conservatism Concept
Q6. Making provision for bad and doubtful debts.
Answer: Conservatism Concept
Q7. In gold mining, revenue is recognised in the accounting period in which the gold is mined.
Answer: Realization Concept
Q8. Classification of assets as current assets and fixed assets.
Answer: Going Concern Concept
Q9. Transactions are recorded at their original cost.
Answer: Cost Concept

Session 2 Class Notes — Key Takeaways

  • 12 Accounting Concepts form the foundation of all financial reporting
  • Business Entity: Owner and business are separate legal entities
  • Going Concern: Business assumed to continue indefinitely (RIL, Infosys examples)
  • Money Measurement: Only monetary transactions recorded (human capital excluded)
  • Cost Concept: Assets at acquisition cost, depreciated over time
  • Dual Aspect: Every debit has a credit — basis of double entry
  • Conservatism: "Anticipate no profit, provide for all probable losses" — provision for doubtful debts, stock at lower of cost/NRV
  • IFRS 15 / Ind AS 115: 4 steps for revenue recognition; over-time vs point-in-time
  • Matching: Expenses matched with revenues of the same period
  • Materiality is an exception to Full Disclosure
  • Consistency: Same accounting policies (LIFO, WDV) period to period
