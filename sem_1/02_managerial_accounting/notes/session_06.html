Understanding Financial Statements: P&L & Balance Sheet

The Income Statement tells you about performance, the Balance Sheet about position. Together they begin to reveal the true story of a business.

T1 Chapters 12-13 (Maheswari)Mid-Sem Syllabus~45 min read
Real-World Problem

How did HDFC Bank's merger with HDFC Ltd change what investors see in the financial statements?

When HDFC Ltd merged into HDFC Bank in July 2023, the combined entity's financial statements changed dramatically: the balance sheet doubled in size, the P&L showed interest income from both lending and housing loans, and the cash flow statement reflected massive changes in working capital. Investors needed to understand all three statements to judge whether the merger created value.

You cannot understand a business by reading just one financial statement. The Income Statement tells you about performance, the Balance Sheet about position, and the Cash Flow Statement about liquidity. This session teaches you to read between the lines.

1 Income Statement (P&L) — Performance Over a Period Beginner

The Income Statement shows what the company earned and spent during a period (typically April to March in India). Here is the structure:

Revenue from Operations
Core business income (sales of goods/services)
▼ minus COGS
Gross Profit
Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold
▼ minus Operating Expenses
EBIT (Operating Profit)
Gross Profit - Employee costs, Depreciation, Admin, Selling expenses
▼ minus Interest
Profit Before Tax (PBT)
EBIT - Interest + Other Income
▼ minus Tax
Net Profit (PAT)
The bottom line — what shareholders ultimately earned
Gross Profit = Revenue - COGS
EBIT = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses
PAT = PBT - Tax

India follows Schedule III format under the Companies Act, which uses the "function-of-expense" approach. Non-operating items (interest income, FX gains, exceptional items) are shown separately.

Daily Life Example

Your monthly "Income Statement" as a salaried person:
Revenue = Salary ₹1,00,000
COGS equivalent = Rent + Food ₹35,000
Gross Profit = ₹65,000
Operating Expenses = Transport ₹5,000 + Electricity ₹2,000 + Phone ₹1,000
EBIT = ₹57,000
Interest = EMI interest ₹8,000
Tax = TDS ₹10,000
Net Savings (PAT) = ₹39,000

2 Balance Sheet — Position at a Point in Time Beginner

The Balance Sheet is a snapshot of what the company owns and owes on a specific date (e.g., 31 March 2025).

Assets (What We Own)

Non-Current:

  • Property, Plant & Equipment (PPE)
  • Intangible Assets (goodwill, patents)
  • Long-term Investments

Current:

  • Inventory
  • Trade Receivables (Debtors)
  • Cash & Cash Equivalents
  • Prepaid Expenses

Liabilities + Equity (What We Owe / Own)

Non-Current Liabilities:

  • Long-term Borrowings
  • Deferred Tax Liability

Current Liabilities:

  • Trade Payables (Creditors)
  • Short-term Borrowings
  • Taxes Payable

Equity:

  • Share Capital
  • Reserves & Surplus
Assets = Liabilities + Equity (always, on every balance sheet, everywhere)

Trending Example (2024-25)

Tata Motors FY24: Reported record PAT of ₹31,807 crore (a dramatic turnaround from losses). But the real story was in operating cash flow: ₹53,476 crore — significantly higher than PAT, indicating high-quality earnings. Capex was ₹35,000 crore, showing massive reinvestment. The three statements together painted a picture of genuine business transformation, not accounting trickery.

Session 6 — Key Takeaways

  • Income Statement: Revenue → Gross Profit → EBIT → PBT → PAT (flow of performance)
  • Balance Sheet: Assets = Liabilities + Equity (snapshot of position)
  • India follows Schedule III format for financial statements
  • Read between the lines: Compare revenue growth with cash flow growth, check capex vs depreciation
  • No single statement tells the full story — always read all three together
  • Revenue growth without cash flow = RED FLAG
