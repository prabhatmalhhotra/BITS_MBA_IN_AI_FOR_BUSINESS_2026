## Chapter 1 — Introduction to Accounting

### 1.1 What is accounting?
Accounting is the language of business — the systematic process of identifying, recording, classifying, summarising, analysing and communicating financial information to users for decision-making.

### 1.2 Three branches

| Branch | Purpose | Audience | Frequency |
|--------|---------|----------|-----------|
| Financial accounting | Historical reporting per regulation | External (investors, lenders, tax) | Quarterly / Annual |
| Cost accounting | Compute product/service cost | Internal (operations, pricing) | Continuous |
| Management accounting | Planning, decision-making, control | Internal (managers) | As needed |

### 1.3 Users of accounting information
- **Internal**: management, employees.
- **External**: investors, lenders, suppliers, customers, government, regulators, public.

### 1.4 GAAP, principles & conventions

**Concepts** (the "what"):
- Business entity — business is separate from its owners.
- Going concern — assumes operations continue indefinitely.
- Money measurement — only quantifiable items recorded.
- Cost concept — assets at historical cost (with later impairment).
- Dual aspect — every transaction has a debit and credit.
- Accrual — record when earned/incurred, not when cash flows.
- Periodicity — split life of business into periods.
- Matching — match revenues with related expenses.
- Realisation — recognise revenue when earned.

**Conventions** (the "how"):
- Consistency — use same methods across periods.
- Conservatism (prudence) — anticipate losses, not gains.
- Materiality — record items material to decisions.
- Full disclosure — all relevant info disclosed in statements/notes.

### 1.5 Indian Accounting Standards (Ind AS)
Converged with IFRS. Mandatory for listed companies and large unlisted entities. Key Ind AS: 1 (Presentation), 2 (Inventories), 16 (PPE), 18/115 (Revenue), 109 (Financial Instruments).

### 60-second recap
Three branches solve three different problems. GAAP gives the rule-book. Concepts decide what to record; conventions decide how.

## Chapter 2 — The Accounting Cycle

### 2.1 Double-entry system
Every transaction has two equal effects: a debit (Dr) and a credit (Cr). Foundation: **Assets = Liabilities + Equity**.

### 2.2 Rules of debit and credit (modern approach)

| Account type | Debit | Credit |
|--------------|-------|--------|
| Assets | Increase | Decrease |
| Liabilities | Decrease | Increase |
| Equity | Decrease | Increase |
| Revenues | Decrease | Increase |
| Expenses | Increase | Decrease |

Mnemonic: "**DEAD CLIC**" — **D**ebit **E**xpenses, **A**ssets, **D**rawings; **C**redit **L**iabilities, **I**ncome, **C**apital.

### 2.3 Journal entries
Format: Date | Account Dr | Account Cr | Narration.

Example: ₹50,000 cash sale.
```
Cash A/c            Dr  50,000
   To Sales A/c            50,000
(Being cash sale of merchandise)
```

### 2.4 Ledger
T-accounts; debits on left, credits on right. Balance = (sum of larger side) − (sum of smaller side).

### 2.5 Trial balance
List of ledger balances; debits and credits should match. Used to detect arithmetic errors. Does NOT detect errors of omission, commission (right amount, wrong account), or principle.

### 2.6 Subsidiary books
- Cash book (single, double, triple-column)
- Purchases book / sales book / returns books
- Bills receivable / payable books
- Journal proper (for everything else)

### 60-second recap
Every transaction = a balanced pair of debits & credits. Journal first, then ledger, then trial balance. Trial balance proves arithmetic, not correctness.

## Chapter 3 — Financial Statements

### 3.1 Trading account
Shows gross profit (or loss):

```
Sales − Sales returns               XXX
Less: Cost of goods sold
       Opening stock         XX
       + Purchases (net)     XX
       + Direct expenses     XX
       − Closing stock      (XX)    XX
                                    ----
Gross Profit                        XXX
```

### 3.2 Profit & Loss account
Shows net profit:

```
Gross Profit                        XXX
+ Other income                      XXX
− Operating expenses
   (admin, selling, distribution)   XXX
− Depreciation                      XXX
− Interest                          XXX
                                    ----
Profit Before Tax                   XXX
− Tax                               XXX
                                    ----
Net Profit                          XXX
```

### 3.3 Balance sheet (Schedule III)

**Equity & Liabilities** | **Assets**
:--- | :---
Shareholders' funds (equity, reserves) | Non-current assets (PPE, intangibles, investments)
Non-current liabilities (long-term debt, deferred tax) | Current assets (inventory, debtors, cash, prepaid)
Current liabilities (creditors, short-term debt, taxes payable) |

A = E + L always.

### 3.4 Common adjustments
- **Closing stock** — debit to Trading; show as current asset.
- **Outstanding expenses** — debit to P&L; show as current liability.
- **Prepaid expenses** — credit to P&L (deduct); show as current asset.
- **Accrued income** — credit to P&L (add); show as current asset.
- **Income received in advance** — debit to P&L (deduct); show as current liability.
- **Depreciation** — debit to P&L; deduct from asset book value.
- **Bad debts written off / provision for doubtful debts** — debit to P&L; deduct from debtors.

### 3.5 Cash flow statement (AS-3 / Ind AS 7)

Three sections:

**Operating activities**
- Indirect method: Start from PBT → adjust non-cash (depreciation, provisions) → adjust working capital changes → tax paid.
- Direct method: List operating receipts/payments.

**Investing activities**: purchase/sale of PPE, investments; interest/dividend received.

**Financing activities**: issuance/repayment of equity & debt; dividends paid; interest paid.

Cash and cash equivalents: opening + net change = closing.

### 60-second recap
Trading → gross profit. P&L → net profit. Balance sheet → snapshot at a point. Cash flow → reconciles cash movements across operating, investing, financing.

## Chapter 4 — Depreciation, Provisions & Reserves

### 4.1 Depreciation methods

| Method | Formula |
|--------|---------|
| Straight Line (SLM) | (Cost − Scrap) / Useful life |
| Written Down Value (WDV) | Book value × rate |
| Units of production | (Cost − Scrap) × (units used / total units) |

SLM allocates equal annually; WDV charges more in early years (matches asset usage of many machines + tax benefits).

### 4.2 AS-10 / Ind AS 16 essentials
- Component approach: significant parts of an asset depreciated separately if useful lives differ.
- Residual value reviewed at each balance-sheet date.
- Subsequent expenditure: capitalise if extends useful life or capacity; otherwise expense.

### 4.3 Provisions vs reserves

| Aspect | Provision | Reserve |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Purpose | Known liability/expense, amount estimated | Strengthens financial position |
| Charge | Charge against profit (P&L) | Appropriation of profit |
| Examples | Provision for doubtful debts, tax | General reserve, capital reserve |

### 60-second recap
Depreciation spreads cost of an asset over life. SLM = equal; WDV = front-loaded. Provisions reduce profit; reserves are post-profit appropriations.

## Chapter 5 — Inventory Valuation

### 5.1 Methods

| Method | Logic | Behaviour in inflation |
|--------|-------|------------------------|
| FIFO (First-In-First-Out) | Oldest cost → COGS | Higher closing stock, higher profit, higher tax |
| LIFO (Last-In-First-Out) | Newest cost → COGS | Lower closing stock, lower profit (banned under Ind AS) |
| Weighted Average | Average cost smooths fluctuations | Middle of FIFO and LIFO |

### 5.2 AS-2 / Ind AS 2
Inventories valued at **lower of cost and net realisable value (NRV)**.
- Cost includes purchase price, conversion, and other costs to bring to current location/condition.
- NRV = expected selling price − costs to complete and sell.

### Worked example
Opening stock: 100 units @ ₹50.
Purchase 1: 200 units @ ₹55.
Purchase 2: 150 units @ ₹60.
Sales: 300 units.

FIFO: COGS = 100×50 + 200×55 = 5,000 + 11,000 = 16,000. Closing stock = 150 units @ ₹60 = ₹9,000.

Weighted Average: total cost = 5,000 + 11,000 + 9,000 = 25,000 / 450 units = ₹55.56/unit.
COGS = 300 × 55.56 = ₹16,667. Closing stock = 150 × 55.56 = ₹8,333.

### 60-second recap
FIFO under inflation → higher profit but matches new sales with old costs. Weighted average smooths. LIFO not permitted under Ind AS.

## Chapter 6 — Financial Statement Analysis

### 6.1 Three approaches
- **Horizontal** — line item compared period-over-period (% change).
- **Vertical / Common-size** — each item as % of base (sales for P&L, total assets for BS).
- **Trend** — index numbers over multi-year span (e.g., 2020 = 100).

### 6.2 Key ratios

#### Liquidity
- **Current ratio** = Current assets / Current liabilities. Healthy ≈ 1.5 to 2.
- **Quick (acid-test)** = (CA − Inventory − Prepaid) / CL. ≥ 1 desirable.
- **Cash ratio** = Cash / CL.

#### Solvency
- **Debt-equity** = Long-term debt / Equity. Lower = safer (industry-dependent).
- **Interest coverage** = EBIT / Interest. > 3 comfortable.
- **Debt to assets** = Total debt / Total assets.

#### Profitability
- **Gross profit margin** = GP / Sales × 100.
- **Net profit margin** = NP / Sales × 100.
- **ROCE** (Return on Capital Employed) = EBIT / (Equity + LT Debt) × 100.
- **ROE** = Net profit / Equity × 100.
- **ROA** = Net profit / Total assets × 100.

#### Efficiency / activity
- **Inventory turnover** = COGS / Average inventory.
- **Debtors turnover** = Net credit sales / Average debtors. **Days sales outstanding** = 365 / Debtors turnover.
- **Creditors turnover** = Net credit purchases / Average creditors.
- **Asset turnover** = Sales / Total assets.

### 6.3 Du Pont decomposition

ROE = (Net profit / Sales) × (Sales / Total assets) × (Total assets / Equity)
    = Net margin × Asset turnover × Equity multiplier
    = Profitability × Efficiency × Leverage

This isolates the levers a manager can pull to improve ROE.

### 60-second recap
Ratios are doors into the financials. Liquidity → can the firm pay short-term bills? Solvency → can it service long-term debt? Profitability → is it making money? Efficiency → is it using assets well? Du Pont links them through ROE.

## Chapter 7 — Cost Accounting Concepts

### 7.1 Cost classification

| Basis | Types |
|-------|-------|
| Function | Production, Administration, Selling, Distribution, R&D |
| Behaviour | Fixed, Variable, Semi-variable, Step |
| Traceability | Direct, Indirect |
| Decision relevance | Relevant, Sunk, Opportunity |
| Time | Historical, Predetermined |
| Inventory | Product (capitalised), Period (expensed) |

### 7.2 Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) analysis

Definitions:
- **Contribution** = Sales − Variable cost
- **Contribution per unit** = Selling price − Variable cost per unit
- **P/V ratio** = Contribution / Sales × 100
- **Break-even (units)** = Fixed cost / Contribution per unit
- **Break-even (₹)** = Fixed cost / P/V ratio
- **Margin of safety** = Actual sales − Break-even sales
- **Profit** = (Sales × P/V ratio) − Fixed cost

### 7.3 Operating leverage
DOL = Contribution / Profit. High DOL → small change in sales → big change in profit (great in growth, painful in decline).

### Worked example
Selling price = ₹100, Variable cost = ₹60, Fixed cost = ₹4,00,000. Target profit = ₹2,00,000.
- Contribution per unit = 40; P/V = 40%.
- BEP units = 4,00,000 / 40 = 10,000 units. BEP sales = 10,00,000.
- Units for target profit = (4,00,000 + 2,00,000) / 40 = 15,000 units.

### 60-second recap
CVP = sales × P/V − fixed cost. Break-even is where contribution equals fixed cost. Higher fixed costs raise leverage.

## Chapter 8 — Marginal vs Absorption Costing

### 8.1 Definitions
- **Marginal (variable) costing**: Only variable costs assigned to products. Fixed manufacturing overhead expensed.
- **Absorption costing**: All manufacturing costs (variable + fixed) assigned to products. Fixed overhead in inventory until sold.

### 8.2 Profit difference
Profit difference = (Closing stock units − Opening stock units) × Fixed overhead rate per unit.
- Production > Sales → Absorption profit > Marginal profit.
- Production < Sales → Absorption profit < Marginal profit.

### 8.3 Decision applications

| Decision | Rule |
|----------|------|
| Make-or-buy | Compare buying price with marginal cost; if buy < marginal cost → buy (subject to capacity & strategic considerations) |
| Special order at low price | Accept if price > variable cost AND no spare capacity is sacrificed for regular orders |
| Drop a product | Drop only if its segment contribution is negative; otherwise it covers fixed costs |
| Sell or process further | Process if incremental revenue > incremental cost beyond split-off point |
| Limiting factor | Maximise contribution per unit of limiting factor (e.g., per machine hour) |

### 60-second recap
Marginal costing is for decisions; absorption is for external reporting. The difference rests in fixed-overhead treatment. Decisions follow contribution, not allocated cost.

## Chapter 9 — Budgeting and Budgetary Control

### 9.1 Definition
A budget is a quantitative expression of plans for a future period.

### 9.2 Types

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Master budget | Comprehensive — combines all functional budgets |
| Functional budgets | Sales, production, materials, labour, overhead, cash, capital expenditure |
| Fixed budget | Single output level; useful for stable environments |
| Flexible budget | Adjusts for actual activity level — better for variance analysis |
| Zero-based budget | Every expense justified afresh from zero each cycle |
| Rolling (continuous) budget | Always 12 months ahead — drop the past month, add a new future month |

### 9.3 Cash budget
Tracks expected cash inflows (sales receipts, debtor collections, asset sales) and outflows (purchases, salaries, expenses, capex, taxes, dividends) month-by-month. Identifies surplus/shortage so the company can raise finance or invest excess.

### 9.4 Benefits & limitations
**Benefits**: planning discipline, coordination, control, motivation, performance evaluation.
**Limitations**: time-consuming, may be padded, can suppress innovation, environment may change rendering budget irrelevant.

### 60-second recap
A budget is plan + benchmark. Flexible beats fixed for control. Zero-based forces real justification. Cash budget is the operational lifeline.

## Chapter 10 — Standard Costing & Variance Analysis

### 10.1 Standard costs
Pre-set targets for cost per unit. Difference between standard and actual = variance.

### 10.2 Material variances
- **Material Cost Variance** = (Standard cost of actual output) − (Actual cost) = SQ × SP − AQ × AP
- **Material Price Variance** = AQ × (SP − AP)
- **Material Usage Variance** = SP × (SQ − AQ)
- Material Cost Variance = Price Variance + Usage Variance

When more than one material:
- **Mix Variance** = SP × (Revised SQ − Actual mix)
- **Yield Variance** = SP × (Standard mix for actual output − Revised SQ)

### 10.3 Labour variances
- **Labour Cost Variance** = SH × SR − AH × AR
- **Rate Variance** = AH × (SR − AR)
- **Efficiency Variance** = SR × (SH − AH)
- **Idle-time Variance** = SR × Idle hours
- Cost = Rate + Efficiency + Idle-time.

### 10.4 Overhead variances
- **Variable Overhead Variance** = Spending + Efficiency.
- **Fixed Overhead Variance** = Budget + Volume.

### 10.5 Significance of variances
Investigate when variance is large in absolute terms or as % of standard, and when persistent or controllable. Don't waste effort on small / random variances.

### 60-second recap
Standard − Actual = Variance. Decompose into price/rate (out of operation manager's control) and quantity/efficiency (in-control). Always reconcile components.

## Chapter 11 — Responsibility Accounting & Performance Measurement

### 11.1 Responsibility centres

| Centre | Manager controls | Performance measure |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|
| Cost centre | Costs only | Cost variance |
| Revenue centre | Revenue only | Sales achieved vs target |
| Profit centre | Costs + revenues | Profit margin |
| Investment centre | Costs + revenues + investment | ROI, Residual Income, EVA |

### 11.2 ROI vs Residual Income

- **ROI** = Operating profit / Investment × 100.
- **Residual Income (RI)** = Operating profit − (Investment × Required rate of return).
- **EVA (Economic Value Added)** = NOPAT − (Capital × WACC).

ROI can encourage rejection of profitable projects below average. RI/EVA encourages acceptance of any project earning above the cost of capital.

### 11.3 Transfer pricing
Setting price at which one division sells to another:
- Market-based — preferred when competitive market exists.
- Cost-based (variable / full / cost-plus) — when no market.
- Negotiated — division managers negotiate.

### 11.4 Balanced Scorecard
Four perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Growth. Translates strategy into measurable KPIs across all four.

### 60-second recap
Hold managers accountable only for what they control. RI/EVA beat ROI for capital allocation. Balanced Scorecard prevents "managing-by-numbers" myopia.

## Chapter 12 — Decision-making with Accounting Information

### 12.1 Relevant cost analysis
Relevant costs are **future cash flows** that **differ between alternatives**. Sunk costs and committed costs are NOT relevant.

### 12.2 Capital budgeting basics

| Method | Formula | Decision rule |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Payback period | Years till cumulative cash inflow = investment | Accept if < target |
| ARR (Accounting Rate of Return) | Average profit / Average investment | Accept if > target |
| NPV | Σ CFt / (1+r)^t − Initial investment | Accept if NPV > 0 |
| IRR | Rate where NPV = 0 | Accept if IRR > cost of capital |
| Profitability Index | PV of inflows / Investment | Accept if PI > 1 |

NPV is theoretically the best — accounts for time value of money and absolute value addition.

### 60-second recap
Decisions look forward. Strip out sunk costs. Use NPV/IRR for any non-trivial capital decision. Payback is a screening tool, not a decision tool.
