### Q2. (12 m) The following ratios were extracted for two competing firms in the same industry:

| Ratio | Firm A | Firm B | Industry avg |
|-------|------:|------:|------------:|
| Current ratio | 1.8 | 1.2 | 1.7 |
| Quick ratio | 1.1 | 0.7 | 1.0 |
| Debt-equity | 0.6 | 1.4 | 0.8 |
| Interest coverage | 6 | 2.2 | 4 |
| GP margin | 28% | 32% | 30% |
| NP margin | 9% | 7% | 8% |
| ROE | 18% | 21% | 17% |
| Inventory turnover | 7 | 4 | 6 |
| Debtors turnover | 9 | 6 | 8 |

Comment on the financial position and performance of both firms. Which would you invest in and why?

**Answer framework**

**Liquidity** (current, quick): A is healthier and in line with industry. B is below norms — a potential short-term cash crunch.

**Solvency** (D/E, interest coverage): A is conservatively financed and comfortably services debt (6×). B is highly leveraged (D/E 1.4) and dangerously close to its interest coverage floor (2.2×) — a profit dip could trigger default.

**Profitability**: B has higher GP and ROE (driven by leverage), but lower NP margin — interest expense is eating profitability. A's profitability is solid, with lower risk.

**Efficiency**: A has better inventory and debtor turnover, suggesting tighter operations.

**Verdict**: For a long-term investor seeking stable returns, **Firm A** is preferable — better liquidity, lower leverage, stronger operations, with profitability close to industry. B's higher ROE comes from leverage; its quality of earnings is weaker. Firm B suits only an aggressive, risk-tolerant investor and should be avoided in volatile macro conditions.

### Q3. (10 m) A company has the following data:
- Selling price = ₹200/unit
- Variable cost = ₹120/unit
- Fixed cost = ₹16,00,000

Compute (a) BEP in units and ₹, (b) Sales required for ₹4,00,000 profit, (c) Margin of safety if actual sales = 30,000 units, (d) New BEP if SP drops to ₹180.

**Answer**

(a) Contribution per unit = 200 − 120 = ₹80. P/V = 40%.
- BEP units = 16,00,000 / 80 = **20,000 units**.
- BEP sales (₹) = 20,000 × 200 = **₹40,00,000** (or 16,00,000 / 0.4).

(b) Required units = (FC + Profit) / Contribution per unit = (16,00,000 + 4,00,000) / 80 = **25,000 units**. Sales = 25,000 × 200 = **₹50,00,000**.

(c) Actual sales = 30,000 × 200 = ₹60,00,000.
- Margin of Safety = 60,00,000 − 40,00,000 = **₹20,00,000** or **33.3%** of sales.

(d) New contribution per unit = 180 − 120 = ₹60.
- New BEP units = 16,00,000 / 60 = **26,667 units**. Notice 33% increase in BEP for a 10% price cut — leverage of pricing on profitability.

### Q4. (15 m) XYZ Ltd manufactures Product A. Currently it sells 8,000 units at ₹400 per unit. Variable cost is ₹240/unit. Fixed cost is ₹6,00,000.

A foreign customer has placed a special order for 2,000 units at ₹290/unit. Acceptance will require ₹50,000 of additional fixed cost (special packaging) but no displacement of regular sales.

**(i)** Should the order be accepted on financial grounds?
**(ii)** Discuss 3 non-financial factors before deciding.

**Answer**

(i) Special-order analysis — relevant cost approach.

Incremental revenue: 2,000 × 290 = ₹5,80,000.
Incremental variable cost: 2,000 × 240 = ₹4,80,000.
Incremental fixed cost: ₹50,000.
**Incremental profit = 5,80,000 − 4,80,000 − 50,000 = ₹50,000 ⇒ Accept.**

(ii) Non-financial considerations:
- **Pricing precedent**: Will offering ₹290 to one customer create downward pressure on regular ₹400 pricing in the home market? Consider geographic separation, brand reputation, contract clauses.
- **Capacity / opportunity cost**: Will accepting deplete capacity needed for higher-margin work later in the year?
- **Customer relationship & strategy**: Is this an entry into a new export market with growth potential, or a one-off deal? Strategic value can outweigh short-term margin.
- **Quality & operational risk**: Special packaging/timeline risk — penalty clauses?
- **Regulatory / forex risk**: Export documentation, currency hedging, payment terms.

### Q5. (12 m) Standard cost of producing one unit:
- Material: 5 kg @ ₹40/kg = ₹200
- Labour: 4 hours @ ₹50/hr = ₹200

Actual production = 1,000 units. Actual figures: Material consumed 5,200 kg @ ₹42/kg; Labour 3,800 hrs @ ₹52/hr.

Compute material price, usage, total variances and labour rate, efficiency, total variances. Comment.

**Answer**

**Material variances**

SQ for actual output = 5 × 1,000 = 5,000 kg. SP = ₹40. AQ = 5,200. AP = ₹42.

- Material Cost Variance = SQ × SP − AQ × AP = 5,000 × 40 − 5,200 × 42 = 2,00,000 − 2,18,400 = **₹18,400 (A)**dverse.
- Material Price Variance = AQ × (SP − AP) = 5,200 × (40 − 42) = **₹10,400 (A)**.
- Material Usage Variance = SP × (SQ − AQ) = 40 × (5,000 − 5,200) = **₹8,000 (A)**.
- Check: 10,400 (A) + 8,000 (A) = 18,400 (A). ✓

**Labour variances**

SH for actual output = 4 × 1,000 = 4,000 hours. SR = ₹50. AH = 3,800. AR = ₹52.

- Labour Cost Variance = SH × SR − AH × AR = 4,000 × 50 − 3,800 × 52 = 2,00,000 − 1,97,600 = **₹2,400 (F)**avourable.
- Labour Rate Variance = AH × (SR − AR) = 3,800 × (50 − 52) = **₹7,600 (A)**.
- Labour Efficiency Variance = SR × (SH − AH) = 50 × (4,000 − 3,800) = **₹10,000 (F)**.
- Check: 7,600 (A) + 10,000 (F) = 2,400 (F). ✓

**Comments**: Materials cost more (price up 5%) and were used in greater quantity than standard — investigate supplier price changes and process wastage. Labour cost was higher per hour (overtime?) but efficient enough that workers finished in fewer hours than standard — net favourable. Possibly skilled/experienced operators substituted for usual workforce.

### Q7. (15 m) PQR Ltd produces 3 products A, B, C. Data below:

| | A | B | C |
|--|--:|--:|--:|
| Selling price (₹) | 60 | 75 | 90 |
| Variable cost (₹) | 35 | 45 | 60 |
| Machine hours / unit | 1 | 1.5 | 2 |
| Maximum demand (units) | 12,000 | 8,000 | 6,000 |

Total available machine hours = 24,000. Fixed costs = ₹2,00,000.

Determine the optimal product mix and the resulting profit.

**Answer**

Step 1 — Compute contribution per unit and per machine hour.

| | A | B | C |
|--|--:|--:|--:|
| Contribution / unit | 25 | 30 | 30 |
| Machine hours / unit | 1 | 1.5 | 2 |
| Contribution / machine hour | 25 | 20 | 15 |
| Rank | 1 | 2 | 3 |

Step 2 — Allocate machine hours by rank.

- A first: 12,000 units × 1 hr = 12,000 hrs. Cumulative = 12,000.
- B next: 8,000 × 1.5 = 12,000 hrs. Cumulative = 24,000. Capacity exhausted.
- C: 0 units (no hours left).

Step 3 — Compute total contribution.
- A: 12,000 × 25 = ₹3,00,000
- B: 8,000 × 30 = ₹2,40,000
- C: 0
- **Total contribution = ₹5,40,000**.

Step 4 — Profit = Contribution − Fixed cost = 5,40,000 − 2,00,000 = **₹3,40,000**.

**Optimal mix**: A 12,000 + B 8,000 + C 0 units.

### Q8. (10 m) Explain the differences between marginal and absorption costing. Show the impact on reported profit when production exceeds sales using a simple numerical illustration.

**Answer**

**Differences (5 m)**

| Aspect | Marginal | Absorption |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Treatment of fixed manufacturing overhead | Period cost (expensed in full) | Product cost (included in inventory) |
| Inventory value | Variable cost only | Full manufacturing cost |
| Profit measurement | Contribution-margin format | Gross-profit format |
| Decision-making suitability | Strong (relevant cost) | Weak — fixed cost allocation can mislead |
| External reporting | Not allowed under accounting standards | Required under Ind AS / GAAP |

**Numerical (5 m)**

Production = 10,000 units; Sales = 8,000 units. Variable manufacturing cost = ₹40/unit. Fixed manufacturing overhead = ₹1,00,000. Selling price = ₹70.

| | Marginal | Absorption |
|---|--:|--:|
| Sales (8,000 × 70) | 5,60,000 | 5,60,000 |
| Less: Variable cost of goods sold (8,000 × 40) | 3,20,000 | 3,20,000 |
| Less: Fixed mfg OH absorbed in COGS (1,00,000 × 8,000/10,000) | — | 80,000 |
| Less: Fixed mfg OH (period expense) | 1,00,000 | — |
| **Profit** | **1,40,000** | **1,60,000** |

Difference = ₹20,000 = closing stock 2,000 × ₹10 (fixed cost per unit). Under absorption, ₹20,000 of fixed cost is "stored" in inventory and not yet expensed.

**Insight**: When production > sales, absorption shows higher profit. When production < sales, lower. This is why absorption-costing income can be manipulated through over-production — relevant for managerial bonus design.

### Q9. (12 m) The following ratios were computed for Alpha Ltd:

| Ratio | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 |
|-------|------:|------:|------:|
| GP margin | 35% | 33% | 30% |
| NP margin | 12% | 9% | 7% |
| ROE | 24% | 18% | 14% |
| Asset turnover | 1.5 | 1.4 | 1.3 |
| Debt-equity | 0.5 | 0.7 | 1.0 |
| Inventory days | 60 | 75 | 90 |

Diagnose the company's deteriorating performance using ratio analysis. Suggest 4 corrective actions.

**Answer**

**Diagnosis**

1. **Margin compression**: GP margin dropped 5 percentage points → input cost inflation not passed on to customers, or pricing pressure. NP margin halved → operating expenses or interest growing faster than revenue.
2. **Operational inefficiency**: Asset turnover declining → revenues not keeping pace with asset base. Inventory days rising 50% → poor demand forecasting / slow-moving stock.
3. **Increasing leverage**: D/E doubled to 1.0 → company taking on debt to fund operations or investments. Combined with margin compression, interest burden is squeezing profits.
4. **Du Pont decomposition**:
   - Year 1 ROE = 12% × 1.5 × (1 + 0.5) ≈ 27% (close to reported)
   - Year 3 ROE = 7% × 1.3 × (1 + 1.0) = 18.2% (gap due to non-equity multiplier specifics — point is the trajectory is downward).
   - Profitability is the biggest drag.

**Corrective actions**

1. **Pricing & cost review**: Renegotiate raw-material contracts; selective price increase for premium SKUs; cut variable cost of low-margin SKUs.
2. **Inventory rationalisation**: ABC analysis; clearance of slow-moving items; demand-driven planning to bring inventory days back to ~60.
3. **Working capital tightening**: Improve debtor collection; lengthen creditor payment cycle without damaging supplier relations → reduce reliance on debt.
4. **Capital structure rebalancing**: Halt new debt; consider equity infusion or retained-earnings reinvestment to bring D/E below 0.7.
5. (Optional) **Asset productivity audit**: Identify under-utilised PPE; sell/lease idle capacity to lift asset turnover.

### Q10. (10 m) A company is considering the following 4 capital projects. Cost of capital = 10%. Compute NPV and rank them. Also state the implications of using NPV vs IRR when projects are mutually exclusive.

| Project | Investment (₹ lakh) | Annual cash flow (₹ lakh) | Life (years) |
|---------|------:|------:|------:|
| W | 50 | 14 | 5 |
| X | 80 | 22 | 5 |
| Y | 30 | 10 | 4 |
| Z | 100 | 25 | 6 |

PV factor at 10%: 5 yr annuity = 3.7908; 4 yr = 3.1699; 6 yr = 4.3553.

**Answer**

| Project | PV of inflows | Investment | NPV |
|---------|------:|------:|------:|
| W | 14 × 3.7908 = 53.07 | 50 | **3.07** |
| X | 22 × 3.7908 = 83.40 | 80 | **3.40** |
| Y | 10 × 3.1699 = 31.70 | 30 | **1.70** |
| Z | 25 × 4.3553 = 108.88 | 100 | **8.88** |

**Ranking by NPV (highest first)**: Z > X > W > Y. All have positive NPV → all worthwhile if independent and unlimited capital.

If capital is constrained (say ₹100 lakh), pick combinations:
- Z alone: NPV = 8.88, uses ₹100 lakh.
- X + W: NPV = 6.47, uses ₹130 lakh (over budget).
- X + Y: NPV = 5.10, uses ₹110 lakh (over).
- W + Y: NPV = 4.77, uses ₹80 lakh — leaves ₹20 idle.

Best within ₹100 lakh = Z.

**NPV vs IRR (mutually exclusive)**: For mutually exclusive projects, NPV is preferred because IRR can give misleading rankings — particularly when projects differ in scale or when cash-flow timing is non-conventional (multiple sign changes ⇒ multiple IRRs). NPV directly measures wealth addition in absolute rupees and is consistent with shareholder-value maximisation.

### Q11. (15 m) Discuss in detail the Balanced Scorecard framework. How does it overcome the shortcomings of purely financial performance measures? Illustrate with KPIs across all four perspectives for a B2C retail business.

**Answer framework**

**Limitations of pure financial measures (3 m)**
- Backward-looking; reflect past results, not future capability.
- Don't capture customer satisfaction, employee engagement, innovation.
- Encourage short-termism (hitting quarter at expense of strategy).
- Hard to interpret without operational context.
- Vulnerable to manipulation (revenue recognition, expense capitalisation).

**Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton, 1992) (4 m)** — translates strategy into objectives, KPIs, targets, and initiatives across 4 perspectives:

| Perspective | Question | Example for retail |
|-------------|----------|---------------------|
| Financial | How do we look to shareholders? | Revenue growth, ROCE, NP margin, EBITDA |
| Customer | How do customers see us? | NPS, retention, market share, brand recall |
| Internal process | What must we excel at? | Stock-out rate, on-shelf availability, fulfilment time |
| Learning & growth | Can we improve & innovate? | Employee engagement, training hours, % revenue from new products |

**Strategy map (3 m)**: shows cause-effect linkages across perspectives — improved learning → better internal processes → satisfied customers → financial performance.

**Illustrative BSC for B2C retail (5 m)**

| Perspective | Objective | KPI | Target | Initiative |
|-------------|-----------|-----|-------:|------------|
| Financial | Grow EBITDA | EBITDA margin | 12% | SKU rationalisation |
| Financial | Same-store sales growth | SSSG % | 8% YoY | Loyalty programme |
| Customer | Improve loyalty | NPS | ≥ 60 | Quarterly NPS surveys, closed-loop feedback |
| Customer | Customer retention | 12-month retention | ≥ 75% | Personalised offers via CRM |
| Internal | Reduce stock-outs | OOS rate | < 2% | AI-driven demand forecasting |
| Internal | Speed up checkout | Avg checkout time | < 90 sec | Self-checkout kiosks |
| Learning | Workforce skills | Avg training hrs / employee | 40 / yr | Internal academy |
| Learning | Innovation pipeline | % revenue from products < 12 mo | 15% | Open innovation lab |

**Conclusion**: BSC is not a metric dashboard; it is a strategy management system that aligns operational decisions to long-term goals. Done well, it surfaces leading indicators (engagement, NPS) that drive future financial outcomes.

### Q12. (12 m) A division within a large company shows ROI = 22%, while the company's cost of capital = 14%. The division manager is offered a new project requiring ₹50 lakh investment with expected operating profit ₹9 lakh. Should the manager accept it?

(a) Decision based on ROI.
(b) Decision based on Residual Income.
(c) Discuss the dysfunction of ROI-based decision-making and how RI / EVA correct it.

**Answer**

(a) **ROI of new project** = 9 / 50 × 100 = **18%**. Division's existing ROI = 22%.
   Since 18% < 22%, accepting will pull division's overall ROI down. Manager (rationally maximising her ROI metric) will **reject**.

(b) **Residual Income**:
   - Expected RI = Operating profit − (Investment × Cost of capital)
   - = 9 − (50 × 0.14) = 9 − 7 = **₹2 lakh** positive.
   - Project's return (18%) > company's cost of capital (14%) → **value-creating**. Manager should **accept**.

(c) **Dysfunction**: ROI-based managers reject projects earning above the cost of capital but below current divisional ROI. This "high-ROI division shrinks itself" — value-destroying for shareholders.

**RI / EVA fix this** by rewarding managers for any positive economic value added, regardless of relative ROI. EVA goes further by adjusting accounting profit (NOPAT − capital × WACC) and is a popular performance metric in capital-intensive companies (Tata Steel, Asian Paints have used variants).

**Caveat**: RI requires agreement on cost of capital (or hurdle rate), and small divisions can find absolute RI numbers smaller — best used alongside ROI rather than instead of it.
