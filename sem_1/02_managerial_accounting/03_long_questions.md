## Mid-Sem scope (Sessions 1-8)

### LQ-1 (Sessions 1-2) — Accounting concepts in business context
**Prompt.** A startup founder claims, "Accounting is just historical bookkeeping — irrelevant for a fast-moving tech firm." Critically evaluate this view and discuss how accounting concepts (going concern, accrual, matching, prudence) actually shape strategic decisions in a SaaS firm.

**Skeleton**: Branches of accounting (FA/MA/Tax/Sustainability) → MA is forward-looking, decision-support → going concern justifies historical cost & R&D capitalisation under IFRS → accrual & matching distort cash but reflect economic reality → prudence creates downward bias on intangibles in SaaS → unit economics, CAC/LTV, deferred revenue all rely on accrual → conclusion: accounting is not historical record alone but the lens that turns business strategy into measurable outcomes.

### LQ-2 (Sessions 3-4) — Journalising and final accounts

Adjustments: Closing stock ₹40,000; Outstanding wages ₹3,000; Prepaid insurance ₹1,500; Depreciation 10 % p.a. on machinery ₹2,00,000; Provision for doubtful debts at 5 % on debtors.

|-------------|-------:|-------:|
| Opening stock | 25,000 | |
| Purchases / Sales | 1,80,000 | 3,00,000 |
| Wages | 22,000 | |
| Salaries | 36,000 | |
| Insurance | 6,000 | |

| Machinery | 2,00,000 | |

**Skeleton (fully worked)**:

*Trading A/c (year ended 31-Mar-2025)*

| Equity & Liabilities | ₹ | Assets | ₹ |
|----------------------|---:|--------|---:|
| Capital 2,30,000 + NP 45,500 | 2,75,500 | Machinery (2,00,000 − 20,000 dep) | 1,80,000 |

### LQ-3 (Session 5) — IFRS / IAS impact analysis
**Prompt.** Discuss how IFRS 15, IAS 2, IAS 16, and IAS 38 each shape the financial statements of a (a) software company, (b) FMCG firm, (c) manufacturer. Mention one Indian Ind-AS equivalent for each.

**Skeleton**: IFRS 15: software → recognise revenue per performance obligation (license vs SaaS subscription differ); FMCG → recognise on delivery; Manufacturing → recognise on transfer of control / project milestones (Ind-AS 115).
IAS 2: software → minimal inventory; FMCG → FIFO/Weighted Avg, NRV; Manufacturer → process costing, conversion costs (Ind-AS 2).
IAS 16: software → low PPE; FMCG → moderate PPE, high turnover; Manufacturer → high PPE, component depreciation (Ind-AS 16).
IAS 38: software → development capitalised if PIRATE met → big asset boost vs US GAAP; FMCG → marketing expenses; Manufacturer → product development capitalised (Ind-AS 38).

### LQ-4 (Session 5) — Quality of financial reporting
**Prompt.** A listed company's PAT grew 25 % YoY but operating cash flow declined 12 %. Inventory days rose from 60 to 95; debtor days from 45 to 80. (a) List five red flags this triggers. (b) What disclosures in the annual report would you scrutinise? (c) Recommend three remediation actions for management.

**Skeleton**: (a) earnings quality concern, channel stuffing, capitalising opex, related-party transactions, aggressive revenue recognition; (b) auditors' report (KAMs), notes on revenue recognition policy, related-party transactions, contingent liabilities, segment reporting, ESG disclosures; (c) tighten credit policy, write-down obsolete inventory, cash-flow-based incentives for sales team.

### LQ-5 (Sessions 6-7) — Reading between the lines
**Prompt.** Compare two firms with similar revenue but very different P&L and Balance Sheet patterns: Firm A (high gross margin, high D/E, capital-intensive); Firm B (low margin, asset-light, high asset turnover). (a) Which industries do they likely belong to? (b) Identify three risks for each. (c) Which firm has higher operating leverage and why?

**Skeleton**: A → likely Pharma/Telco/Power → risks: regulatory, refinancing, demand shock; B → likely FMCG/E-commerce → risks: thin margin, competitive, brand erosion. Operating leverage higher for A (high fixed costs); a 10 % revenue dip hurts A's EBIT disproportionately.

### LQ-6 (Session 8) — Liquidity & solvency assessment
**Prompt.** From the following data, compute and interpret Current Ratio, Quick Ratio, Debt-Equity, Interest Coverage, and Working Capital. Comment on the firm's short-term and long-term financial health.

| Item | ₹ Cr |
|------|-----:|
| Current Assets | 250 |
| Current Liabilities | 180 |
| Inventory | 80 |
| Long-term Debt | 200 |
| Equity | 350 |
| EBIT | 90 |
| Interest Expense | 18 |

**Skeleton**: CR = 250/180 = 1.39 (slightly below ideal 1.5); QR = (250−80)/180 = 0.94 (just below 1.0 — minor concern); D/E = 200/350 = 0.57 (moderate leverage); ICR = 90/18 = 5.0 (comfortable); WC = 250−180 = 70 cr. Conclusion: short-term liquidity stretched but solvency comfortable; recommend tighter inventory management and small WC cushion.

### LQ-7 (Sessions 9-10) — DuPont decomposition
**Prompt.** Two firms in the same industry have ROE = 18 %. Decompose using 5-step DuPont. Discuss which firm is better quality and why.

| Driver | Firm X | Firm Y |
|--------|------:|------:|
| Tax Burden (PAT/PBT) | 0.75 | 0.70 |
| Interest Burden (PBT/EBIT) | 0.85 | 0.95 |
| EBIT Margin | 0.18 | 0.10 |
| Asset Turnover | 0.90 | 1.50 |
| Equity Multiplier | 1.74 | 1.80 |

**Skeleton**: Verify ROE: X = 0.75×0.85×0.18×0.90×1.74 ≈ 17.96 %; Y = 0.70×0.95×0.10×1.50×1.80 ≈ 17.96 %. Same ROE, different DNA. X is margin-driven (premium positioning); Y is turnover-driven (cost leadership). X is more sustainable if margins hold; Y is volume-dependent. Comment on industry context (luxury vs commodity); X is more attractive in competitive pressure scenarios; Y is more vulnerable to margin compression.

### LQ-9 (Sessions 14-15) — CVP & Decision making
**Prompt.** XYZ Ltd manufactures two products A and B. Selling price ₹100 (A), ₹150 (B); variable cost ₹60 (A), ₹90 (B); machine hours required 2 (A), 5 (B). Total fixed cost ₹6,00,000; available machine hours 12,000. (a) Compute CM per unit and per machine hour for each. (b) Determine optimal product mix to maximise profit. (c) Compute total profit. (d) If A's variable cost rises ₹10 due to material inflation, redo the analysis.

**Skeleton**:
- (a) CM: A = 40, B = 60. CM/MH: A = 20, B = 12. **A is more profitable per scarce resource.**
- (b) Produce only A: 12,000/2 = 6,000 units. (Assumes unlimited demand.)
- (c) Profit = 6,000 × 40 − 6,00,000 = ₹2,40,000 − 6,00,000 = (₹3,60,000 loss). Need higher demand or revisit fixed costs.
- (d) New CM: A = 30, CM/MH = 15 (still > B's 12). Same conclusion. New profit at 6,000 units = 6,000 × 30 − 6,00,000 = (₹4,20,000 loss).
- Discuss: if can sell mix, do at least BEP for A: 6,00,000/40 = 15,000 units (need 30,000 MH — infeasible).

### LQ-10 (Session 14) — Make-or-buy decision
**Prompt.** A firm currently makes a component at variable cost ₹120/unit, fixed overheads ₹50/unit (₹2,00,000 total at 4,000 units). A vendor offers the same at ₹150/unit. (a) Compute relevant cost of making vs buying. (b) State whether to outsource and why. (c) Discuss two strategic factors beyond cost.

**Skeleton**:
- Relevant cost of making = variable cost only (assuming fixed overhead is unavoidable) = 120 × 4,000 = ₹4,80,000.
- Cost of buying = 150 × 4,000 = ₹6,00,000.
- Continue making — cheaper by ₹1,20,000.
- BUT if **40 %** of fixed overhead (₹80,000 of the ₹2,00,000 total) is avoidable on outsourcing → relevant cost of making = ₹4,80,000 + ₹80,000 = ₹5,60,000 vs buying ₹6,00,000 → still cheaper to make by ₹40,000.
- *Tipping point*: if avoidable fixed ≥ ₹1,20,000 (i.e. ≥ 60 % of total fixed overhead), the make/buy decision flips — the firm becomes indifferent and outsourcing wins on strategic flexibility.
- Strategic: quality control, IP protection, capacity for new products, vendor lock-in, supply chain risk.

### LQ-11 (Session 14) — Special order
**Prompt.** A factory operating at 70 % capacity (12,000 units) is offered an export order for 3,000 units at ₹85/unit. Selling price domestic ₹120, variable cost ₹70, fixed cost ₹3,00,000 (constant). (a) Should the order be accepted? (b) What if it would displace 1,000 domestic units?

**Skeleton**:
- (a) Spare capacity available (capacity = 17,143; current 12,000; +3,000 = 15,000 < capacity). Variable cost per unit ₹70; offered price ₹85; CM/unit = ₹15. Total CM = 3,000 × 15 = ₹45,000. **Accept** (positive contribution, no fixed cost change).
- (b) Lost CM from displaced 1,000 domestic units = 1,000 × (120-70) = ₹50,000. Total benefit = 45,000 − 50,000 = (₹5,000) loss. **Reject** — opportunity cost outweighs gain.

### LQ-12 (Session 16) — Budgeting and variance
**Prompt.** A firm budgeted: 10,000 units × ₹50 SP = ₹5,00,000 sales; standard variable cost ₹30/unit (Material ₹15, Labour ₹10, Overhead ₹5); fixed cost ₹1,00,000. Actual: 9,500 units sold at ₹52; material ₹1,55,000 (10,200 kg @ ₹15.20 actual; standard 1 kg @ ₹15); labour ₹98,000; fixed ₹1,05,000. (a) Sales price variance. (b) Sales volume variance (CM basis). (c) Material price and usage variance. (d) Comment.

**Skeleton**:
- Sales Price Variance = (Actual SP − Budget SP) × Actual Q = (52 − 50) × 9,500 = ₹19,000 F.
- Sales Volume Variance (CM basis) = (Actual Q − Budget Q) × Std CM = (9,500 − 10,000) × 20 = ₹10,000 A.
- Std qty for actual output = 9,500 kg; AQ = 10,200 kg.
- Material Price Variance = AQ × (SP − AP) = 10,200 × (15 − 15.20) = ₹2,040 A.
- Material Usage Variance = SP × (SQ − AQ) = 15 × (9,500 − 10,200) = ₹10,500 A.
- Comment: Higher selling price worked, but volume fell short, and material cost over-spent (both price and usage). Overall variance ₹3,540 A approx → investigate procurement and scrap rate.

### LQ-13 (Cross-cutting) — Strategic case
**Prompt.** A manufacturer is reviewing its annual planning. Discuss how it can integrate (a) zero-based budgeting, (b) IFRS-aligned reporting, (c) CVP-based pricing decisions, (d) variance analysis for performance management, into a holistic financial control system. What are two organisational pre-requisites?

**Skeleton**:
- ZBB → challenges every cost head, identifies waste; first year-onerous, then biennial.
- IFRS-aligned → adopt IFRS 15 revenue recognition, IFRS S1-S2 ESG disclosure for investor confidence.
- CVP-based pricing → use contribution analysis for product mix, special orders, discount decisions.
- Variance analysis → monthly review against flexible budget; Management by exception; tied to KPIs/incentives.
- Pre-requisites: (i) integrated ERP for real-time data; (ii) cost-conscious culture, top-management commitment; (iii) clear responsibility-centre structure (cost/profit/investment centres).
