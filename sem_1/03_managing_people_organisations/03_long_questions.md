## Mid-Sem scope (Sessions 1-8)

### LQ-1 (Session 1) — Manager's job in modern organisations
**Prompt.** "A manager who lacks people skills cannot succeed today, regardless of technical excellence." Critically evaluate this view using Mintzberg's roles, Katz's skills, and the contemporary challenges (gig, remote, AI, diversity).

**Skeleton**: Define OB → Mintzberg's 10 roles (Interpersonal/Informational/Decisional) → Katz's three skills with ascending importance of conceptual + interpersonal at higher levels → contemporary challenges where soft skills matter most: managing remote/hybrid teams (trust, async communication), gig workers (low-touch motivation), AI (change-leadership), diversity (inclusion behaviours), well-being (empathic leadership). Conclusion: technical skills are the entry ticket; people skills are the differentiator.

### LQ-2 (Session 2) — HRM as strategic partner
**Prompt.** Discuss the evolution of HRM from a transactional Personnel function to a strategic partner. Explain Ulrich's HR roles model and apply it to a typical Indian IT services company.

**Skeleton**: Personnel → IR/welfare; HRM → strategic, integrated, value-creation. Ulrich's 4 roles: Strategic Partner (align HR with business strategy), Administrative Expert (efficient processes), Employee Champion (engagement), Change Agent (culture & transformation). For Indian IT services: Strategic Partner — talent supply for billable revenue; Admin Expert — payroll, compliance for 200,000+ employees; Employee Champion — career growth, engagement to reduce attrition; Change Agent — reskilling for AI/cloud era.

### LQ-3 (Session 3) — Job satisfaction & OCB
**Prompt.** Analyse the determinants and consequences of job satisfaction. How does it relate to OCB, productivity, absenteeism, turnover, and counterproductive work behaviour? What can a manager do to improve job satisfaction in a service-oriented firm?

**Skeleton**: Determinants — job characteristics, manager quality, pay, working conditions, social relations. Consequences (correlations): + OCB, + productivity (modest), + customer satisfaction, − absenteeism, − turnover, − CWB. Manager actions: enrich job (autonomy, skill variety), recognise contribution, fair compensation, supportive supervision, growth opportunities. Specific to service: empower frontline, EI training, reduce role conflict.

### LQ-4 (Session 4) — Personality and Big Five
**Prompt.** Discuss the Big Five personality model. Which traits predict job performance, leadership emergence, and team effectiveness? How can HR use personality assessments responsibly in selection and team formation?

**Skeleton**: OCEAN — Openness (creativity, learning), Conscientiousness (work ethic, reliability — strongest predictor), Extraversion (sales, leadership emergence), Agreeableness (teamwork), Neuroticism (negatively related to performance and well-being). Use in HR: personality test for selection (combine with cognitive ability), team formation (mix of complementary types), career counselling. Cautions: validity, cultural bias, faking, ethical concerns; never sole criterion.

### LQ-5 (Session 5) — Decision-making biases
**Prompt.** Discuss common biases that affect managerial decision making. For each of (a) Anchoring, (b) Confirmation bias, (c) Overconfidence, (d) Escalation of commitment — describe one real-world business example and one debiasing technique.

**Skeleton**: Anchoring — starting price in negotiations; debias by considering range. Confirmation — analyst seeking only data confirming hypothesis; debias by devil's advocate, pre-mortem. Overconfidence — CEO inflated revenue forecasts; debias by base rates, calibration training. Escalation — continuing failed project (Concorde fallacy); debias by sunk-cost framing, separate evaluator. Frame within bounded rationality (Simon).

### LQ-6 (Session 6) — Emotional Intelligence in leadership
**Prompt.** Define Emotional Intelligence using Goleman's model. Explain why EI matters more than IQ for managerial effectiveness. Illustrate with one example each of leader behaviour reflecting Self-awareness, Self-management, Social-awareness, and Relationship-management.

**Skeleton**: EI = ability to perceive, understand, manage, use emotions. Goleman's 4 components: Self-awareness (recognise own emotions — leader who acknowledges stress before snapping), Self-management (regulate — leader who pauses before responding to bad news), Social-awareness (empathy — leader who senses team morale dip), Relationship-management (manage relationships — leader who resolves conflict via dialogue). EI > IQ at senior levels because complex problems require influencing others; meta-analysis evidence; can be developed (vs IQ which is largely fixed).

### LQ-7 (Session 7) — Comparing motivation theories
**Prompt.** Compare and contrast Maslow's Need Hierarchy, Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory, McClelland's Acquired Needs Theory, and Equity Theory. Which is most useful for designing modern compensation and engagement systems? Justify.

**Skeleton**: Maslow — content theory, 5-level hierarchy; intuitive but unproven sequencing. Herzberg — Hygienes (pay, conditions) ≠ Motivators (achievement, growth) — implication: fix hygiene, then add motivators. McClelland — nAch/nAff/nPow vary by individual. Equity — perceived fairness vs others. For modern systems: combine Equity (pay benchmarking) + Herzberg (motivator-rich job design) + Self-Determination (autonomy/competence/relatedness) + Expectancy (line-of-sight to rewards). Recommend a hybrid.

### LQ-8 (Session 8) — Job design & motivation
**Prompt.** Explain the Job Characteristics Model (JCM). Apply it to redesign the role of a customer-service agent in a BPO. Calculate the conceptual change in Motivating Potential Score (MPS) before and after redesign.

**Skeleton**: JCM 5 dimensions: Skill variety, Task identity, Task significance, Autonomy, Feedback. MPS = ((SV + TI + TS)/3) × Autonomy × Feedback. Before redesign (BPO agent answering scripted calls): low SV (3), low TI (3), low TS (4), low Autonomy (3), low Feedback (4) → MPS ≈ 40. After redesign (agent owns end-to-end customer issue, problem-solving authority, peer feedback): SV=6, TI=7, TS=7, Autonomy=7, Feedback=7 → MPS ≈ 327. ~8× increase in motivating potential. Plus job rotation, enrichment, MBO/OKR for goal clarity.

## Comprehensive scope (Sessions 9-16)

### LQ-9 (Session 9) — Group dynamics
**Prompt.** Discuss Tuckman's stages of group development. What can a leader do at each stage to accelerate the team's progress to high performance? Address the risk of groupthink and social loafing.

**Skeleton**: Forming (clarify purpose, set ground rules) → Storming (mediate conflict, encourage diverse views) → Norming (reinforce shared norms, celebrate small wins) → Performing (delegate, remove obstacles, recognise) → Adjourning (debrief, retain learnings). Groupthink mitigation: appoint devil's advocate, encourage dissent, use anonymous voting. Social loafing mitigation: smaller groups, individual accountability, peer review.

### LQ-10 (Session 10) — Effective teams & communication
**Prompt.** A virtual cross-functional team is struggling with poor communication and missed deadlines. Diagnose the issues using OB frameworks, and propose 5 specific interventions to make this team effective.

**Skeleton**: Diagnose: communication barriers (filtering, async overload, emotion in text), low cohesion, no clear roles, accountability dilution, time-zone fatigue. Interventions: (1) Charter — explicit roles, decision rights, escalation paths; (2) Communication norms — sync/async cadence, RACI matrix, shared workspace; (3) Active-listening + concise writing training; (4) Visible KPI dashboard with individual + team metrics; (5) Periodic in-person/video off-sites for trust-building (psychological safety).

### LQ-11 (Session 11) — Leadership theories
**Prompt.** Compare and contrast Trait, Behavioural, Contingency, and Transformational theories of leadership. Which best explains today's business leaders (e.g., Satya Nadella, Indra Nooyi)? Cite examples.

**Skeleton**: Trait — common traits (intelligence, EI, conscientiousness); modest predictive validity. Behavioural — Initiating Structure + Consideration; both matter. Contingency (Fiedler, Hersey-Blanchard) — best style depends on situation/follower readiness. Transformational — Vision, Intellectual stimulation, Inspirational motivation, Individualised consideration, Idealised influence. Nadella: transformational + servant (cultural reset at Microsoft, "growth mindset"). Nooyi: transformational ("Performance with Purpose"), high EI. Conclusion: hybrid view; transformational + EI dominate at top levels.

### LQ-12 (Session 12) — Organisation structure choice
**Prompt.** Discuss the 6 elements of organisation structure. Compare functional, divisional, matrix, and network structures. Which would you recommend for (a) a traditional bank, (b) an e-commerce startup, (c) a global consulting firm? Justify.

**Skeleton**: 6 elements: Specialisation, Departmentalisation, Chain of command, Span of control, Centralisation, Formalisation. Functional (bank — depth in operations, risk, compliance — mechanistic). Divisional (e-commerce — by category/geography for agility, decentralised). Matrix or Network (consulting — project-based, cross-functional, expertise sharing). Trade-offs: functional efficient/silos, divisional duplication/agile, matrix expertise/dual-reporting confusion.

### LQ-13 (Session 13) — Change management
**Prompt.** A 50-year-old Indian PSU is undertaking digital transformation. Apply Lewin's and Kotter's models to plan the change. Discuss likely sources of resistance and propose specific interventions.

**Skeleton**: Lewin — Unfreeze (create dissatisfaction with status quo, communicate burning platform), Change (pilot projects, training, new processes), Refreeze (anchor in policies, KPI, recognition). Kotter 8 steps applied: urgency (PSU losing market share), coalition (CEO + CTO + HR + Union leaders), vision (digital-first PSU), communicate frequently, empower (remove approvers, provide tools), short-term wins (digitise one process), sustain (rolling rollout), anchor (HR rewires hiring/training/promotion). Resistance sources: habit, fear of redundancy, union protection, security; interventions: transparent communication, reskilling, no-layoff commitment, change champions.

### LQ-14 (Session 14) — Recruitment & selection process
**Prompt.** Design an end-to-end recruitment & selection process for a mid-level Data Scientist role in an Indian banking firm. Discuss sourcing channels, selection tools, and ways to mitigate common selection biases.

**Skeleton**: Job analysis → JD (skills, technical, behavioural). Sourcing: LinkedIn, employee referrals, niche communities (Kaggle, GitHub), campus (top tier-1 colleges), specialised consultants. Selection: (1) Application screening; (2) Technical screen (SQL, Python coding); (3) Take-home case study (predictive model on banking data); (4) Technical panel interview (model deep-dive); (5) Behavioural interview using STAR format; (6) Hiring manager + senior leadership; (7) Reference check; (8) Offer. Bias mitigation: structured interview with pre-defined competency questions, scoring rubric, diverse interview panels, blind résumés (remove name/photo for first screen), tracker for diversity outcomes.

### LQ-15 (Session 15) — Compensation strategy
**Prompt.** Discuss internal and external equity in compensation design. Explain pay-for-performance and broadbanding. Recommend a compensation structure for a tech startup that wants to attract senior engineers but has limited cash.

**Skeleton**: Internal equity → job evaluation (point method) → grades. External equity → market salary surveys (Mercer, Aon) → benchmark to median/75th percentile depending on positioning. Pay-for-performance: bonus tied to OKRs, company performance multiplier. Broadbanding: collapse 12 grades to 4 wide bands → flexibility for cross-functional moves and promotions. Tech startup (cash-constrained): below-market base + significant ESOPs (4-year vest, 1-year cliff) + clear roadmap to liquidity event + premium non-cash benefits (best-in-class equipment, learning budget, flexibility); communicate total rewards transparently.

### LQ-16 (Session 16) — Performance management overhaul
**Prompt.** A traditional Indian conglomerate uses annual graphic-rating-scale appraisals tied to bell-curve forced distribution. Employees and managers are dissatisfied. Propose a modern performance management system. Justify each design choice.

**Skeleton**: Diagnose problems with existing: rater bias, halo, leniency, gaming, demoralising forced ranking, late feedback. Redesign: (1) Quarterly OKRs — clear goals; (2) Continuous feedback (1:1 weekly, monthly check-in); (3) BARS for behavioural competencies; (4) 360-degree input for senior roles; (5) Calibration meetings (replace mechanical forced distribution); (6) Decouple performance feedback from compensation conversations; (7) Manager training on coaching skills; (8) Tech platform (e.g., SAP SuccessFactors, Workday) for real-time tracking; (9) Differentiated rewards (high-performers retained with stretch projects + ESOPs); (10) ELC linkage — performance management ties to compensation, promotion, succession, training.

### LQ-17 — Diversity & Inclusion (HSTalks tie-in)
**Prompt.** Discuss the business case for diversity and inclusion. What does "deep-level diversity" mean? Recommend three D&I interventions for an Indian IT services firm with 40 % female workforce but only 10 % female leadership.

**Skeleton**: Business case (innovation, decision quality, market reach, ESG investor preference). Surface vs deep-level. Interventions: (1) Sponsorship programs (not mentorship — sponsors actively advocate for promotions); (2) Bias-free promotion process (structured criteria, calibration); (3) Flexibility infrastructure (back-to-work programs after maternity, hybrid work, childcare support). Track progress via leadership pipeline metrics (% women in each grade).
