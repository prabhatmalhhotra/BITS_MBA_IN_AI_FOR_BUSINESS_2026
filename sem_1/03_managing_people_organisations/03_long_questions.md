### Q1. (15 m) "OB is a multidisciplinary applied behavioural science." Discuss with reference to its contributing disciplines, levels of analysis, and modern challenges.

**Framework**

1. *Definition (1 m)* — OB is the study of behaviour within organisations to improve effectiveness.
2. *Multidisciplinary nature (5 m)* — Psychology (personality, motivation), Sociology (group dynamics, culture), Social Psychology (attitudes, change), Anthropology (cross-cultural), Political science (power). Provides example contributions of each discipline.
3. *Levels of analysis (3 m)* — Individual (perception, motivation), Group (teams, communication), Organisation (structure, culture, change). Each builds on the previous.
4. *Modern challenges (4 m)* — Workforce diversity, globalisation, AI/automation, gig & remote work, mental health, ethical leadership, sustainability/ESG.
5. *Practical relevance (2 m)* — Modern managers use OB diagnostically: identifying root causes of low engagement, building cohesive virtual teams, leading AI-driven transformation. Cite an Indian example (Infosys 2.0 transformation under Vishal Sikka and Salil Parekh).

### Q2. (12 m) Compare and contrast Maslow's hierarchy, Herzberg's two-factor and McClelland's acquired needs theories. Apply them to motivate (i) a young software engineer, (ii) a senior project manager, (iii) a customer-care executive in a telecom call centre.

**Framework**

| Theory | Premise | Practical implication |
|--------|---------|------------------------|
| Maslow | Needs hierarchy — pre-potency rule | Address lower needs (pay, safety) before higher (autonomy, growth) |
| Herzberg | Hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction; motivators drive engagement | Don't expect raises to motivate beyond a point — design intrinsic richness |
| McClelland | Three acquired needs: nAch, nAff, nPow | Match role to dominant need profile |

**Application**

(i) Young software engineer: high nAch + need for autonomy. Provide stretch assignments, intrinsic motivators (challenging problems, recognition), open-source contribution time.
(ii) Senior project manager: nPow (institutional). Empower with budget authority, delegate strategic initiatives, executive visibility.
(iii) Call-centre executive: hygiene critical (working hours, stress, supervisor quality), nAff strong (peer connection). Predictable shifts, peer-led learning groups, recognition rituals, career path to escape the queue.

### Q3. (10 m) Discuss Vroom's Expectancy Theory. Use it to diagnose why incentive schemes sometimes fail to motivate employees.

**Framework**

Theory: Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence.
- Expectancy: belief that effort → performance.
- Instrumentality: belief that performance → reward.
- Valence: value placed on the reward.

**Why incentive schemes fail**

- *Expectancy = 0*: Employees believe the targets are impossible (set too high; ill-equipped). Fix: realistic goals, training, resources.
- *Instrumentality = 0*: Past payouts have been delayed or politicised; employees don't trust the link. Fix: transparent measurement, timely payout.
- *Valence = 0*: Reward doesn't matter to the employee (e.g., extra cash for a senior who values flexibility). Fix: cafeteria-style benefits, individual conversations.

**Indian example**: Sales-incentive scheme at a telecom that based payout on subscriber acquisition without quality of acquisition — sales agents acquired low-revenue subscribers easily; profit dropped. Fix: change KPI to revenue × tenure, restoring instrumentality alignment.

### Q4. (15 m) Mini-case: A 12-member software product team has been working remotely for 18 months. Productivity has plateaued, communication is slower, two senior engineers have resigned, and trust between the front-end and back-end sub-teams is eroding. Diagnose the situation using OB frameworks and recommend a 90-day intervention plan.

**Framework**

**Diagnose**
1. *Group development (Tuckman)*: After two senior exits the team is back at "Forming/Storming" — roles must be re-established.
2. *Trust (Mayer's model)*: Three dimensions broken — ability (uncertain post-resignations), benevolence (perceived blame), integrity (unclear accountability).
3. *Communication*: Async-first works for information transfer but starves the team of relationship-building cues. The plateau likely reflects accumulated micro-misalignments.
4. *Motivation (JCM)*: Autonomy and feedback may have eroded; skill variety reduced if work has become repetitive maintenance.
5. *Conflict*: Likely relationship conflict ("they're slow / they're sloppy") — destructive variant.

**90-day intervention plan**

| Phase | Days | Actions |
|-------|------|---------|
| Stabilise | 1–15 | One-on-ones with all 10 remaining members; short manifesto on team purpose; pair lead engineers from front-end and back-end as "champions" |
| Re-form | 15–45 | Two-day in-person offsite (workshop); rebuild norms; revisit shared goals; clarify roles using RACI |
| Re-engage | 45–75 | Re-design jobs for higher autonomy; rotate weekly meeting facilitator; introduce 2-week "joint pod" mini-projects across sub-teams |
| Reinforce | 75–90 | Run a NPS-style internal team survey; recognise wins publicly; commit to sustained quarterly offsites |

**Metrics**: pulse survey (engagement up 15 points), cycle time (reduced 20%), cross-team PR reviews (rising 30%), retention.

### Q5. (12 m) Compare and contrast trait, behavioural, contingency and contemporary leadership theories. Why has leadership thinking evolved from "what leaders are" to "what leaders do, in context"?

**Framework**

| Approach | Focus | Limitation |
|----------|-------|------------|
| Trait | Personal characteristics | Identifies potential, not behaviour; ignores context |
| Behavioural (Ohio, Michigan, Managerial Grid) | Two dimensions: task & people | Ignores situation; "best style" varies |
| Contingency (Fiedler, Hersey-Blanchard, Path-Goal) | Match style to situation | Complex, harder to apply universally |
| Contemporary (Transformational, Servant, Authentic, e-Leadership) | Vision, ethics, virtual teams | Difficult to measure; cultural dependencies |

**Why the evolution**
- Empirical: traits alone explained <10% of leader effectiveness.
- Practical: managers needed actionable advice (behaviours can be trained, traits cannot).
- Environmental: globalisation, AI, virtual teams require leaders to flex style. Modern thinking acknowledges leaders are made by deliberate practice, situational awareness and ethical commitment, not born great.

**Implication for AI-business leaders**: A blend of transformational (vision for AI), authentic (trust with anxious teams) and ethical leadership (governance) is essential.

### Q6. (10 m) Discuss the five sources of power (French & Raven). With an example from a typical IT services company, explain how a project manager can build power even without high formal authority.

**Framework**

Five sources: legitimate, reward, coercive, expert, referent.

A project manager may have limited formal authority (matrix structure: team members report to functional managers) but can build influence through:
- **Expert power**: deep technical credibility — recognised as the go-to person.
- **Referent power**: charisma, fairness, helping team members succeed → people choose to follow.
- **Information power** (extension): controlling key project knowledge & artefacts.
- **Connection power**: relationships with senior leaders unlock resources.

Tactical moves: rational persuasion (data, clear ROI), inspirational appeals (vision of impact), consultation (involve in decisions), coalition building.

Avoid coercive power — undermines long-term influence.

### Q7. (15 m) Discuss the concept of organisational culture. Explain Schein's three-level model and Hofstede's cultural dimensions. How can a cross-cultural management team avoid culture-clash failures during a global merger?

**Framework**

**Definition (2 m)**: Shared meaning that distinguishes the organisation; the unwritten rules of "how we do things here."

**Schein's model (4 m)**
- Artefacts: visible — dress code, office layout, language, ceremonies.
- Espoused values: stated norms — mission statements, codes of conduct.
- Underlying assumptions: deepest unconscious beliefs — hardest to surface and change.

Example: A start-up's "Move fast and break things" artefact (artefact: open hackathons) often fails on integration with a slow-moving incumbent.

**Hofstede dimensions (4 m)** — PDI, IDV, MAS, UAI, LTO, IND. Examples: India high PDI (employees expect direction); US low PDI (push-back welcomed); Japan high UAI (clear processes); Sweden low MAS (consensus, care).

**Avoiding culture-clash in M&A (5 m)**
1. Pre-deal cultural due diligence (parallel to financial DD).
2. Joint leadership team with explicit decision-making protocol.
3. Cultural integration office reporting to CEO.
4. Symbolic acts: shared offsite, joint town halls, retain meaningful artefacts of acquired firm.
5. Talent retention: identify A-players, secure with retention bonuses.
6. Communication discipline — high-context for India ops, low-context for US.
7. Track culture-fit metrics quarterly (engagement, regrettable attrition, integration milestones).

### Q8. (12 m) Distinguish functional and dysfunctional conflict. Discuss the Thomas-Kilmann conflict-handling styles. When should each style be used?

**Framework**

**Functional vs dysfunctional**
- Functional (task / process at moderate level): improves decision quality, surfaces alternatives, drives innovation.
- Dysfunctional (relationship): emotional, personal — destroys trust, impedes collaboration, increases turnover.

**Thomas-Kilmann five styles** (assertiveness × cooperativeness):

| Style | Use when |
|-------|----------|
| Competing | Quick decisive action needed; unpopular but right action; vital issue |
| Collaborating | Both sides too important to compromise; merging insights; high-trust setting |
| Compromising | Equal-power parties; temporary settlement; time pressure |
| Avoiding | Trivial issue; no power to influence; need cooling-off |
| Accommodating | Issue more important to other; build social credit; you are wrong |

Best leaders use Collaborating as default; choose other styles consciously based on situation.

### Q9. (10 m) Define organisational structure. Compare functional, divisional and matrix structures, and recommend when each is appropriate.

**Framework**

**Definition**: Formal arrangement of jobs, tasks, authority and communication.

| Structure | Strengths | Weaknesses | When ideal |
|-----------|-----------|------------|------------|
| Functional | Specialisation, efficiency, expertise | Silos, slow cross-function decisions, narrow career paths | Single product, stable environment, small/medium size |
| Divisional | Customer/product/geo focus, accountability, faster decisions | Duplication, sub-optimisation | Multiple products, geographies, large diverse firms |
| Matrix | Dual focus, expertise sharing, flexibility | Conflicting authority, ambiguous, complex | Project-based work, knowledge industries |

Modern hybrids: matrix for product teams + functional centres of excellence (Spotify model), agile squads + chapters + tribes.

### Q10. (15 m) Mini-case: A 25-year-old PSU is rolling out an AI-based customer-service chatbot. Forty per cent of front-line agents fear job loss. Senior management has assured no layoffs but morale is low and unions have raised concerns. Apply Kotter's 8-step change model and ADKAR to design the change strategy.

**Framework**

**Kotter's 8 steps**

1. **Create urgency** — share data on rising customer expectations and competitor adoption (LIC vs new private insurers).
2. **Build a coalition** — include union reps, line managers, IT, HR, customer-care leadership.
3. **Form a vision** — "augmented agents, smarter customers, better service."
4. **Communicate the vision** — town halls, FAQs, explainer videos, written commitments on no-layoffs and reskilling.
5. **Empower action** — remove barriers (e.g., outdated KPIs, fear of being measured by AI). Provide training credits.
6. **Generate short-term wins** — pilot in 2 branches; publish metrics on agent productivity, satisfaction.
7. **Sustain acceleration** — extend pilots, embed in business plan, include in performance reviews.
8. **Anchor in culture** — celebrate stories of redeployed agents, integrate AI into onboarding curriculum.

**ADKAR (individual transitions)**

- **Awareness**: why change is needed (customer expectations, competition).
- **Desire**: WIIFM — easier work, fewer mundane queries, possible promotion paths to AI supervisors.
- **Knowledge**: training on chatbot interface, escalation rules, content authoring.
- **Ability**: hands-on labs; assigned mentors; measurable competency benchmarks.
- **Reinforcement**: monthly recognition for early adopters; updated KPIs; long-term role redesign.

**Risk mitigation**: union MoU on retraining commitments; transparency on metrics; ethics committee for AI deployment.

### Q11. (10 m) Discuss the Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham). How can the model be used to redesign a routine call-centre job?

**Framework**

**Five core dimensions** → Critical psychological states → Outcomes (motivation, satisfaction, performance, low absenteeism).
- Skill variety
- Task identity
- Task significance
- Autonomy
- Feedback

MPS = ((SV + TI + TS)/3) × Autonomy × Feedback.

**Routine call-centre job redesign**

| Dimension | Current | Redesign |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Skill variety | Repetitive scripts | Cross-train on adjacent products, allow craft of narrative |
| Task identity | Each call isolated | Assign customer accounts; agent owns end-to-end relationship |
| Task significance | Disconnected from impact | Show downstream impact: customer reviews, NPS lift |
| Autonomy | Heavy script enforcement | Discretion within guardrails (e.g., issue ₹500 goodwill credit without escalation) |
| Feedback | Annual review | Real-time dashboard + weekly coaching |

Result: higher engagement, lower attrition, improved CSAT.

### Q12. (12 m) "Strong organisational culture is a double-edged sword." Discuss with examples. How can leaders manage culture during a digital / AI transformation?

**Framework**

**Strong culture — pros (3 m)**: identity, alignment, lower coordination costs, higher commitment, talent magnet (Apple, Tata, Mahindra Rise).

**Cons (3 m)**: barrier to change ("not how we do it here"), barrier to diversity, M&A integration risk (Daimler-Chrysler), insulation from external feedback (Nokia pre-iPhone).

**Managing culture during AI transformation (6 m)**

1. **Articulate the new identity**: from "process-led" to "data + judgement-led."
2. **Recruit AI-native talent** but pair with seasoned domain experts.
3. **Symbolic acts**: leaders openly use AI tools, share experiments and failures.
4. **New rituals**: monthly AI demos, internal hackathons, AI ethics reviews.
5. **Story-telling**: surface employee-led wins to make AI tangible and human.
6. **Reskilling at scale**: budget allocation, certification incentives.
7. **Guardrails**: ethics committee, fairness audits, human-in-the-loop principle, transparent communication on what AI will and will not do.
8. **Measure**: engagement scores, AI tool adoption, % decisions involving data, attrition by segment.

Indian example: Tata Steel's culture journey from production-led to digital-first, anchored in safety, "ownership" and respect for humans alongside data.

**Conclusion**: A strong culture isn't an obstacle to transformation if leaders re-anchor the assumptions in service of the new strategy, while preserving the values that make the firm distinctive.
