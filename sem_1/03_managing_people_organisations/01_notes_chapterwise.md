# Managing People & Organisations — Chapter-wise Notes

> Anchored to Robbins & Judge (T1). Application examples drawn from Indian corporate contexts.

## Chapter 1 — Introduction to Organisational Behaviour

### 1.1 What is OB?
A field of study that investigates the impact individuals, groups and structure have on behaviour within organisations, with a view to applying such knowledge to improve effectiveness.

Three levels of analysis: **Individual → Group → Organisation system**.

### 1.2 Why OB matters
- Workforce diversity (gender, age, neurodiversity, remote-onsite)
- Globalisation; cross-cultural teams
- Technology & AI changing the nature of work
- Knowledge workers replacing manual workers
- Gig and freelance economy
- Mental health and wellbeing as legitimate corporate concerns

### 1.3 Disciplines contributing to OB

| Discipline | Contribution |
|------------|--------------|
| Psychology | Personality, learning, motivation, leadership, training |
| Sociology | Group dynamics, communication, organisational culture |
| Social psychology | Behaviour change, attitudes, conflict |
| Anthropology | Comparative cultures, organisational environment |
| Political science | Conflict, power, intra-org politics |

### 1.4 Models of OB (Davis)

| Model | Managerial assumption | Employee orientation |
|-------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Autocratic | Authority | Obedience |
| Custodial | Economic resources | Security |
| Supportive | Leadership | Job performance |
| Collegial | Partnership | Self-discipline |
| System | Trust, community, meaning | Psychological ownership |

Modern best-in-class organisations (Google, Tata, Infosys) lean toward Supportive / Collegial / System.

### 60-second recap
OB is the science of behaviour at work. It blends psychology, sociology and organisation theory. The aim is to make organisations more humane *and* more effective — these are not in conflict.

## Chapter 2 — Individual Behaviour

### 2.1 Personality

**Big Five (OCEAN)** — most validated framework:
- **O**penness to experience — creative, curious vs conventional
- **C**onscientiousness — disciplined, organised vs impulsive
- **E**xtraversion — sociable, assertive vs reserved
- **A**greeableness — cooperative, trusting vs antagonistic
- **N**euroticism — anxious, moody vs emotionally stable

Conscientiousness is the strongest predictor of job performance across nearly all roles.

**MBTI**: 16 types from 4 dichotomies (E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P). Popular in industry but psychometric validity weaker than Big Five.

Other traits: locus of control (internal vs external), self-monitoring (high = adapts behaviour to context), Machiavellianism, narcissism, self-esteem.

### 2.2 Perception
The process by which individuals organise and interpret sensory impressions.

**Attribution theory** (Heider): how we explain causes of behaviour. We classify causes as internal (dispositional) or external (situational), based on:
- Distinctiveness — does the person behave this way only in this situation?
- Consensus — do others behave the same way?
- Consistency — does the person behave this way regularly?

**Common perception biases**:
- Fundamental attribution error — we over-attribute others' behaviour to internal factors.
- Self-serving bias — we attribute our successes internally and failures externally.
- Halo — extending overall impression from one trait.
- Stereotyping — judging based on group membership.
- Projection — assuming others share our traits.
- Contrast — comparing recently-encountered persons.

### 2.3 Learning
Permanent change in behaviour due to experience.
- **Classical conditioning** (Pavlov) — paired stimuli, automatic response.
- **Operant conditioning** (Skinner) — behaviour shaped by consequences (reinforcement, punishment).
- **Social learning** (Bandura) — observation + imitation. Foundation of mentoring and behaviour-modelling training.

Reinforcement schedules: continuous, fixed-interval, variable-interval, fixed-ratio, variable-ratio. Variable-ratio is most resistant to extinction (why slot machines work).

### 2.4 Values & Attitudes
- **Values**: enduring beliefs about what is right/desirable. Schwartz's circumplex of 10 universal values.
- **Attitudes**: evaluative statements about objects, people, or events. Three components: cognitive (belief), affective (feeling), behavioural (intention).
- **Job satisfaction**: positive feeling about one's job. Predicts organisational citizenship behaviour, customer satisfaction, lower turnover.
- **Organisational commitment**: affective (emotional), continuance (cost), normative (obligation).

### 2.5 Emotions & Moods
- **Affective Events Theory**: workplace events trigger emotional reactions, which influence attitudes and behaviour.
- **Emotional labour**: managing emotions to satisfy job requirements (smiling at customers).
- **Emotional Intelligence (EI)** — Goleman's 4 dimensions:
  1. Self-awareness
  2. Self-management
  3. Social awareness (empathy)
  4. Relationship management

EI is increasingly important in AI-augmented workplaces — humans add value where machines cannot (empathy, creativity, judgement).

### 60-second recap
People are predictable in patterns: their personality (Big Five), how they perceive (with biases), how they learn (reinforcement), what they value, how they feel. Effective managers diagnose first, then act.

## Chapter 3 — Motivation

### 3.1 Definition
Processes that account for an individual's intensity, direction and persistence of effort toward attaining a goal.

### 3.2 Early theories

#### Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs
Five-level pyramid: Physiological → Safety → Social/Belongingness → Esteem → Self-actualisation. Higher needs become salient only after lower needs are reasonably satisfied. Modern critique: not strictly hierarchical, cultural variations.

#### McGregor's X and Y
- Theory X: People dislike work, must be coerced. Managers control closely.
- Theory Y: People view work as natural, seek responsibility. Managers empower.

#### Herzberg's Two-Factor (Motivation-Hygiene)
- **Hygiene factors** (extrinsic): salary, work conditions, company policy, supervision. Their absence causes dissatisfaction; presence merely prevents it.
- **Motivators** (intrinsic): achievement, recognition, work itself, responsibility, growth. Drive satisfaction and motivation.

Implication: paying more solves dissatisfaction but doesn't motivate. Job redesign is needed.

#### McClelland's Acquired Needs
- nAch — need for achievement
- nAff — need for affiliation
- nPow — need for power (personal vs institutional)

Best managers are usually high in nPow (institutional) and moderate nAff.

### 3.3 Contemporary theories

#### Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)
Three innate needs: autonomy, competence, relatedness. Intrinsic motivation thrives when satisfied. Extrinsic rewards can crowd out intrinsic motivation if they undermine autonomy.

#### Goal-Setting Theory (Locke)
SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) with feedback drive performance. Difficult goals + commitment = highest performance.

#### Expectancy Theory (Vroom)
Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence
- Expectancy: effort → performance probability
- Instrumentality: performance → reward probability
- Valence: value placed on the reward

If any term is zero, motivation is zero.

#### Equity Theory (Adams)
Employees compare their input/output ratio to a referent. Perceived inequity → tension → behavioural change (reduce input, demand output, change referent, distort perception, leave).

#### Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham)
Five core job dimensions:
- Skill variety
- Task identity
- Task significance
- Autonomy
- Feedback

Combine into Motivating Potential Score (MPS) = ((SV + TI + TS)/3) × Autonomy × Feedback. Higher MPS → higher intrinsic motivation, satisfaction, lower absenteeism.

### 3.4 Application in workplace
- Match rewards to dominant needs (use nAch for individual targets, nPow for managerial roles).
- Job enrichment beats job rotation for sustained engagement.
- Pay-for-performance only when (i) measurable performance and (ii) employees believe in the link.
- Recognition is cheap, intrinsic, and powerful — under-used.

### 60-second recap
Maslow/Herzberg/McGregor are foundational frames. Modern theories (Vroom, Goal-setting, JCM) are diagnostic and actionable. Motivation = ability × opportunity × willingness — never blame the employee in isolation.

## Chapter 4 — Group & Team Dynamics

### 4.1 Group vs Team

| Group | Team |
|-------|------|
| Shared information goal | Collective performance goal |
| Neutral / negative synergy | Positive synergy |
| Individual accountability | Mutual accountability |
| Random / varied skills | Complementary skills |

### 4.2 Tuckman's stages of group development
1. **Forming** — orientation, polite, exploration of structure.
2. **Storming** — conflict over roles, control.
3. **Norming** — close relationships, cohesion, agreed norms.
4. **Performing** — fully functional, focus on task.
5. **Adjourning** — wrap-up, dissolution.

Manager's job is different at each stage: structure in Forming, mediation in Storming, support in Norming, get-out-of-the-way in Performing.

### 4.3 Group properties
- **Roles** — expected behaviour patterns (initiator, harmoniser, devil's advocate). Belbin defined 9 team roles.
- **Norms** — accepted standards of behaviour. Influence behaviour with minimal monitoring.
- **Status** — socially defined position. Inequity creates tension.
- **Size** — small groups (5–7) better for action, large for fact-finding.
- **Cohesiveness** — closeness; high cohesion + high alignment with goals → high performance. High cohesion + low alignment → low performance.

### 4.4 Group decision-making
**Pros**: more information, diverse views, acceptance.
**Cons**: time, conformity, dominance, ambiguous responsibility.

**Groupthink** (Janis): cohesive groups suppress dissenting views, leading to poor decisions (Bay of Pigs, Challenger). Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, rationalisation, peer pressure, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, mind-guards.
Prevention: appoint a devil's advocate, encourage dissent, leader speaks last, second-chance meetings.

**Groupshift / polarisation**: group decisions tend to be more extreme than individual decisions of members.

Techniques: brainstorming, nominal group technique, Delphi (anonymous expert iterations), e-meetings.

### 4.5 Team effectiveness model
**Context** (resources, leadership, climate of trust, performance evaluation) → **Composition** (member abilities, personality, role allocation, diversity, size) → **Work design** (autonomy, skill variety, identity, significance) → **Process** (common purpose, specific goals, team efficacy, conflict, social loafing).

### 4.6 Virtual teams (highly relevant for WILP)
Distributed teams using technology to collaborate. Challenges: reduced social cues, harder trust-building, time-zone management. Best practices: established communication norms, video for hard conversations, regular asynchronous updates, occasional in-person meetings.

### 60-second recap
Teams beat groups when synergy is needed. Manage Tuckman stages explicitly. Watch for groupthink in cohesive teams. Virtual teams need extra deliberate trust-building.

## Chapter 5 — Communication

### 5.1 Process model
Sender → Encoding → Channel → Decoding → Receiver → Feedback. Noise can interfere at any stage.

### 5.2 Channels
- **Formal**: hierarchy-based (downward, upward, lateral).
- **Informal / grapevine**: rumour networks; surprisingly accurate (75%+).

### 5.3 Barriers
Filtering, selective perception, information overload, emotions, language, silence, lying, communication apprehension, gender differences.

### 5.4 Cross-cultural communication
High-context (Japan, China, India — meaning embedded in context, relationships) vs low-context (US, Germany — meaning explicit in words). Idioms, body language, eye contact differ.

### 5.5 Digital & remote communication
Async-first culture (written-by-default), clear status updates, "default to action" + "disagree and commit" patterns. Avoid "reply-all" bloat. Use video for relationship-building, async for information transfer.

### 60-second recap
Communication breaks at encoding, channel, or decoding. Cultural and personal filters distort meaning. Choose the right medium for the message — high-emotional content needs synchronous + visual.

## Chapter 6 — Leadership

### 6.1 Trait theories
Early research: leaders share traits (intelligence, drive, integrity, self-confidence, knowledge). Limitation: traits identify potential, not behaviour.

### 6.2 Behavioural theories

**Ohio State Studies**: two dimensions
- **Initiating Structure** — task focus
- **Consideration** — relationship focus

**Michigan Studies**: similar — production-oriented vs employee-oriented.

**Managerial Grid** (Blake & Mouton): 9×9 grid plotting concern for production vs concern for people. Best style 9,9 (Team) — high on both.

### 6.3 Contingency theories

**Fiedler's Contingency Model**: leadership effectiveness depends on situation. LPC scale measures task vs relationship orientation. Match leader to situation favourableness (leader-member relations, task structure, position power).

**Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership**: leader style adjusts to follower readiness:
- R1 (low ability, low willingness) → S1 Telling
- R2 (low ability, high willingness) → S2 Selling
- R3 (high ability, low willingness) → S3 Participating
- R4 (high ability, high willingness) → S4 Delegating

**Path-Goal Theory** (House): leader clarifies path to goal. Four styles: directive, supportive, participative, achievement-oriented; choose based on follower characteristics + environmental factors.

### 6.4 Contemporary theories
- **Transformational leadership** (Bass): inspire through vision, intellectual stimulation, individualised consideration, idealised influence (4 I's). Outperforms transactional in dynamic settings.
- **Transactional leadership**: contingent reward, management-by-exception. Effective in stable settings.
- **Charismatic leadership**: vision, willingness to take risks, sensitivity to environment & follower needs, unconventional behaviour.
- **Authentic leadership**: self-awareness, transparency, balanced processing, internalised moral perspective. Builds trust.
- **Servant leadership** (Greenleaf): leader's first priority is to serve others.
- **Ethical leadership**: just decision-making, honesty, role-modelling.
- **e-Leadership**: leading distributed/virtual teams; demands hyper-clear communication and outcome-based trust.

### 6.5 Trust and credibility
Three dimensions: integrity, benevolence, ability. Trust takes years to build, seconds to break. Without trust, no leadership.

### 60-second recap
Trait → behavioural → contingency → contemporary. No single best style. Match style to follower readiness and situation. Modern leaders blend transformational vision with authentic ethics.

## Chapter 7 — Power, Politics & Conflict

### 7.1 French & Raven's bases of power

| Source | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Legitimate | From position in hierarchy |
| Reward | Ability to give desired rewards |
| Coercive | Ability to punish |
| Expert | Special knowledge or skill |
| Referent | Identification, charisma |

Personal power (expert + referent) > positional power (legitimate + reward + coercive) for sustained influence.

### 7.2 Politics
Activities not required as part of formal role but influence the distribution of advantages and disadvantages within the organisation.
- **Antecedents**: scarce resources, ambiguous rules, performance ambiguity, low trust, role conflict, individual self-monitoring, organisational culture.
- **Outcomes**: stress, turnover, decreased job satisfaction.
- **Coping**: build network, develop expertise, image management (legitimate), avoid backstabbing, document decisions.

### 7.3 Conflict

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Task conflict | Disagreement over goals/content — moderate level can be productive |
| Relationship conflict | Interpersonal — almost always destructive |
| Process conflict | How work gets done — low level useful |

**Conflict process**: potential opposition → cognition & personalisation → intentions → behaviour → outcomes.

**Thomas-Kilmann conflict-handling styles** (mapped on assertiveness × cooperativeness):
- Competing — high A, low C
- Collaborating — high A, high C
- Compromising — moderate both
- Avoiding — low A, low C
- Accommodating — low A, high C

Choose style based on stakes and relationships. Collaborating is ideal but expensive (time).

### 7.4 Negotiation
- **Distributive** (win-lose, fixed pie) — bargaining over price.
- **Integrative** (win-win, expand pie) — focus on interests, not positions.

Five-step process: preparation → defining ground rules → clarification & justification → bargaining & problem-solving → closure & implementation.

BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement) defines your walk-away.

### 60-second recap
Power has many sources; the personal kinds (expert, referent) endure. Politics is inevitable — manage it ethically. Functional conflict (task) helps; dysfunctional conflict (relationship) kills. Always prepare your BATNA.

## Chapter 8 — Organisational Structure & Design

### 8.1 Six elements
1. **Work specialisation** — division of labour into specific tasks.
2. **Departmentalisation** — function, product, geography, customer, process.
3. **Chain of command** — unbroken line of authority.
4. **Span of control** — number of subordinates per manager. Wider = flatter, faster, cheaper but harder to monitor.
5. **Centralisation vs decentralisation** — where decisions are made.
6. **Formalisation** — degree to which jobs are standardised.

### 8.2 Common designs

| Design | Strength | Weakness |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Simple | Speed, flexibility | Risky if owner fails |
| Functional | Efficiency, specialised expertise | Silos, slow cross-function decisions |
| Divisional | Customer/product focus | Duplication of resources |
| Matrix | Dual focus, expertise sharing | Conflicting authority, complex |
| Network/virtual | Flexibility, global access | Loss of control, dependence on partners |
| Flat / holacratic | Empowerment | Coordination challenges at scale |

### 8.3 Mechanistic vs Organic

| Mechanistic | Organic |
|-------------|---------|
| High specialisation | Cross-functional teams |
| Rigid hierarchy | Free flow of info |
| Many rules | Few rules |
| Centralised | Decentralised |
| Best for stable environments | Best for dynamic environments |

### 8.4 Determinants of structure
- **Strategy** (Chandler: structure follows strategy). Innovation strategy → organic; cost-min strategy → mechanistic.
- **Size** — larger firms tend to be more formalised.
- **Technology** — routine tech → mechanistic.
- **Environment** — turbulent environment → organic.

### 60-second recap
Six structural elements + matching design. Modern firms increasingly hybrid (matrix/agile). Structure must follow strategy and environment, not vice versa.

## Chapter 9 — Organisational Culture

### 9.1 Definition
Shared meaning held by members that distinguishes the organisation from others. The "way things are done around here."

### 9.2 Schein's three levels
1. **Artefacts** — visible (dress code, office layout, ceremonies).
2. **Espoused values** — stated norms (mission statement, codes).
3. **Basic underlying assumptions** — unconscious, taken-for-granted beliefs. The deepest layer; hardest to change.

### 9.3 How culture is created and sustained
- Founders' philosophy → Selection of compatible people → Socialisation of new hires → Top-management actions reinforce.

Socialisation phases: prearrival → encounter → metamorphosis.

### 9.4 Strong vs weak culture
**Strong**: core values widely shared & intensely held. Higher cohesion, lower turnover, can become inflexible.
**Weak**: looser; more adaptable but less aligned.

### 9.5 Functions and dysfunctions
**Functions**: identity, commitment, control, behavioural consistency, sense-making.
**Dysfunctions**: barrier to change, barrier to diversity, barrier to mergers (cultural clash).

### 9.6 Hofstede's cultural dimensions
- **Power distance** (PDI) — acceptance of hierarchy. India high; Sweden low.
- **Individualism vs Collectivism** (IDV) — US high; India moderate.
- **Masculinity vs Femininity** (MAS) — competitiveness vs care. Japan high masculine.
- **Uncertainty avoidance** (UAI) — discomfort with ambiguity. Greece high; Singapore low.
- **Long-term vs Short-term orientation** (LTO) — China high.
- **Indulgence vs Restraint** (IND) — gratification orientation.

### 60-second recap
Culture is the iceberg: artefacts above, assumptions below. Strong culture = double-edged sword. Hofstede helps navigate cross-cultural management.

## Chapter 10 — Organisational Change & Development

### 10.1 Forces of change
Economic (recession, currency), competitive (new entrants, AI), technology (digital, automation), demographic (millennials, gig), social (DEI, ESG), regulatory.

### 10.2 Resistance to change
- **Individual sources**: habit, security, economic factors, fear of unknown, selective information processing.
- **Organisational sources**: structural inertia, limited focus of change, group inertia, threat to expertise, threat to power, threat to resource allocations.

Tactics to overcome: education + communication, participation, building support, implementing fairly, manipulation/cooptation (last resort), coercion (final).

### 10.3 Change models

#### Lewin's 3-step
- **Unfreeze** — create motivation; force-field analysis (driving forces vs restraining forces).
- **Change** — implement new state; train, communicate.
- **Refreeze** — reinforce; integrate into culture.

#### Kotter's 8-step
1. Create urgency
2. Form a powerful coalition
3. Create a vision
4. Communicate the vision
5. Empower action (remove obstacles)
6. Generate short-term wins
7. Sustain acceleration
8. Anchor in culture

#### ADKAR (Prosci)
- **A**wareness, **D**esire, **K**nowledge, **A**bility, **R**einforcement. Useful for individual transitions.

### 10.4 Organisational Development (OD) interventions
- Sensitivity training (T-groups)
- Survey feedback
- Process consultation
- Team building
- Inter-group development
- Appreciative inquiry

### 10.5 Managing AI-driven transformation (highly relevant for this MBA)
- Communicate the "why" — what business problem AI solves, not the technology.
- Reskilling roadmap; identify roles getting redesigned vs displaced.
- Quick-win pilots before enterprise scale.
- Ethical guardrails: bias audit, explainability, human-in-the-loop for decisions affecting people.
- Address fear of job loss with retraining + clear redeployment paths.

### 60-second recap
Resistance is normal — expect, diagnose, address. Lewin frames change in 3 steps; Kotter operationalises in 8; ADKAR personalises. AI change uniquely needs psychological safety + reskilling.

## Chapter 11 — Human Resource Management Foundations

### 11.1 HRM functions
**Acquisition** — workforce planning, recruitment, selection, onboarding.
**Development** — training, career planning, performance management.
**Motivation** — compensation, benefits, rewards, employee engagement.
**Maintenance** — retention, employee relations, exits.

### 11.2 Job analysis → JD + JS
- **Job description**: what the job involves (duties, responsibilities, working conditions).
- **Job specification**: what the job requires from the person (skills, qualifications, traits).

### 11.3 Recruitment and selection
- **Sources**: internal (transfers, promotions, referrals) vs external (job boards, campus, agencies, social media).
- **Selection process**: application → tests (cognitive, personality, work sample) → interviews (structured > unstructured) → reference check → medical → offer.
- Validity & reliability of selection methods. Structured interview validity ~ 0.51; cognitive ability test ~ 0.51; work sample ~ 0.54.

### 11.4 Training & development
**Training** — short-term, job-specific. **Development** — long-term, career-oriented.

Training methods: on-the-job (job rotation, mentoring), off-the-job (lectures, e-learning, simulations), blended.

ADDIE model: **A**nalysis, **D**esign, **D**evelopment, **I**mplementation, **E**valuation.

Kirkpatrick's 4 levels of training evaluation: Reaction, Learning, Behaviour, Results.

### 11.5 Performance management
- Continuous feedback > annual ratings.
- 360-degree feedback for behavioural roles.
- Forced ranking — controversial; can demotivate.
- OKRs (Objectives & Key Results) — popular in tech firms.

### 11.6 Compensation
**Direct**: base salary, variable pay (bonuses, commissions, ESOPs).
**Indirect**: benefits (insurance, leave, gratuity, food coupons).

Compensation philosophy: lead, lag, or match the market.

### 11.7 Employee engagement & retention
Engagement = emotional commitment + discretionary effort. Drivers: meaningful work, fair pay, growth, manager quality, recognition, autonomy.

### 11.8 HR Analytics (link to AI)
Data-driven HR decisions: predicting attrition, optimising recruiting funnel, employee sentiment analysis, skills-graph mapping, fairness audits of hiring algorithms. Foundation for the **AI in HRM** elective in Sem 4.

### 60-second recap
HRM is the operational arm of OB principles. Get hiring right, develop continuously, motivate fairly, retain proactively. AI is changing every HR sub-function — be the manager who leads the change, not the one who is changed.

## Chapter 12 — Stress, Wellbeing & Organisational Effectiveness

### 12.1 Stress
A psychological response to environmental demands.

**Sources**: environmental (economic, political), organisational (task, role, interpersonal), personal (family, finances).

**Symptoms**: physiological (BP, heart disease), psychological (anxiety, depression), behavioural (absenteeism, turnover, alcohol use).

**Coping**: time management, exercise, social support, relaxation, meditation. Organisational interventions: realistic job previews, employee assistance programmes (EAP), wellness programmes.

### 12.2 Work-life integration
Not balance (zero-sum) but integration: design boundaries that suit the individual. Flexible work, remote, async-first.

### 12.3 Diversity, Equity & Inclusion (DEI)
- **Diversity** = mix.
- **Equity** = fair access and treatment.
- **Inclusion** = whether the mix is integrated and heard.

Business case: better decisions (diverse perspectives reduce groupthink), wider talent pool, better customer understanding.

### 12.4 Ethics in OB / HR
- Whistle-blowing, sexual harassment, dignity at work.
- POSH Act (Indian context).
- Privacy in employee monitoring (especially for AI-based productivity tools).
- Algorithmic fairness in hiring/firing.

### 60-second recap
People perform best when well. Stress and wellbeing are competitive advantages, not soft topics. DEI + ethics are the modern manager's table-stakes, not aspirations.
