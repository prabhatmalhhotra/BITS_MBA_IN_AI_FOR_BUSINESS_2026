How is an organisation designed? Why do some companies have 10 hierarchy levels and others have 2? This session covers structural design choices and their impact on behaviour.

Real-World Problem

Reliance Jio disrupted telecom with a flat, agile structure — while BSNL's bureaucracy couldn't keep up

When Jio launched in 2016, it moved at startup speed despite being part of Reliance (a conglomerate). Decision-making was centralised at the top (Mukesh Ambani) but execution was fast because of a relatively flat structure and empowered teams. BSNL, a government bureaucracy with 10+ levels of hierarchy, couldn't respond for months.

Structure determines speed. Session 12 explains how organisations are designed and why structure must match strategy.

1 Six Key Elements of Structure Beginner

ElementKey QuestionOptions
Work SpecialisationHow much are tasks subdivided?High (assembly line) vs Low (craftsmanship)
DepartmentalisationHow are jobs grouped?Function, Product, Geography, Process, Customer, Matrix
Chain of CommandWho reports to whom?Unity of command; authority & responsibility
Span of ControlHow many subordinates per manager?Narrow (5-6) vs Wide (20+)
CentralisationWhere are decisions made?Centralised (top) vs Decentralised (spread)
FormalisationHow rule-bound are jobs?High (McDonald's SOP) vs Low (Google 20% time)

2 Mechanistic vs Organic Intermediate

Mechanistic (Burns & Stalker)

  • High specialisation, rigid departmentalisation
  • Clear chain of command
  • Narrow span, centralised
  • High formalisation
  • Best for: stable environments, efficiency, mass production

Example: Indian Railways, BSNL, Government departments

Organic

  • Cross-functional teams, flexible roles
  • Free flow of information
  • Wide span, decentralised
  • Low formalisation
  • Best for: dynamic environments, innovation

Example: Flipkart, Razorpay, Google India

4 What Determines Structure? Advanced

FactorHow It Shapes Structure
StrategyInnovation → organic; Cost minimisation → mechanistic; Imitation → hybrid
SizeBigger orgs tend toward mechanistic (more formalisation, specialisation)
TechnologyWoodward: Unit production (organic), Mass production (mechanistic), Process (organic)
EnvironmentDynamic/complex → organic; Stable/simple → mechanistic
Trending Example (2024-25)

Holacracy and flat structures in Indian startups: Some Indian startups (like Gojek India and parts of Flipkart) experimented with flat, self-organising structures inspired by Zappos' holacracy. Most reverted to some hierarchy after scaling past 200-300 employees — pure flatness creates decision-making bottlenecks at scale.

Session 12 — Key Takeaways

  • 6 elements: Specialisation, Departmentalisation, Chain of command, Span, Centralisation, Formalisation
  • Mechanistic (rigid, formal, hierarchical) vs Organic (flexible, decentralised, adaptive)
  • Designs: Simple, Bureaucracy, Matrix, Virtual, Team-based
  • Determinants: Strategy (innovation/cost), Size, Technology (Woodward), Environment
  • Matrix = dual reporting; good for expertise sharing but causes power conflicts
