Motivation Concepts

Why do some people work passionately while others do the bare minimum? This session covers every major motivation theory — from Maslow's pyramid to Vroom's formula.

Real-World Problem

Tata Steel offers the same salary to all entry-level engineers — yet some are driven and some coast. Why?

At Tata Steel Jamshedpur, 50 graduate engineers join in the same batch. Same pay, same designation, same office. Yet within a year, some are taking initiative, volunteering for tough projects, and getting noticed — while others do the bare minimum. If pay is equal, what drives the difference?

The answer lies in motivation — the internal forces that direct behaviour. Session 7 covers every major motivation theory from Maslow to Vroom, giving you the conceptual toolkit to understand why people do what they do.

1 What is Motivation? Beginner

Motivation = the processes that account for an individual's intensity (how hard), direction (toward what), and persistence (for how long) of effort toward attaining a goal.

2 Early Theories of Motivation Intermediate

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (1943)

Five levels of needs arranged in a pyramid. Lower needs must be (largely) satisfied before higher needs activate:

5. Self-Actualisation
Reaching full potential, creativity, purpose
▲
4. Esteem
Recognition, status, achievement, respect
▲
3. Social / Belonging
Friendship, love, team, community
▲
2. Safety
Job security, health insurance, savings
▲
1. Physiological
Food, water, shelter, salary for basics

Alderfer's ERG Theory

Simplified Maslow into 3 categories: Existence (physiological + safety), Relatedness (social), Growth (esteem + self-actualisation). Key difference: frustration-regression — if higher needs frustrated, person regresses to lower needs.

McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y

Theory X (Pessimistic)

  • People dislike work
  • Must be coerced, controlled, threatened
  • Avoid responsibility
  • Want security above all

Theory Y (Optimistic)

  • Work is natural as play
  • Self-directed when committed to goals
  • Seek responsibility
  • Creative and innovative

Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory (Motivation-Hygiene)

Hygiene Factors (Extrinsic)

Presence prevents dissatisfaction but does NOT motivate

  • Pay, Job security
  • Working conditions
  • Company policies
  • Supervision quality
  • Relationship with peers

Motivators (Intrinsic)

Presence creates motivation and satisfaction

  • Achievement
  • Recognition
  • Work itself
  • Responsibility
  • Growth/Advancement
Exam Tip: Herzberg's key insight: the opposite of satisfaction is NOT dissatisfaction — it's "no satisfaction." Hygiene factors and motivators operate on separate continuums. This is a very common trick question.

McClelland's Three Needs

3 Contemporary Theories Advanced

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) — Deci & Ryan

Intrinsic motivation is driven by three innate needs:

Autonomy
Control over own work
Competence
Feeling effective, mastery
Relatedness
Connection with others

Cognitive Evaluation Theory (subset of SDT): Extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation if perceived as controlling.

Goal-Setting Theory (Locke)

Specific, difficult goals + feedback = higher performance. Goals work because they direct attention, energise effort, increase persistence, and foster strategy development.

SMART Goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.

Equity Theory (Adams)

People compare their input/outcome ratio to a referent. If inequity is perceived, they act to restore equity:

My Outcomes / My Inputs   vs   Other's Outcomes / Other's Inputs

Responses to inequity: Change inputs (work less), change outcomes (ask for raise), distort perception, change referent, or leave.

Justice TypeFocusExample
DistributiveFairness of outcomes"She got a bigger bonus for same work"
ProceduralFairness of process"The promotion criteria were transparent"
InteractionalFairness of treatment/communication"My manager explained the decision respectfully"

Expectancy Theory (Vroom)

Motivation = Expectancy (E→P) × Instrumentality (P→R) × Valence (R value)
Expectancy
Can my effort lead to good performance?
→
Instrumentality
Will performance lead to rewards?
→
Valence
Do I value the reward?

If any of the three is zero, motivation is zero. All three must be positive.

Daily Life Example

Studying for exam: Expectancy = "If I study hard, will I score well?" (depends on difficulty). Instrumentality = "Will a high score lead to a good grade?" (yes, clear grading policy). Valence = "Do I care about the grade?" (yes, for placement/GPA). If any link breaks — e.g., "marks are random regardless of study" — you lose motivation.

Trending Example (2024-25)

"Quiet quitting" as an Expectancy Theory failure: When employees feel effort won't lead to recognition or promotion (low instrumentality), or that rewards don't match their values (low valence), they reduce effort to the contractual minimum. Indian tech employees report this when annual increments are 5-7% but inflation is 6% — the "reward" feels meaningless.

Session 7 — Key Takeaways

  • Motivation = intensity + direction + persistence of effort
  • Maslow: 5 levels (Physiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-Actualisation)
  • Herzberg: Hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction; Motivators create satisfaction (two separate continuums)
  • McClelland: nAch (achievement), nPow (power), nAff (affiliation)
  • SDT: Autonomy + Competence + Relatedness drive intrinsic motivation
  • Goal-Setting: Specific + difficult + feedback = best performance; SMART goals
  • Equity (Adams): Compare input/outcome ratios; 3 justice types (distributive, procedural, interactional)
  • Expectancy (Vroom): M = E × I × V; if any = 0, motivation = 0
