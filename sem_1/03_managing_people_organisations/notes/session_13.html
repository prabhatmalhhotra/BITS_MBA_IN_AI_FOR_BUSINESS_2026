Organisational Culture & Change

Culture is the invisible force that shapes behaviour more than any policy. This session covers how culture forms, how it's maintained, and how organisations manage change.

Real-World Problem

When Tata acquired Jaguar Land Rover, the biggest challenge wasn't technology — it was culture clash

Tata Motors' Indian management style (hierarchical, relationship-driven) met JLR's British engineering culture (formal, process-driven). Early integration attempts failed until Tata gave JLR significant autonomy. The lesson: culture eats strategy for breakfast.

Session 13 covers organisational culture (the "personality" of an organisation) and organisational change (how to transform it without breaking it).

1 What is Organisational Culture? Beginner

Organisational Culture = a system of shared meaning held by members that distinguishes one organisation from another.

3 Organisational Change Intermediate

Forces for Change

  • Workforce demographics, Technology disruption, Economic shocks, Competition, Social trends, World politics

Resistance to Change

Individual Resistance

  • Habit, Security, Economic fear
  • Fear of unknown
  • Selective information processing

Organisational Resistance

  • Structural inertia
  • Limited focus of change
  • Group inertia
  • Threat to expertise/power

Trending Example (2024-25)

Zomato's culture transformation under Deepinder Goyal: After going public and facing profitability pressure, Zomato shifted from a "move fast and break things" startup culture to a more disciplined, metrics-driven culture. This involved Lewin's unfreeze (acknowledging the old way wasn't sustainable), change (new KPIs, restructured teams), and refreeze (reinforcing cost-consciousness as a norm).

Exam Tip: Lewin's 3-step and Kotter's 8 steps are extremely high-frequency. For Kotter, remember: it starts with urgency (not vision!) and ends with anchoring in culture. Schein's 3 levels of culture is another favourite.

Session 13 — Key Takeaways

  • Culture = shared meaning system; 7 characteristics (Robbins)
  • Schein's 3 levels: Artefacts → Espoused Values → Basic Assumptions
  • Culture created by founders, sustained by top management + socialisation
  • Employees learn via stories, rituals, symbols, language
  • Resistance to change: Individual (habit, fear) + Organisational (inertia, power)
  • Lewin: Unfreeze → Change → Refreeze
  • Kotter: 8 steps from urgency to anchoring
  • Senge: 5 disciplines of learning organisation (Systems Thinking = core)
