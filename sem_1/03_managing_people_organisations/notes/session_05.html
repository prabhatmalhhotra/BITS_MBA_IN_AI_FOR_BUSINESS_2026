Perception & Decision Making

We don't see the world as it is — we see it as we are. This session explores how perception biases and cognitive shortcuts affect every decision at work.

Real-World Problem

Two interviewers evaluate the same Flipkart candidate — and reach opposite conclusions. How?

Candidate Priya interviews at Flipkart. Interviewer A (who just rejected a terrible candidate) thinks Priya is "brilliant by comparison." Interviewer B (who just passed a stellar candidate) thinks Priya is "average." Same person, same resume, wildly different perceptions.

This is the contrast effect — one of many perceptual biases that distort how we see reality. Session 5 shows how perception and cognitive biases affect every decision at work.

1 What is Perception? Beginner

Perception is the process by which individuals organise and interpret sensory impressions to give meaning to their environment.

Key insight: What we perceive can be substantially different from objective reality. Behaviour is based on our perception of reality, not reality itself.

Factors Influencing Perception

Perceiver
Attitudes, motives, interests, experience, expectations
Target
Novelty, motion, sounds, size, background, proximity
Situation
Time, work setting, social setting
Daily Life Example

You notice a luxury car in a college parking lot (novelty of target). If you're interested in cars (perceiver interest), you look closely. If it's raining and you're rushing (situation), you barely notice. Same car, different perception based on all three factors.

2 Attribution Theory (Kelley) Intermediate

When we observe behaviour, we try to determine whether it was caused by internal (personality, ability) or external (situation, luck) factors. Kelley's model uses three criteria:

CriterionQuestionIf HIGHIf LOW
DistinctivenessDoes person behave differently in other situations?External attributionInternal attribution
ConsensusDo others behave the same way?External attributionInternal attribution
ConsistencyDoes the person always behave this way?Internal attributionExternal attribution

Common Attribution Errors

Fundamental Attribution Error

We over-attribute others' behaviour to internal causes (personality) and underestimate situational factors.

"He's late because he's lazy" (ignoring traffic jam)

Self-Serving Bias

We attribute our own success to internal factors and failure to external ones.

"I got promoted because I'm talented" vs "I didn't get promoted because the manager is biased"

4 Decision-Making Models Intermediate

Rational Model

  • Define the problem clearly
  • Identify ALL criteria and alternatives
  • Evaluate all options objectively
  • Choose the optimal solution

Assumes perfect information — rarely possible in real life

Bounded Rationality (Herbert Simon)

  • Limited information and cognitive ability
  • Simplified model of reality
  • Satisfice (satisfy + suffice) rather than optimise
  • Choose the first "good enough" option

How decisions are actually made in organisations

Intuitive Decision Making: Pattern recognition from experience — not irrational, but subconscious processing of accumulated expertise. Common among experienced managers.

Group Decision Making

Pros

  • More complete information
  • Diverse perspectives
  • Greater acceptance of decisions

Cons

  • Time-consuming
  • Conformity pressure
  • Groupthink (Janis)
  • Ambiguous responsibility

Groupthink (Irving Janis): When group desire for harmony overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives. Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, suppression of dissent, self-censorship.

Techniques to improve: Brainstorming, Nominal Group Technique (NGT), Delphi method, Devil's Advocate.

Trending Example (2024-25)

Escalation of commitment at Byju's: Despite mounting losses and declining enrolments, Byju's continued aggressive spending on acquisitions and marketing. The founder kept doubling down. This is a textbook case of escalation of commitment — refusing to cut losses because of past investment ("sunk cost fallacy").

Exam Tip: Attribution theory and decision biases are heavily tested. Practice identifying which bias is at play in given scenarios. Remember: Fundamental Attribution Error = judging others; Self-Serving Bias = judging yourself.

Session 5 — Key Takeaways

  • Perception = organising/interpreting sensory impressions; influenced by perceiver, target, situation
  • Attribution (Kelley): Distinctiveness + Consensus + Consistency determine internal vs external
  • Fundamental Attribution Error: over-attribute others to internal; Self-Serving Bias: credit self, blame others
  • Shortcuts: Selective perception, Halo/Horns, Contrast, Stereotyping
  • Rational vs Bounded Rationality (Simon): satisfice, not optimise
  • Key biases: Overconfidence, Anchoring, Confirmation, Availability, Escalation of Commitment
  • Groupthink (Janis): conformity suppresses good decisions; counter with NGT, Delphi, Devil's Advocate
