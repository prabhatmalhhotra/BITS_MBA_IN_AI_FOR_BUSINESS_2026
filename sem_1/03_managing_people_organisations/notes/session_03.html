How do employees feel about their work? What shapes satisfaction and commitment? How does diversity affect teams? This session explores the individual's inner world at work.

Real-World Problem

Zomato fires 500 employees — survivors become disengaged. Why?

In 2023, Zomato went through multiple rounds of layoffs. The employees who survived felt anxious, guilty, and disconnected. Productivity dropped even though "the best" were retained. Glassdoor reviews turned negative. Engagement survey scores tanked.

  • Why do survivors feel dissatisfied even when they still have jobs?
  • How does organisational commitment break down?
  • Can diversity of thought actually help a struggling company?
Session 3 answers these questions through the lens of attitudes, job satisfaction, and diversity — the building blocks of how individuals feel about their work.

1 Attitudes — The ABC Model Beginner

Attitudes are evaluative statements (favourable or unfavourable) about objects, people, or events. They have three components:

Affective
Feeling: "I hate my job"
+
Behavioural
Intention: "I'm going to quit"
+
Cognitive
Belief: "My pay is unfair"
=
Attitude
Overall evaluation

Mnemonic: ABC — Affective, Behavioural, Cognitive.

Cognitive Dissonance (Festinger, 1957)

When your attitudes and behaviours are inconsistent, you feel discomfort (dissonance) and try to reduce it by changing either the attitude or the behaviour.

Daily Life Example

You believe "smoking is bad" (attitude) but you smoke at parties (behaviour). The dissonance makes you uncomfortable, so you either quit smoking (change behaviour) or rationalise: "I only smoke socially, it's not that bad" (change attitude). This is cognitive dissonance.

Meyer-Allen's Three Components of Commitment

Affective Commitment

"I want to stay because I love this place"

Emotional attachment. Most desired by orgs.

Continuance Commitment

"I need to stay because leaving is costly"

Golden handcuffs, EMI obligations, vested ESOPs.

Normative Commitment: "I ought to stay because they invested in me" — moral obligation.

Daily Life Example

Think of your gym membership: Affective = "I love working out here, the trainers are great." Continuance = "I already paid for 2 years, can't waste the money." Normative = "The trainer took special interest in me, I'd feel bad leaving."

3 Job Satisfaction — Drivers & Outcomes Intermediate

What Drives Job Satisfaction?

  • Nature of work — challenging, interesting, autonomy
  • Pay — fair compensation (not necessarily highest)
  • Supervision — supportive, competent managers
  • Promotion opportunities — clear growth path
  • Co-workers — friendly, collaborative team
  • Working conditions — safe, comfortable environment

Measurement: Single global rating ("Overall, how satisfied are you?" 1-5) or Job Descriptive Index (JDI) — multi-faceted.

Outcomes of Satisfaction & Dissatisfaction

High Satisfaction leads to...Low Satisfaction leads to...

Better customer serviceHigher turnover
More OCB (helping behaviours)Counter-productive work behaviour (CWB)

Exam Tip: EVLN is a favourite framework. Map it to scenarios: an employee who starts coming late = Neglect; one who raises issues in town halls = Voice; one who silently waits for things to improve = Loyalty.

4 Diversity in Organisations Intermediate

Surface-Level Diversity

  • Age, Gender, Race, Ethnicity
  • Disability, Physical appearance
  • Easily visible, often basis for stereotyping

Deep-Level Diversity

  • Personality, Values, Attitudes
  • Cognitive styles, Beliefs
  • Takes time to discover; more important for teamwork

Why Diversity Matters

  • Better decisions — diverse teams consider more alternatives
  • Innovation — different perspectives spark creativity
  • Talent pool — access wider market of candidates
  • Market reach — diverse workforce understands diverse customers

Indian Context

  • Companies Act Section 149 — at least 1 woman director on listed company boards
  • POSH Act 2013 — mandatory Internal Complaints Committee for 10+ employees
  • Reservation policies — SC/ST/OBC representation in public sector
  • Persons with Disabilities Act 2016 — 4% reservation in government jobs
Trending Example (2024-25)

Gen Z's workplace expectations: Young Indian employees (born 1997-2012) entering the workforce demand mental health days, flexible hours, purpose-driven work, and open conversations about pay. Companies like Razorpay and Zerodha are redesigning HR policies to attract Gen Z — recognising that deep-level diversity across generations requires different management approaches.

Session 3 — Key Takeaways

  • Attitudes = ABC (Affective + Behavioural + Cognitive)
  • Cognitive dissonance (Festinger) = discomfort from inconsistency between attitude and behaviour
  • 5 major job attitudes: Satisfaction, Involvement, Commitment, POS, Engagement
  • Meyer-Allen commitment: Affective (want), Continuance (need), Normative (ought)
  • Job satisfaction drivers: work, pay, supervision, promotion, co-workers, conditions
  • EVLN: Exit, Voice, Loyalty, Neglect — responses to dissatisfaction
  • Diversity: Surface-level (visible) vs Deep-level (invisible); both matter
  • DEI: Diversity + Equity + Inclusion; Indian context includes POSH, Companies Act, PwD Act
