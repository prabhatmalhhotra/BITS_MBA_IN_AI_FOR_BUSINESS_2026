Q1 · Case Study 6 marks
Low Motivation at Axis Electronics.
Axis Electronics came up with a performance-based incentive scheme to increase sales. Sales executives were promised attractive cash bonus and fast-track promotion if they exceeded monthly targets. However, sales performance did not improve meaningfully. Many employees felt the targets were too high and beyond their reach due to limited market demand. Some believed rewards were not always awarded fairly, while others felt the incentives were not attractive enough compared to the extra effort required.
Using Vroom's Expectancy Theory, explain why the incentive scheme failed to motivate employees.
✓ Model Answer

According to Vroom's Expectancy Theory, motivation depends on Expectancy, Instrumentality, and Valence. (3 marks)

Explanation (3 marks): At Axis Electronics:

  • Expectancy was low — employees believed that extra effort would not lead to better performance due to unrealistic targets and external constraints.
  • Instrumentality was weak — employees were unsure whether good performance would actually result in rewards, owing to lack of trust in the appraisal system.
  • Valence was low — some employees did not value the incentives enough to justify the additional effort.

Since all three components were weak, overall motivation was low, leading to the failure of the incentive scheme.

Q2 · Mintzberg's Roles 10 marks
Mr. Amul Kumar — Plant Manager case.
Amul (8 yrs tenure, technically strong) chairs morning production reviews, handles attendance/safety/quality issues, resolves supervisor-worker conflicts, motivates teams. He meets vendors, auditors, clients; attends industry associations. He collects performance data, shares updates with senior management, communicates policy changes. During a machinery breakdown he coordinated maintenance, negotiated with suppliers, and decided to outsource part of production temporarily.
Using Henry Mintzberg's roles, identify the roles displayed by Mr. Amul. Justify.
✓ Model Answer (10 × 1 = 10)

The case illustrates that Mr. Amul performs multiple managerial roles as classified by Mintzberg — across Interpersonal, Informational, and Decisional categories.

🤝 Interpersonal

  • Figurehead — represents the company in meetings with vendors, auditors, clients, and industry associations
  • Leader — motivates workers, resolves conflicts, handles grievances, maintains discipline & morale
  • Liaison — interacts regularly with internal departments and external stakeholders

📡 Informational

  • Monitor — collects performance data, reviews daily reports, observes attendance/safety issues
  • Disseminator — communicates policy changes, shares updates with employees, passes info to senior management
  • Spokesperson — represents plant operations in discussions with clients, vendors, auditors

⚖️ Decisional

  • Entrepreneur — initiates improvements; outsourced production during breakdown
  • Disturbance Handler — resolves disputes; handles operational breakdowns
  • Resource Allocator — approves leave, assigns responsibilities, manages manpower
  • Negotiator — negotiates with suppliers during breakdowns; ensures cost-effective solutions
Q3 · Attribution Theory 6 marks
Meena's manager observes her sales performance has declined over the past two months. He initially thinks she is not serious about her job. Later, he finds out that market demand has dropped significantly in her region.
Using Attribution Theory, identify and explain the phenomenon while analysing the manager's initial judgment.
✓ Model Answer
Fundamental Attribution Error

(3 marks) This reflects the Fundamental Attribution Error — the tendency to underestimate the influence of external factors and overestimate the influence of internal factors when making judgements about the behavior of others. Behaviour is attributed to personal factors rather than situational ones.

Explanation (3 marks): The manager initially made an internal attribution (believed to be under personal control), assuming Meena's poor performance was due to lack of seriousness. Later evidence shows the decline was due to external factors (market demand). According to Attribution Theory, both internal and external causes must be considered before making judgments.

Q4 · Emotional Labour 6 marks
Riya is a customer service executive in a hotel. A guest speaks rudely to her and blames her for a delay that was not her fault. Although she feels upset and frustrated, she continues to smile, speaks politely, and apologizes as per company policy.
Using the concept of Emotional Labour, explain Riya's behaviour. Discuss the types of emotional labour involved and its possible impact on employees and organizations.
✓ Model Answer

The situation illustrates Emotional Labour.

  • Emotional labour = managing and regulating emotions to meet organizational expectations during service interactions.
  • Riya suppresses real feelings (frustration) and displays required emotions (politeness, calmness).
  • This represents Surface Acting — employees fake or modify outward expressions without changing internal feelings.
  • If she genuinely tried to feel empathy toward the guest, it would be Deep Acting.
  • Impact on employee: continuous emotional labour may lead to stress, burnout, and emotional exhaustion.
  • Impact on organization: benefits include maintained customer satisfaction and service quality.

Thus, emotional labour is essential in service roles but must be managed carefully to protect employee well-being.

Q5 · Personality & Leadership 7 marks
Extroversion is often considered a "good" quality for a leader to have. Why might introversion be considered an equally positive quality? Explain in YOUR OWN WORDS.
✓ Model Answer

While extroversion is often seen as desirable because extroverts tend to be sociable, talkative, and assertive, introversion also has many positive qualities that are equally important:

  1. Deep Thinking and Reflection — introverts are thoughtful and enjoy reflecting on ideas, leading to deep insights and creative problem-solving.
  2. Strong Listening Skills — they listen more attentively, making others feel heard and valued.
  3. Independent Work Ability — comfortable working alone, beneficial for tasks requiring focus and independence.
  4. Careful Decision-Making — they consider options carefully before acting, reducing impulsive mistakes.
  5. Quality Over Quantity in Relationships — they value deeper, more meaningful relationships, contributing to trusted & strong personal connections.

Conclusion: Introversion, like extroversion, brings unique strengths to individuals and groups. In many situations, the quiet strengths of introverted people are highly valuable and equally positive.

Q2 · Applied OB 5 marks
A team in a manufacturing company has recently shown a drop in productivity. The manager notices that employees feel their efforts are not recognized and they have no clarity about their growth path. Using the key concepts of OB, explain how OB tools or approaches should the manager apply in this situation?
✓ Model Answer

A drop in productivity due to lack of recognition and unclear growth paths highlights key OB concepts: motivation, perception, leadership behavior, and job satisfaction.

  1. Motivation theories — strengthen motivators such as recognition, achievement, career advancement. Implement regular appreciation, praise, celebrating small wins. Expectancy Theory can ensure a clear effort → performance → reward link.
  2. Perception & Communication — clarify growth opportunities through performance discussions, sharing career paths, providing transparent promotion criteria. Reduces ambiguity, improves confidence.
  3. Leadership behavior — adopt a supportive leadership style: offer feedback, coaching, show concern for employee needs. Builds trust, encourages engagement.
  4. Organizational culture & job design — redesign tasks with more autonomy and variety; provide skill development and cross-training opportunities.
  5. Employee development programs — training workshops, mentoring, structured learning paths reinforce motivation and performance.

By applying motivation theories, communication, supportive leadership, job design, and development systems, the manager can address concerns, improve satisfaction, and restore productivity.

"Organizational behavior cannot offer reasonably accurate explanations of human behavior or make valid predictions." Is this true or false?
✓ Model Answer
FALSE (sample answer — student answers may vary)

Explanation: Human beings are complex, and few — if any — simple and universal principles explain organizational behavior. Since human beings are not alike, the ability to make simple, accurate, and sweeping generalizations is limited. However, this does not mean one cannot offer reasonably accurate explanations of human behavior or make valid predictions. OB uses systematic study, contingency thinking, and situational analysis to do exactly that.

Q4 · Perception 5 marks
During the annual appraisal, a manager rates Kavya as "excellent" in all performance areas because she is always cheerful and respectful. However, her productivity records show average results and delayed submissions. Identify the perceptual shortcut. Explain how it affects appraisal accuracy and suggest measures to avoid such bias.
✓ Model Answer
Halo Effect
  • The manager allows one positive trait (cheerful and respectful behavior) to influence overall evaluation.
  • Other performance indicators such as productivity and timeliness are ignored.
  • This leads to biased and inaccurate performance appraisal.
  • It reduces objectivity in HR decisions.
  • Other employees may feel unfairly treated.
  • To avoid this bias: use structured appraisal systems, multiple performance criteria, and measurable indicators.
Q5 · Affective Events Theory 5 marks
Rahul works in a software company. One morning, his manager publicly appreciates him for completing a difficult project before the deadline — he feels proud and motivated, and performs enthusiastically all week helping teammates. A few days later, the same manager criticizes him harshly in front of colleagues for a minor mistake — he feels embarrassed and upset, motivation drops, he avoids taking initiative.
Using Affective Events Theory, explain Rahul's reactions. Discuss how workplace events influence emotions and job performance.
✓ Model Answer

The situation explains Affective Events Theory (AET).

  • AET states that workplace events trigger emotional reactions, which influence employee attitudes and behaviors.
  • Manager's appreciation = positive work event → positive emotions (pride, motivation).
  • These improved Rahul's job performance and organizational citizenship behavior (helping teammates).
  • Public criticism = negative work event → negative emotions (embarrassment, frustration).
  • These reduced his motivation and initiative.
  • Thus, daily workplace events shape employees' emotional responses, which directly affect job satisfaction, performance, and behavior.

Conclusion: managers should handle workplace events carefully — emotional reactions significantly influence employee outcomes.

Q6b · Perceptual Shortcut 5 marks
You are shopping for a car. The salesman first shows you an expensive model loaded with attractive, advanced features — you love it but wonder why he is showing you something way above your budget. He then leads you to a similar car that is 'on sale' — cheaper than the first but still a little over your budget. Compared to the first car, it's a great deal, and you feel you must buy it before someone else does! Identify the shortcut used. (Don't write multiple answers.)
✓ Model Answer
Contrast Effect

Judgement is made by comparing the second car to the first car just evaluated — the inflated "anchor" makes the second option seem like a great deal even though it is over budget.
