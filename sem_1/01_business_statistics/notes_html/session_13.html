Regression Fundamentals

From correlation to prediction: building a mathematical model that explains how one variable depends on another.

T1 Chapter 13 (Levine)Comprehensive Exam~55 min read
Real-World Problem

Nykaa predicts: How does advertising spend affect monthly revenue?

Nykaa's marketing team has 24 months of data: monthly ad spend (X) and revenue (Y). They want a model to predict: "If we increase ad spend by ₹10 lakh, how much additional revenue can we expect?"

Simple linear regression gives you Ŷ = b₀ + b₁X — a prediction equation that quantifies the relationship, its strength (R²), and statistical significance.

1 Types of Regression Models Beginner

TypeEquationWhen to Use
Simple LinearY = β₀ + β₁X + εOne predictor, linear relationship

PolynomialY = β₀ + β₁X + β₂X² + εCurved relationship

2 OLS Estimation Intermediate

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) finds the line that minimises Σ(Yᵢ - Ŷᵢ)² — the sum of squared residuals.

b₁ = SSXY / SSX = Σ(Xᵢ - X̄)(Yᵢ - Ȳ) / Σ(Xᵢ - X̄)²

b₀ = Ȳ - b₁ × X̄

b₁ (slope) = change in Y for each 1-unit increase in X. b₀ (intercept) = predicted Y when X = 0 (may not have practical meaning).

Daily Life Example

Study hours and marks: If b₁ = 5.2, it means every additional hour of study is associated with a 5.2-point increase in marks. If b₀ = 30, a student who studies 0 hours is predicted to score 30 (baseline from attending class). The prediction for 6 hours: Ŷ = 30 + 5.2(6) = 61.2 marks.

SST = Σ(Yᵢ - Ȳ)²    SSR = Σ(Ŷᵢ - Ȳ)²    SSE = Σ(Yᵢ - Ŷᵢ)²

R² = SSR / SST = 1 - SSE/SST

0 ≤ R² ≤ 1   (proportion of variation explained by the model)

Adjusted R²

R²(adj) = 1 - [(1 - R²)(n - 1) / (n - k - 1)]

Penalises adding predictors that do not genuinely improve the model. Always ≤ R². Use for comparing models with different numbers of predictors.

Standard Error of Estimate

S(YX) = √[SSE / (n - 2)]

4 Inference on the Slope Advanced

t = b₁ / S(b₁)     df = n - 2

H₀: β₁ = 0 (no linear relationship)

If we reject H₀, the slope is significantly different from zero — X is a useful predictor of Y.

Trending Example (2024)

CRED's user engagement model: CRED's data science team reportedly uses regression models to predict user engagement (Y) based on cashback offered (X). Their R² of approximately 0.72 means cashback explains about 72% of the variation in engagement — strong, but 28% comes from other factors like app UX, brand loyalty, and timing.

Exam Tip: For regression questions, always: (1) write the equation, (2) interpret b₁ in context, (3) report R² and what it means, (4) test if the slope is significant. Also know the difference between SST, SSR, SSE and how they relate.

Session 13 — Key Takeaways

  • Simple linear regression: Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε
  • OLS minimises Σ(residual)²; b₁ = SSXY/SSX; b₀ = Ȳ - b₁X̄
  • R² = SSR/SST = proportion of Y variation explained by X (0 to 1)
  • Adjusted R² penalises unnecessary predictors
  • t-test for slope: H₀: β₁ = 0; reject means X is a significant predictor
  • LINE assumptions: Linearity, Independence, Normality, Equal variance
  • Session 14 covers diagnostic checks for these assumptions
