Basic Probability

The language of uncertainty. Every statistical inference, from confidence intervals to hypothesis tests, is built on probability theory.

Real-World Problem

PhonePe's fraud detection: Is this transaction suspicious?

PhonePe processes over 500 crore transactions per month. Their fraud detection system must decide in milliseconds: "Given that this transaction is from a new device, at an unusual hour, for a large amount — what is the probability it is fraudulent?"

  • P(Fraud) overall is very low (~0.01%) — this is the prior probability
  • P(New device | Fraud) is high (~80%) — this is the likelihood
  • P(New device | Not fraud) is moderate (~5%) — most legitimate users stick to their phone
  • Using Bayes' theorem, PhonePe combines these to compute P(Fraud | New device) — the posterior
This session teaches you the probability toolkit — from basic axioms to Bayes' theorem — that powers decisions under uncertainty.

1 Approaches to Probability Beginner

ApproachDefinitionExample
Classical (A Priori)P(A) = favourable outcomes / total equally likely outcomesP(Head) = 1/2; P(rolling 6) = 1/6
Empirical (Relative Frequency)P(A) = number of times A occurred / total trialsOut of 10,000 loans, 300 defaulted: P(default) = 3%
SubjectiveDegree of belief based on experience"I think there is a 70% chance RBI cuts rates next quarter"

Fundamental Axioms

1.   0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1   for any event A
2.   P(S) = 1   where S is the sample space (all possible outcomes)
3.   For mutually exclusive A, B:   P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)
Daily Life Example

Classical: Drawing a card from a deck — P(Ace) = 4/52 = 1/13. Empirical: You notice 3 out of your last 10 Swiggy orders were late — P(late) ≈ 30%. Subjective: "I feel there is a 60% chance it will rain today" based on looking at the clouds.

2 Probability Rules Intermediate

Complement Rule

P(A') = 1 - P(A)

The probability that A does NOT happen. Often easier to compute the complement.

Addition Rule (OR)

General:   P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

If Mutually Exclusive:   P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)   [since P(A ∩ B) = 0]

Multiplication Rule (AND)

General:   P(A ∩ B) = P(A|B) × P(B)

If Independent:   P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

Mutually Exclusive

  • Events cannot happen together
  • P(A ∩ B) = 0
  • Example: Rolling a 3 AND a 5 on one die
  • Mutually exclusive events are NEVER independent (unless one has P=0)

Independent

  • One event does not affect the other
  • P(A|B) = P(A)
  • Example: Flipping two coins
  • P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
Daily Life Example

Addition rule: What is the probability your Swiggy order is from a Chinese OR South Indian restaurant? If P(Chinese) = 30% and P(South Indian) = 25% and some restaurants serve both (P(both) = 5%), then P(Chinese or South Indian) = 30% + 25% - 5% = 50%.

Exam Tip: The most common mistake is forgetting to subtract P(A ∩ B) in the addition rule. If the question says "mutually exclusive," then P(A ∩ B) = 0 and you just add. If it does not say that, you MUST subtract the overlap.

3 Conditional Probability Intermediate

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)     [given B has occurred]

Conditional probability is the probability of A happening given that B has already happened. It "shrinks" the sample space to only those outcomes where B is true.

Independence Test

Events A and B are independent if and only if:

P(A | B) = P(A)    OR equivalently    P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
Daily Life Example

You are stuck in Bangalore traffic (event B). What is the probability you will be late for your meeting (event A)? P(Late | Traffic) is much higher than P(Late) in general. The condition "traffic" changes the probability dramatically — that is conditional probability.

4 Bayes' Theorem Advanced

P(Aᵢ | B) = P(B | Aᵢ) × P(Aᵢ) / Σ P(B | Aⱼ) × P(Aⱼ)

In words: Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence

Prior P(A)
What you believed before
×
Likelihood P(B|A)
How likely is evidence if A true
→
Posterior P(A|B)
Updated belief after evidence

Worked Example: Medical Testing

A disease affects 1% of the population. A test has:

  • Sensitivity = P(Positive | Disease) = 99%
  • Specificity = P(Negative | No Disease) = 95%

If you test positive, what is the probability you actually have the disease?

StepCalculationValue
P(Disease)Prior0.01
P(No Disease)1 - 0.010.99
P(+ | Disease)Sensitivity0.99
P(+ | No Disease)1 - Specificity0.05
P(+)0.99 × 0.01 + 0.05 × 0.990.0594
P(Disease | +)(0.99 × 0.01) / 0.059416.7%

Despite a 99% accurate test, a positive result only means a 16.7% chance of disease! This counter-intuitive result occurs because the disease is rare (low prior).

Trending Example (2023-2024)

COVID rapid antigen tests were a real-world Bayes' theorem lesson for millions of Indians. When prevalence dropped to very low levels (say 0.5%), even a test with 90% sensitivity and 95% specificity gave a PPV (Positive Predictive Value) of only ~8%. ICMR guidelines recommended RT-PCR confirmation precisely because of this Bayesian math.

Exam Tip: Bayes' theorem is almost guaranteed in mid-sem. Practice the 2x2 table approach — it is much less error-prone than memorising the formula. Fill in the joint probabilities first, then compute the conditional. Common scenarios: medical testing, quality control (defective from Machine A vs B), and spam detection.

5 Counting Rules Intermediate

Permutations (Order Matters)

P(n, r) = n! / (n - r)!

Example: How many ways can 3 winners (Gold, Silver, Bronze) be chosen from 10 athletes? P(10, 3) = 10 × 9 × 8 = 720.

Combinations (Order Does Not Matter)

C(n, r) = n! / [r! × (n - r)!]

Example: How many ways can a committee of 3 be chosen from 10 people? C(10, 3) = 120.

Permutation

  • Order matters
  • Arranging, ranking, sequencing
  • "In how many orders..."
  • P(n,r) ≥ C(n,r) always

Combination

  • Order does NOT matter
  • Selecting, choosing, picking
  • "In how many ways to choose..."
  • Foundation for binomial (Session 5)
Daily Life Example

Permutation: Your phone has a 4-digit PIN from digits 0-9. Number of possible PINs = P(10,4) = 5,040 (if no digit repeats). With repetition allowed: 10&sup4; = 10,000. Combination: Choosing 5 toppings from 12 available at Domino's = C(12,5) = 792 combinations.

Session 4 — Key Takeaways

  • Three approaches: Classical (equally likely), Empirical (relative frequency), Subjective (belief)
  • Complement: P(A') = 1 - P(A)
  • Addition rule: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B); subtract overlap!
  • Multiplication rule: P(A ∩ B) = P(A|B) × P(B); for independent events = P(A) × P(B)
  • Conditional probability: P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)
  • Independence: P(A|B) = P(A) — knowing B does not change A's probability
  • Bayes' theorem: Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence — key for updating beliefs
  • Counter-intuitive Bayes: even accurate tests have low PPV when the condition is rare
  • Permutations = order matters; Combinations = order does not matter
