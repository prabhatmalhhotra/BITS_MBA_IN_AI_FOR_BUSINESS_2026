The Normal Distribution

The bell curve that governs everything from exam scores to stock returns. Master the Z-score and you unlock the entire world of inferential statistics.

Real-World Problem

Tata Motors QC: Will this batch of engine parts meet specifications?

Tata Motors manufactures crankshaft pins with a target diameter of 25.00 mm. The machining process produces parts with μ = 25.00 mm and σ = 0.05 mm, following a normal distribution. Engineering tolerance is 25.00 ± 0.10 mm.

  • What percentage of parts will be out of tolerance? (Z-score problem)
  • What diameter should they set as the rejection threshold? (Inverse normal)
  • If they tighten tolerance to ±0.08 mm, how does the defect rate change?
The normal distribution and Z-scores are the backbone of quality control, risk management, and every confidence interval or hypothesis test you will encounter.

1 Continuous Probability Distributions Beginner

Unlike discrete distributions (Session 5), continuous distributions deal with variables that can take any value in a range (weight, time, temperature).

Key Concept: Probability = Area Under the Curve

For continuous distributions:
P(X = any single point) = 0
P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = area under f(x) between a and b

This is a fundamental shift from discrete thinking. The probability of weighing exactly 70.000...kg is zero. We can only talk about ranges: P(69.5 ≤ X ≤ 70.5).

Daily Life Example

Your morning commute time is continuous — it could be 28.3 min, 28.31 min, etc. You can ask "What is the probability my commute takes between 25 and 35 minutes?" but not "What is the probability it takes exactly 28.347 minutes?" (that probability is zero).

2 The Normal Distribution Intermediate

The PDF (Probability Density Function)

f(x) = (1 / σ√(2π)) × e^(-(x - μ)² / (2σ²))

You do NOT need to compute this by hand — use Z-tables or software. But understand what it means:

Properties of the Normal Curve

  • Bell-shaped and symmetric about μ
  • Mean = Median = Mode = μ
  • Completely defined by two parameters: μ (centre) and σ (spread)
  • Total area under the curve = 1
  • Tails approach but never touch the x-axis (extends to ±∞)

The 68-95-99.7 Rule

68.27% of data

μ ± 2σ

μ ± 3σ

3 The Standard Normal (Z) Distribution Intermediate

Standardisation Formula

Z = (X - μ) / σ

Z ~ N(0, 1)   [mean = 0, std dev = 1]

Every normal distribution can be converted to the standard normal using Z-scores. This lets us use one universal table for all normal problems.

How to Solve Normal Distribution Problems

Step 1: Draw the curve
Sketch the bell curve, mark μ, shade the area you want
▼
Step 2: Standardise
Convert X to Z using Z = (X - μ) / σ
▼
Step 3: Look up Z-table
Table gives P(Z < z) = area to the LEFT
▼
Step 4: Adjust for the question
P(Z > z) = 1 - P(Z < z); P(a < Z < b) = P(Z < b) - P(Z < a)

Inverse Normal (Finding X given probability)

X = μ + Z × σ

Example: What is the 95th percentile of daily orders? Z = 1.645 (from table). X = 1200 + 1.645 × 150 = 1446.75 orders.

Exam Tip: Z-table problems are the bread and butter of the mid-sem. Practice these three types: (1) P(X < a), (2) P(X > a), (3) P(a < X < b), and (4) finding X given a probability (inverse). Always draw the curve and shade the area first — it prevents sign errors.

4 Other Continuous Distributions Advanced

Uniform Distribution

f(x) = 1 / (b - a)   for a ≤ x ≤ b

Mean = (a + b) / 2    Variance = (b - a)² / 12

Every value in the range is equally likely. Example: arrival time of the next metro (if it comes every 10 min, your wait is Uniform[0, 10]).

Exponential Distribution

f(x) = λe^(-λx)   for x ≥ 0

Mean = 1/λ    Variance = 1/λ²

Models time between Poisson events. If calls arrive at λ = 5/hour (Poisson), then time between calls is Exponential with mean = 1/5 hour = 12 minutes. It is memoryless: P(wait > 10 more min | already waited 5 min) = P(wait > 10 min).

Trending Example (2024)

Nifty 50 daily returns are approximately normally distributed with μ ≈ 0.05% and σ ≈ 1.1% (based on 2020-2024 data). Risk managers at SEBI and mutual funds use this assumption to calculate Value at Risk (VaR): the 99% VaR is μ - 2.326σ = 0.05% - 2.56% = -2.51%, meaning there is only a 1% chance of losing more than 2.51% in a single day.

Daily Life Example

Waiting for an auto-rickshaw: If autos pass by at an average rate of 4 per 10 minutes (λ = 0.4/min), the time until the next auto follows an Exponential distribution with mean = 1/0.4 = 2.5 minutes. The probability of waiting more than 5 minutes: P(X > 5) = e^(-0.4 × 5) = e^(-2) ≈ 13.5%.

Session 6 — Key Takeaways

  • Continuous: probability = area under curve; P(X = point) = 0
  • Normal N(μ, σ²): bell-shaped, symmetric, defined by μ and σ
  • 68-95-99.7 rule: percentage within 1, 2, 3 standard deviations
  • Z = (X - μ)/σ converts any Normal to Standard Normal N(0,1)
  • Z-table gives P(Z < z); for P(Z > z) use complement; for between use subtraction
  • Inverse: X = μ + Zσ to find value at a given percentile
  • Key Z values: 1.645 (90%), 1.96 (95%), 2.576 (99%)
  • Uniform: f(x) = 1/(b-a); Exponential: time between Poisson events, memoryless
