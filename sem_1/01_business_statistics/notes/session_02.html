Organising and Analysing Data

Raw data is meaningless noise. This session teaches you how to organise, tabulate, and visualise data so patterns leap off the page.

Real-World Problem

Flipkart's Big Billion Days: Which product categories drove the most sales?

Imagine you have a raw CSV with 50 lakh transaction rows from Flipkart's Big Billion Days sale. Your boss asks: "Show me which categories sold the most, which price ranges were popular, and whether there's a pattern over the 5-day sale window."

  • Product category (Electronics, Fashion, Home) is categorical — needs bar/pie charts.
  • Transaction value (₹299, ₹15,999) is numerical — needs histograms and frequency distributions.
  • Sales over 5 days is time-series — needs a line/area chart.
  • Price vs discount % is two numerical variables — needs a scatter plot.
Session 2 gives you the full toolkit to transform raw rows into visual stories that drive decisions — tables, charts, and cross-tabulations for both categorical and numerical data.

Daily Life Example

Think of your family's monthly grocery receipt. You mentally group items into categories: Vegetables, Dairy, Snacks, Cleaning. 2 Visualising Categorical Variables Beginner

Three main chart types for categorical data:

Bar Chart

  • Bars represent categories
  • Height = frequency or percentage
  • Bars are separated (gaps between them)
  • Best for comparing categories

Pie Chart

  • Slices of a circle = proportions
  • Must sum to 100%
  • Best for showing "part of whole"
  • Avoid if more than 5-6 categories

Pareto Chart — The "Vital Few" Tool

A Pareto Chart is a bar chart sorted in descending order with a cumulative percentage line overlaid. Named after the 80/20 principle: often ~80% of effects come from ~20% of causes.

Daily Life Example

If you track where your monthly salary goes, a Pareto chart might show: Rent (35%) + EMI (25%) + Groceries (15%) = 75% of your spending. These three "vital few" categories dominate your budget. The remaining 25% is spread across 10+ minor categories.

Side-by-Side and Stacked Bar Charts

For comparing two categorical variables visually. Side-by-side: bars for each sub-group placed next to each other (e.g., payment method by city). Stacked: bars subdivided into segments showing the composition of each category.

Exam Tip: BITS exams love asking "which chart is most appropriate for this scenario?" Remember: Bar chart for comparing categories, Pie chart for part-of-whole (max 5-6 slices), Pareto for identifying dominant categories.

3 Organising Numerical Variables Intermediate

Unlike categorical data where categories are predefined, numerical data needs to be grouped into classes (bins) before you can see the distribution.

Building a Frequency Distribution

Step 1: Decide Number of Classes
Sturges' Rule: classes = 1 + 3.322 x log10(n)

Frequency Distribution Table
Add relative frequency (%) and cumulative frequency
Sturges' Rule: k = 1 + 3.322 x log10(n)
Class width = (Max - Min) / k   (round up to a convenient number)

Example: Delivery Times (minutes)

Suppose n = 200 delivery times, Min = 12 min, Max = 58 min.

Sturges: k = 1 + 3.322 x log10(200) = 1 + 3.322 x 2.301 = 8.65 ≈ 9 classes

Width = (58 - 12) / 9 = 5.1 ≈ 5 minutes per class

Daily Life Example

When you check your screen time app, it shows you a bar chart of daily usage grouped into 1-hour bins (0-1 hr, 1-2 hr, etc.). That is a frequency distribution of your daily phone usage. The app is building a histogram for you behind the scenes.

Histogram

  • For numerical data
  • Bars TOUCH each other
  • X-axis = continuous scale
  • Area of bars = proportion

Bar Chart

  • For categorical data
  • Bars have GAPS
  • X-axis = category labels
  • Height of bars = frequency
Exam Tip: A classic trap question: "Is a histogram the same as a bar chart?" Answer: No. Histogram = numerical data, bars touch, x-axis is continuous. Bar chart = categorical data, bars separated, x-axis is labels. Getting this wrong costs easy marks.

5 Visualising Two Numerical Variables Intermediate

Scatter Plot

A Scatter Plot shows the relationship between two numerical variables. Each dot is one observation plotted by its (X, Y) values.

Time-Series Plot

When one variable is time, connect dots with a line. Used to spot trends, seasonality, and anomalies.

Trending Example (2024)

Zomato's quarterly earnings presentations use time-series plots extensively — showing monthly active users, average order value, and adjusted EBITDA over quarters. Their investor deck showed Gross Order Value crossing ₹8,500 crore, visualised as a time-series plot with a clear upward trend since the pandemic.

Daily Life Example

Scatter plot in real life: Plot your study hours (X) vs your exam marks (Y) for the last 10 tests. If the dots trend upward, studying more is helping. If it is a random cloud, maybe your study method needs improvement, not just the hours.

6 Data Visualisation Best Practices Advanced

Bad charts do not just look ugly — they mislead. Here are the most common mistakes:

MistakeWhy It Is BadFix
Non-zero baseline on bar chartMakes small differences look hugeAlways start Y-axis at 0 for bar charts
3D effectsDistorts perception of bar height and pie slice sizeUse flat 2D charts
Too many pie slicesSlices below 5% become unreadableGroup small categories into "Other"
Missing axis labelsReader has no idea what numbers representLabel both axes with variable name and units
"Chartjunk"Excessive gridlines, colours, icons distract from dataKeep it minimal — data-ink ratio should be high
Dual Y-axesCan be manipulated to show false correlationsUse with caution; prefer separate panels
Trending Example (2025)

Indian Union Budget infographics: The "Rupee comes from / Rupee goes to" pie charts are a textbook example of effective categorical visualisation — simple, clearly labelled, limited categories (8-10), with percentages adding to 100. They have used this format for decades because it works.

Session 2 — Key Takeaways

  • Categorical data: organise with summary tables and cross-tabulations; visualise with bar, pie, and Pareto charts
  • Numerical data: build frequency distributions using Sturges' rule (k = 1 + 3.322 log n); visualise with histograms, polygons, ogives
  • Histogram vs bar chart: histogram = numerical (bars touch), bar chart = categorical (bars separated)
  • Pareto chart = descending bar + cumulative line — identifies the "vital few" causes
  • Scatter plot shows relationship between two numerical variables
  • Time-series plot tracks a numerical variable over time
  • Avoid: 3D effects, non-zero baselines, chartjunk, dual axes, too many pie slices
  • Contingency tables (cross-tabs) are the foundation for chi-square tests later (Session 11)
