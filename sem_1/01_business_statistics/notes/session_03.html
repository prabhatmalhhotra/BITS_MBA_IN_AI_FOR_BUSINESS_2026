Numerical Descriptive Measures

Numbers that summarise an entire dataset in a few values — the mean, spread, shape, and relationships that power every business decision.

Real-World Problem

Zerodha wants to know: "What does a typical investor's portfolio look like?"

Zerodha has 1.2 crore active investors. The CEO asks: "What's the average portfolio value? How much do portfolios vary? Are most investors concentrated at small values or are they spread out?"

  • Average portfolio = Mean (but a few billionaire accounts will pull it up)
  • Typical portfolio = Median (immune to extreme outliers)
  • How much variation = Standard deviation, IQR
  • Are most people small investors? = Skewness (right-skewed means yes)
  • Does portfolio size predict trading frequency? = Correlation
This session gives you every tool to describe a dataset numerically — from averages to spread to shape to relationships.

1 Measures of Central Tendency Beginner

Central tendency answers: "What is a typical value in this dataset?"

Mean (Arithmetic Average)

Sample Mean: X̄ = (X₁ + X₂ + ... + Xₙ) / n = ΣXᵢ / n

The mean uses every value in the dataset, which makes it sensitive to outliers.

Median

The Median is the middle value when data is sorted. If n is even, take the average of the two middle values. It is robust to outliers.

Mode

The most frequently occurring value. A dataset can be unimodal, bimodal, or multimodal. Useful for categorical data too.

Geometric Mean

Geometric Mean = (X₁ × X₂ × ... × Xₙ)^(1/n)

Used for growth rates and compound returns. Always less than or equal to the arithmetic mean.

When to Use Mean

  • Data is symmetric (no extreme outliers)
  • You need to do further calculations
  • Interval or ratio data

When to Use Median

  • Data is skewed (incomes, house prices)
  • Outliers present
  • Ordinal data is acceptable
Daily Life Example

Mean vs Median salary: In a startup with 9 employees earning ₹5-8 lakh and one founder earning ₹2 crore, the mean salary is ~₹25 lakh (misleadingly high). The median is ~₹6.5 lakh (much more "typical"). News headlines often use mean when they should use median — watch out!

2 Measures of Variation Intermediate

Central tendency alone is not enough. Two datasets can have the same mean but wildly different spreads.

Range

Range = Maximum - Minimum

Simple but uses only two values — vulnerable to a single outlier.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

IQR = Q₃ - Q₁

Range of the middle 50% of data. Robust to outliers. Used in boxplots.

Variance and Standard Deviation

Sample Variance: S² = Σ(Xᵢ - X̄)² / (n - 1)

Sample Std Dev: S = √S²

Population Variance: σ² = Σ(Xᵢ - μ)² / N

Why n-1? Using n-1 (called Bessel's correction) makes the sample variance an unbiased estimator of the population variance. With just n, we would consistently underestimate the true spread.

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

CV = (S / X̄) × 100%

Relative measure of variation. Use CV when comparing variability between datasets with different units or different means.

Daily Life Example

CV in cricket: Batsman A averages 45 with SD 10 (CV = 22%). Batsman B averages 30 with SD 10 (CV = 33%). Same absolute variability, but B is relatively more inconsistent. That is why selectors prefer A — lower CV means more reliable performance.

Trending Example (2024)

Mutual fund factsheets from AMFI always report standard deviation alongside returns. A fund returning 15% with SD of 8% is less risky than one returning 16% with SD of 20%. Zerodha Coin and Groww apps now prominently display "risk" (standard deviation) to help investors compare funds beyond just returns.

Exam Tip: Variance formulas are the most commonly tested in mid-sem. Remember: sample uses n-1, population uses N. Also remember the CV formula — it appears in comparison questions ("which dataset is more variable?").

3 Shape: Skewness and Kurtosis Intermediate

Skewness

Skewness = E[(X - μ)³] / σ³

Right-Skewed (Positive)

  • Skewness > 0
  • Long tail to the right
  • Mean > Median > Mode
  • Example: Income, house prices

Left-Skewed (Negative)

  • Skewness < 0
  • Long tail to the left
  • Mean < Median < Mode
  • Example: Age at retirement, easy exam scores

Kurtosis

Kurtosis = E[(X - μ)⁴] / σ⁴

Chebyshev's Rule (Any Distribution)

At least (1 - 1/k²) × 100% of data falls within k standard deviations of the mean

k=2: at least 75%  |  k=3: at least 88.9%
Daily Life Example

Skewness at a salary negotiation: When HR says "average salary is ₹12 lakh," ask for the median too. If the distribution is right-skewed (common for salaries), the median might be only ₹8 lakh. The mean is pulled up by a few senior leaders earning ₹50+ lakh.

Boxplot

Visual representation of the five-number summary. The box spans Q1 to Q3, with a line at the median. Whiskers extend to 1.5 × IQR from the box edges. Points beyond the whiskers are outliers.

Z-Score (Standard Score)

z = (X - X̄) / S

Tells you how many standard deviations a value is from the mean. A z-score beyond ±3 is typically considered an outlier.

Exam Tip: Boxplot interpretation is commonly tested. Know how to identify: median position, IQR, outlier thresholds (Q1 - 1.5×IQR and Q3 + 1.5×IQR), and skewness from the boxplot shape.

5 Covariance and Correlation Advanced

Covariance

Cov(X, Y) = Σ(Xᵢ - X̄)(Yᵢ - Ȳ) / (n - 1)

Positive covariance = X and Y tend to move together. Negative = opposite directions. Problem: covariance depends on the scale of variables, making it hard to compare.

Pearson Correlation Coefficient (r)

r = Cov(X, Y) / (Sₓ × Sᵧ)

Range: -1 ≤ r ≤ +1
r valueInterpretationExample
r = +1Perfect positive linearCircumference vs diameter
r = +0.8Strong positiveStudy hours vs marks
r = 0No linear relationshipShoe size vs IQ
r = -0.7Strong negativePrice vs demand (for normal goods)
r = -1Perfect negative linearRarely seen in real data

Critical warning: Correlation measures linear association only. A perfect U-shaped relationship would give r near 0. Also, correlation does not imply causation.

Trending Example (2024)

Swiggy IPO prospectus (November 2024): In investor filings, Swiggy reported correlations between advertising spend and new user acquisitions. While the correlation was strong (~0.75), analysts noted this does not prove ads cause sign-ups — both could be driven by seasonal demand (festive periods see higher ad budgets AND more users independently).

Daily Life Example

Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated. Does ice cream cause drowning? Of course not. The hidden variable (confounder) is summer heat: more people swim, more people buy ice cream. This is the classic example of why correlation does not equal causation.

Session 3 — Key Takeaways

  • Central tendency: Mean (sensitive to outliers), Median (robust), Mode (most frequent), Geometric Mean (for growth rates)
  • Variation: Range, IQR, Variance (S², divide by n-1), Standard Deviation (S), CV (for comparing across units)
  • Shape: Skewness (positive = right tail, mean > median), Kurtosis (>3 = fat tails)
  • Empirical rule: 68/95/99.7% within 1/2/3 σ (normal data only)
  • Chebyshev: At least (1 - 1/k²) within kσ (any distribution)
  • Five-number summary: Min, Q1, Median, Q3, Max — visualised as a boxplot
  • z-score = (X - mean)/SD; |z| > 3 flags outliers
  • Covariance measures co-movement; Pearson r standardises it to [-1, +1]
  • Correlation is not causation — always check for confounders
