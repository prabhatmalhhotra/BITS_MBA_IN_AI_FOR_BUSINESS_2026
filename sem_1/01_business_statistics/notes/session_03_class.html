3 Central Tendency: Mean Beginner

Mean (Average) = Sum of all values / Number of observations

Mean = (1/N) * Σ xᵢ = Sum of values / N

Professor's Worked Example

Dataset: 8, 9, 1, 4, 3

Mean = (8 + 9 + 1 + 4 + 3) / 5 = 25 / 5 = 5

Applied to Class Dataset (50 values)

MeasureValueMS Excel Function
Mean295.5=AVERAGE(Range)
Median250=MEDIAN(Range)
Mode175=MODE(Range)

Note: Mean (295.5) is pulled up by higher values like 475, while Mode (175) is the most frequently occurring value. The three measures tell different stories about the "centre" of this data.

When to Use Median Over Mean?

Professor gave four specific cases:

Case 1: Skewed Data with Outliers

Dataset: 2, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 11, 18, 1500
Mean = 1566/9 = 174 — distorted by the outlier 1500.
Median = 8 — a much better measure of the centre.

Professor noted: "Distribution of wealth, income, salary etc. are generally skewed — few individuals have enormous wealth. Hence median income and median salary are preferred over mean income and mean salary."

Case 2: When Mean Cannot Be Computed

Olympic Race Example: Professor showed the Men's 5000m results from the 2016 Summer Olympics. Some athletes had DNF (Did Not Finish). You cannot compute a mean finishing time when some values are missing/undefined, but you can still find the median of those who finished.

Case 3: Median is used in image processing to remove noise (median filter), and in voting theory (median voter theorem).

Case 4: Median is used in the 5-number summary and Box Plot to spot whether the distribution is symmetric.

Light Bulb Life Rating Example

Professor showed three types of bulbs: Incandescent (40W), CFL (7W), LED (7W) with different brightness (450 lumens each) and different lifespans (1,000 hrs / 15,000 hrs / 50,000 hrs).
The life rating of the bulbs is the Median value — not the mean, because individual bulb lifespans can vary wildly.

5 Central Tendency: Mode Beginner

Mode = The most frequently occurring value in the data.

Professor's Examples

DatasetModeType
1, 2, 2, 3, 4, 52Unique Mode (Unimodal)
1, 2, 2, 3, 4, 5, 5, 62 and 5Two Modes (Bimodal)
1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6No ModeAll values appear equally

When to Use Mode?

  • Voting: The candidate who secures the largest number of votes is declared the winner (mode of votes)
  • KBC (Kaun Banega Crorepati): Audience poll — the option with the most votes is the mode
  • Random Forest (Machine Learning): Uses Mode (majority voting) across multiple decision trees
  • SpellCheck / Recommendation Systems: The top suggested option is the mode (highest probability)
KBC Audience Poll = Mode

Professor showed a KBC screenshot where audience votes were: A = 26%, B = 44%, C = 13%, D = 17%. The mode answer is B with the highest percentage. This is how the "audience lifeline" works — it is simply picking the Mode of the audience responses.

Session 3 Class Notes — Key Takeaways

  • Chapter 3 covers 6 topics: Central Tendency, Variation, Shape, Quartiles, Covariance/Correlation, Nano case study
  • Summary measures are like vital signs for a dataset — they tell the health of the data at a glance
  • Mean = Sum/N. Sensitive to outliers. Excel: =AVERAGE(Range)
  • Median = Middle value after sorting. Robust to outliers. Preferred for income/salary/wealth data. Excel: =MEDIAN(Range)
  • Mode = Most frequent value. Used in voting, KBC audience poll, Random Forest ML, spell-check
  • Mean may not exist in the dataset; Median and Mode always come from actual data values
  • Professor's tip: Always draw Mean, Median, Mode on your histogram to visualise skewness

  • Variation, Shape, Quartiles deferred to next class
