Defining and Collecting Data

Before you can analyse anything, you need to understand what you're measuring and how to collect it without bias. This session builds that foundation.

Real-World Problem

Zomato wants to know: "Are customers happy with delivery time in Bangalore?"

Imagine you're a data analyst at Zomato. Your manager says: "Find out if our Bangalore customers are satisfied with delivery speed." Sounds simple? But think about it —

  • Who do you ask? All 5 lakh Bangalore users? Or just some?
  • What exactly is "satisfied"? Rating above 4? Delivery under 30 min?
  • How do you pick people to survey without bias?
  • What if only angry customers respond? Is your data still reliable?
This is exactly what Session 1 teaches you — how to define what you're measuring, pick the right sample, and avoid mistakes that ruin your analysis before it even starts.

1 What is Statistics? Beginner

At its core, statistics is the science of learning from data. It has two branches:

Descriptive Statistics

Summarise what you already have.

  • Average delivery time = 28 min
  • 60% rated 4+ stars
  • Bar chart of ratings

Think: "Here's what the data looks like"

Inferential Statistics

Generalise from a sample to a larger population.

  • Sample 500 users → conclude about 5 lakh
  • "We're 95% confident that satisfaction is between 58-62%"

Think: "Here's what we can conclude"

Daily Life Example

Descriptive: You check your phone's screen time — "I used my phone 4.5 hours today." That's describing what happened.

Inferential: Based on last week's average (4.2 hrs/day), you predict you'll use ~30 hrs next week. That's inferring a future pattern from a sample of past data.

2 Variables — What Are You Measuring? Beginner

A Variable is any characteristic that can change from one observation to another. The first step in any data project is to clearly define your variables.

The Variable Family Tree

Every variable falls into one of two families:

  • VARIABLE Any characteristic that varies across observations
    • CATEGORICAL Labels / categories (no math operations)
      • Nominal No natural order — e.g. Gender, City, Payment method (UPI / Card / Cash)
      • Ordinal Has order but gaps aren't equal — e.g. Rating (1-5 stars), Education level, T-shirt size (S/M/L)
    • NUMERICAL Numbers you can do math with
      • Discrete Countable, whole numbers — e.g. No. of orders, No. of children, Defects in a batch
      • Continuous Any value in a range — e.g. Delivery time (28.5 min), Weight (72.3 kg), Temperature
Daily Life Example

Think about a Swiggy order:
• Restaurant name = Categorical (Nominal)
• Your rating (1-5 stars) = Categorical (Ordinal)
• Number of items = Numerical (Discrete)
• Bill amount (₹347.50) = Numerical (Continuous)

The 4 Measurement Scales (NOIR)

Each scale builds on the previous one — like levels in a game. Higher scale = more math you can do.

ScalePropertiesExampleMath you can do
Nominal Names / Labels only Blood group (A, B, AB, O) Count, Mode
Ordinal Names + Order Army rank (Lt, Capt, Major) + Median, Percentiles
Interval Names + Order + Equal gaps Temperature in °C (0°C ≠ "no heat") + Mean, Std Dev
Ratio Names + Order + Equal gaps + True zero Income (₹0 = no income), Weight, Height + Ratios ("twice as heavy")
Exam Tip: Remember NOIR (Nominal, Ordinal, Interval, Ratio) — like the film genre. The most common exam question asks you to classify variables from a scenario. Practice identifying variable type for every real-world example you see.

3 Population vs Sample Beginner

Population (N)

The entire group you want to study.

  • All 5 lakh Zomato Bangalore users
  • All cars manufactured by Tata in 2025
  • All transactions on PhonePe today

Measurement on population = Parameter

Sample (n)

A subset chosen from the population.

  • 500 randomly selected users
  • 100 cars from the production line
  • 1000 transactions picked at random

Measurement on sample = Statistic

Daily Life Example

Your mom makes a pot of dal. She stirs it well and tastes one spoon (sample). If it's good, she assumes the whole pot (population) is good. That one spoon is a "random sample" — and stirring first ensures it represents the whole pot (no bias).

Operational Definition

An Operational Definition is a precise, measurable rule for your variable. Without it, different people will measure differently, and your data becomes meaningless.

Vague DefinitionOperational Definition
"Customer is satisfied"Customer gave rating ≥ 4 out of 5 on post-delivery survey
"Fast delivery"Order delivered within 30 minutes of placement
"Active user"User who placed at least 1 order in the last 30 days

4 Sampling Methods Intermediate

How you pick your sample determines whether your conclusions are trustworthy. There are two families:

Probability Sampling (Preferred — every unit has a known chance of selection)

MethodHow it worksReal ExampleWhen to use
Simple Random (SRS) Every unit has equal chance. Use lottery / random number generator. Pick 500 Zomato users using RAND() in Excel When population is homogeneous & you have a complete list
Systematic Pick every k-th unit. k = N/n QC inspector checks every 50th bottle on a Pepsi production line When units are in a sequence and no hidden pattern exists
Stratified Divide into groups (strata) by a key trait, then SRS within each Survey employees: 30% from Engineering, 30% from Sales, 40% from Ops (proportional to headcount) When population has distinct subgroups you want represented
Cluster Divide into clusters (usually geographic), randomly pick some clusters, survey everyone in those Pick 10 random pincodes in Bangalore, survey all Zomato users in those pincodes When a full list isn't available but clusters are easy to identify

Trending Example (2025)

India's National Family Health Survey (NFHS-6) uses two-stage stratified cluster sampling: Stage 1 — randomly select villages (rural) / census blocks (urban) as clusters. Stage 2 — randomly pick households within those clusters. This lets them survey ~6 lakh households and estimate health indicators for 1.4 billion people.

Daily Life Example

After every Uber ride, you get a pop-up asking for a star rating — that's an online survey (census attempt). But only ~30% of riders actually rate. The other 70% who skip might be the ones who had an average or bad ride. This is non-response bias in action, making Uber's average ratings look artificially high.

6 Types of Errors in Data Collection Advanced

Even a perfectly designed study can go wrong. There are four types of errors to watch for:

Coverage Error
Your list (frame) misses part of the population
Non-Response Error
Selected people refuse or don't reply
Sampling Error
Random variation — different samples give different results
Measurement Error
Bad questions, leading wording, interviewer bias
Error TypeReal ExampleHow to Reduce
Coverage Phone survey excludes people without phones (rural India) Use multiple frames; compare frame to census data
Non-Response Salary survey — high earners less likely to respond Follow-up reminders; incentives; short surveys
Sampling Poll of 100 people vs 10,000 — the smaller one has more sampling error Increase sample size; use efficient sampling design
Measurement "Don't you agree our service is excellent?" (leading question) Neutral wording; pre-test questionnaire; train interviewers
Trending Example (2024-25)

US Election Polls 2024 faced massive non-response bias. Pollsters used online panels where younger, urban, college-educated people were overrepresented. They tried "weighting" adjustments, but the underlying sample itself was skewed. Lesson: no statistical fix can fully compensate for a fundamentally biased sample.

Exam Tip: A common 5-mark question gives you a survey scenario and asks: "Identify all possible sources of error." Practice mapping each of the 4 error types to the scenario. They often hide 2-3 errors in a single paragraph.

Session 1 — Key Takeaways

  • Statistics = Descriptive (summarise) + Inferential (generalise)
  • Variables are either Categorical (Nominal/Ordinal) or Numerical (Discrete/Continuous)
  • NOIR scales: Nominal < Ordinal < Interval < Ratio — each enables more math
  • Population = everyone; Sample = subset. Parameter vs Statistic.
  • Operational definition removes ambiguity — define exactly how you measure
  • Probability sampling (SRS, Systematic, Stratified, Cluster) is preferred over non-probability
  • 4 errors: Coverage, Non-Response, Sampling, Measurement — always identify and minimize
  • The entire pipeline: Question → Define → Frame → Sample → Collect → Check errors → Analyse
