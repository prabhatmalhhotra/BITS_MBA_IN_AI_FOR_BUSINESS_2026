2 Frequency Tables Beginner

Two ways to organise data into frequency tables:

Individual Values

Count frequency of each unique value

  • Works well for few distinct values
  • Shortcoming: Too many rows if values are all unique (e.g., salary data with 500 different values)

Class Intervals

Group values into ranges (bins)

  • Works well for continuous data
  • Shortcoming: Individual values are lost once grouped

3 Column Chart vs Histogram Intermediate

Professor made a clear distinction that many students confuse:

Bar / Column Chart

  • X-axis: Nominal or Ordinal data
  • Bars have gaps between them
  • Categories can be reordered
  • E.g., Sales by product category

Histogram

  • X-axis: Interval or Ratio data
  • Bars have NO gaps (continuous scale)
  • Order is fixed (numeric sequence)
  • E.g., Frequency of income ranges
Exam Tip: The presence or absence of gaps is the visual giveaway. Gaps = categorical (Bar chart). No gaps = numeric continuous (Histogram). Professor stressed this distinction.

Frequency Polygon

Professor explained: a Frequency Polygon smoothens the histogram by connecting midpoints of the bars with lines. Its main advantage: you can overlay two frequency polygons on the same chart to compare two distributions, which is hard to do with overlapping histograms.

6 Analysing a Histogram Intermediate

Professor gave a systematic checklist for reading any histogram:

#What to Look ForWhat It Tells You
1Number of PeaksUnimodal, Bimodal, or Multimodal?
2Number of TailsOne tail or two? Which direction?
3Concentration around peakTight (low variance) or spread out (high variance)?
4SymmetrySymmetric (normal) or skewed (left/right)?
5Smooth vs truncated tailsNatural tapering or abrupt cutoff?
6Acceptable limitsIs data within specification/tolerance?
7Rejection bumpsSmall bumps outside main distribution (defects, anomalies)

Two or More Line Charts (Comparing Series)

When overlaying multiple time series, professor highlighted these analyses:

  • Intersection: Where two lines cross (e.g., break-even point, supply = demand)
  • Vertical distance: Gap between lines at any point in time (e.g., profit = revenue - cost)
  • Horizontal distance: Time lag between similar events on two series
  • Area between curves: Cumulative difference over a period
Professor's Multi-Series Examples

Temperature: Delhi vs Mumbai vs London across months (seasonal patterns, crossings).
Break-even analysis: Revenue line crossing cost line.
Supply/Demand: Equilibrium at intersection.
Cricket: Run rates of two teams during a chase (target vs actual runs).

Session 2 Class Notes — Key Takeaways

  • Data Pipeline: Raw data → Organised tables → Pictorial representation
  • Frequency tables: Individual values (too many rows) vs Class intervals (individual values lost)
  • Bar chart vs Histogram: Gaps = categorical (Nominal/Ordinal); No gaps = numeric (Interval/Ratio)
  • Categorical viz: Pie chart, Bar/Column chart, Tree chart (India exports data)
  • Numeric viz: Histogram, Pie, Treemap, Box Plot, Heat Map (India population 2011)
  • Frequency polygon: Smoothens histogram, enables overlapping comparisons
  • Histogram analysis: 7 things to check (peaks, tails, concentration, symmetry, smooth tails, limits, rejection bumps)
  • Distribution shapes: Normal, Skewed (L/R), Bimodal, Multimodal, Uniform, Exponential, Log-Normal, Power Law
  • Two-variable: Cat+Cat = Contingency table; Num+Num = Scatter; Cat+Num = Grouped plots
  • Time series analysis: 9 things to check (peaks, cycles, randomness, slope, d²y/dx², inflection, outliers, area, target)
  • Multiple line charts: Intersection, vertical/horizontal distance, area between curves
  • HW-01 assigned: Excel-based with 11 worksheets covering all chart types
