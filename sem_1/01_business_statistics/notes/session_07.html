Sampling Distributions

The bridge between a single sample and the population. The Central Limit Theorem is arguably the most important result in all of statistics.

T1 Chapter 7 (Levine) Mid-Sem Syllabus ~45 min read
Class Notes AI Notes
Real-World Problem

Swiggy: Can we trust the average delivery time from a sample of 100 orders?

Swiggy wants to report average delivery time in their quarterly earnings. They cannot measure all 3 crore monthly orders, so they sample 100. The sample mean is 32 minutes. But another sample of 100 might give 31 or 33 minutes. How reliable is any single sample mean?

The sampling distribution tells you how much your sample statistic will vary from sample to sample. The CLT guarantees this distribution is approximately normal, even if individual delivery times are wildly skewed.

1 What Is a Sampling Distribution? Beginner

A Sampling Distribution is the probability distribution of a statistic (like the sample mean) computed from repeated random samples of size n from the same population.

Population (μ, σ)
True but unknown parameters
▼
Take many samples of size n
Sample 1, Sample 2, Sample 3, ...
▼
Compute statistic from each
X̄1, X̄2, X̄3, ...
▼
Sampling distribution of X̄
Distribution of all possible sample means
Daily Life Example

Imagine 50 students in your BITS batch each independently survey 30 Swiggy orders and compute the average delivery time. You will get 50 different means. If you plot those 50 means on a histogram, that histogram approximates the sampling distribution. It will be roughly bell-shaped and centred around the true population mean.

2 Sampling Distribution of the Mean Intermediate

E(X̄) = μ    (unbiased: the average of all sample means equals the population mean)

σ(X̄) = σ / √n    (Standard Error of the Mean)

The Standard Error decreases as n increases. Larger samples give more precise estimates — but precision improves with √n, not n. To halve the standard error, you need to quadruple the sample size.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

For n ≥ 30 (or any n if population is normal):

X̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n)   approximately

Regardless of the shape of the population distribution!

This is revolutionary. Even if delivery times follow a crazy right-skewed distribution, the average of 30+ delivery times will be approximately normally distributed. This justifies using Z-tests and t-tests.

Population
Any shape (skewed, bimodal, uniform...)
→
Sample means (n ≥ 30)
CLT kicks in
→
Approximately Normal
Centred at μ, spread σ/√n
Trending Example (2024)

Election exit polls in India (2024 Lok Sabha): Agencies like Axis My India and C-Voter sampled ~50,000 voters each across 543 constituencies. The CLT is why they could predict national vote share within ±2% despite India's enormous diversity. Each constituency sample mean (vote share) was approximately normal, enabling reliable confidence intervals.

3 Sampling Distribution of the Proportion Intermediate

E(p̂) = π    (population proportion)

σ(p̂) = √[π(1-π)/n]

Approximately normal when nπ ≥ 5 AND n(1-π) ≥ 5
Daily Life Example

You survey 200 people about whether they prefer PhonePe over Google Pay. If the true proportion is π = 0.60, then the standard error of p̂ is √(0.60 × 0.40 / 200) = 0.0346. By CLT, your sample proportion will be approximately N(0.60, 0.0346²). There is a 95% chance your sample proportion falls between 0.60 ± 1.96(0.0346) = 0.53 to 0.67.

4 Finite Population Correction Advanced

FPC = √[(N - n) / (N - 1)]

Apply when n/N > 5% (sampling more than 5% of the population)

Adjusted SE = (σ / √n) × FPC

When you sample a large fraction of the population, there is less variability than infinite-population formulas suggest. The FPC shrinks the standard error.

Exam Tip: The CLT statement ("for n ≥ 30, sampling distribution of mean is approximately normal regardless of population shape") is a frequent short-answer question. Also, be ready to compute standard error and use it in Z-score problems: Z = (X̄ - μ) / (σ/√n). Remember to divide σ by √n — a very common mistake is forgetting the √n.

Session 7 — Key Takeaways

  • Sampling distribution = distribution of a statistic across all possible samples of size n
  • E(X̄) = μ (unbiased); σ(X̄) = σ/√n (standard error decreases with √n)
  • CLT: for n ≥ 30, X̄ is approximately normal regardless of population shape
  • To halve SE, quadruple n (precision improves with √n, not n)
  • Proportion: σ(p̂) = √[π(1-π)/n]; normal when nπ ≥ 5 and n(1-π) ≥ 5
  • FPC = √[(N-n)/(N-1)]; apply when sampling > 5% of population
  • The CLT is the foundation for everything in Sessions 8-11 (CI, hypothesis tests, ANOVA)
