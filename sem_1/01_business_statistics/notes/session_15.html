Introduction to Linear Programming

Optimisation under constraints — how to make the best decision when resources are limited. From product mix to portfolio allocation.

T2 / Class NotesComprehensive Exam~50 min read
Real-World Problem

Amul: What product mix maximises profit given limited milk supply?

Amul has 10,000 litres of milk daily. They can make butter (profit ₹40/kg, needs 5L milk) or cheese (profit ₹60/kg, needs 8L milk). Labour is limited to 800 hours. How much of each should they produce to maximise profit?

This is a classic Linear Programming problem: maximise profit subject to resource constraints. LP provides the mathematically optimal answer.

1 LP Setup Beginner

Every LP problem has four components:

Decision Variables
What to decide (x1, x2, ...)
+
Objective Function
Max or Min (linear in variables)
+
Constraints
Resource limits (≤, ≥, =)
+
Non-negativity
xᵢ ≥ 0

Standard Form Example

Maximise Z = 40x₁ + 30x₂   (profit)

Subject to:
4x₁ + 5x₂ ≤ 200   (machine hours)
2x₁ + 3x₂ ≤ 100   (labour hours)
x₁, x₂ ≥ 0

2 Graphical Solution (2 Variables) Intermediate

Step 1: Plot each constraint as a line
Convert inequality to equation, find x and y intercepts
▼
Step 2: Identify feasible region
Area satisfying ALL constraints simultaneously
▼
Step 3: Find corner (extreme) points
Intersections of constraint lines within feasible region
▼
Step 4: Evaluate Z at each corner
Plug each corner point into objective function
▼
Optimal solution = corner with best Z
Fundamental Theorem: optimal is always at a corner point
Daily Life Example

Meal planning on a budget: You want to maximise nutrition (protein) from two foods: dal (₹30/kg, 25g protein) and paneer (₹300/kg, 18g protein). Budget constraint: ₹600/week. Fridge space constraint: 3 kg max. LP tells you the optimal mix to maximise protein within your budget and fridge limits.

3 Sensitivity Analysis Advanced

ConceptMeaningBusiness Use
Shadow PriceImprovement in Z per unit increase of a binding constraint's RHS"How much is one more machine hour worth?"
Reduced CostPenalty per unit of a non-basic variable forced into solution"How much would profit need to improve for us to produce this product?"
Allowable Increase/DecreaseRange over which current optimal basis stays valid"How much can costs change before we need to re-optimise?"
Trending Example (2024)

Indian Railways uses LP for seat allocation: IRCTC uses linear programming variants to allocate seats across quotas (General, Tatkal, Ladies, Defence) to maximise revenue while meeting policy constraints. Shadow prices tell them the revenue impact of adding one more seat to a quota — this guides investment in adding coaches to high-demand routes.

4 Special Cases Intermediate

CaseWhat HappensHow to Detect
Multiple optimal solutionsObjective function parallel to a binding constraintTwo adjacent corners give same Z value
InfeasibilityNo point satisfies all constraintsNo feasible region exists
UnboundedFeasible region is open; Z can increase foreverUsually a modelling error (missing constraint)

Exam Tip: LP questions in the comprehensive exam typically involve: (1) formulating the problem (define variables, objective, constraints), (2) graphical solution for 2-variable problems, (3) interpreting Solver output (shadow prices, reduced costs). Practice formulation from word problems — that is where most students lose marks.

Session 15 — Key Takeaways

  • LP components: decision variables, objective function, constraints, non-negativity
  • Graphical method: plot constraints, find feasible region, evaluate Z at corners
  • Fundamental Theorem: optimal solution is always at an extreme (corner) point
  • Shadow price = value of one more unit of a binding constraint
  • Reduced cost = how much a non-basic variable's coefficient must improve
  • Special cases: multiple optima, infeasibility, unboundedness
  • Excel Solver (Simplex LP method) is the primary tool for exams
