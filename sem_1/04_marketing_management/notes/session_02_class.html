2 Customer vs Consumer

Customer

Who purchases your product. The person who pays.

Consumer

Who uses your product. The person who actually consumes.

Slide Example

A parent buying diapers: the parent is the customer (purchaser) while the baby is the consumer (user). This distinction matters because marketing communication may need to target different people.

3 What is Consumer Behaviour?

The study of how individuals, groups, and organizations select, buy, use, and dispose of goods, services, ideas, or experiences to satisfy their needs and wants.

Consumer behaviour is influenced by cultural, social, and personal factors (cultural and social are external; personal is internal).

Learning Objectives (4 Key Questions)

  1. How do consumer characteristics influence buying behavior?
  2. What major psychological processes influence consumer responses to the marketing program?
  3. How do consumers make purchasing decisions?
  4. In what ways do consumers stray from a deliberative, rational decision process?

5 Cultural Factors

FactorDefinition
01. CultureIdeas, customs and social behaviour of a particular people or society
02. Sub CultureA distinct group within a larger society that shares a set of beliefs, values, and customs that differ from the dominant culture
03. Social ClassesA division of society based on economic and social status — lower, middle class, upper class, rich. Measured through Socio-Economic Classification (SEC)

6 Social Economic Classification (SEC)

Originally created by Indian Market Research Bureau (IMRB) in 1988.

Urban Grid

  • Old criteria: Education and Occupational criteria (9 groups)
  • New criteria (Govt. of India): Education and ownership of consumer durables (12 groups) — Electricity, gas connection, ceiling fan, 2-wheeler, CTV, Ref, WM, Computer, Car, Air conditioner, Land
  • Occupation Grid for ISEC-Urban: Unskilled Worker (1), Skilled Worker (2), Petty Traders (3), Shop Owners (4), Businessman/Industrialist without employees (5), with 1-9 employees (6), with 10+ employees (7), Self Employed Professional (8), Clerk/Salesman (9), Supervisory Level (10), Officer/Executive Junior (11), Officer/Executive Middle/Senior (12), Owner Farmer (13), Leased Farmer (14)

Rural Grid (4 groups)

  • Education
  • Type of Dwelling (pucca, semi-pucca, katcha)

Household Potential Index (HPI)

Calculated for urban consumers by taking into account possession of particular consumer durable items. An item in more common usage (TV) fetched a low score while one less popular (AC) got a higher score. Education was factored into the score.

HPI Data from Slides

SEC A1 = 1% of households but 39% of HPITop1%. SEC A2 = 1.8%. SEC E+R3 = 35.3% of households but only 1% of HPITop1%. This shows extreme concentration of purchasing power at the top SEC segments.

7 Social Factors: What Influences Consumer Behaviour?

Four key social factors:

  • Reference Groups
  • Cliques
  • Family
  • Roles and Status

What are Reference Groups?

Any individual or group that significantly influences a person's evaluations, aspirations, or behavior. They serve as points of comparison and sources of information, shaping how consumers perceive products, brands, and even their own self-concept.

a) Direct Reference Groups

Groups with whom the individual interacts through direct (face to face) or indirect interaction:

PRIMARY

Family, Friends, Neighbors

  • "I use Nike because all my friends use it."
  • "I bought an SUV because everyone in my neighborhood has one."

SECONDARY

Religious, Professional, Ethnic groups

  • "I buy the medicine the doctor prescribes. I even avoid other brands of the same chemical." (That is why pharma marketing is doctor-centric.)
  • "I eat only vegetarian/halal/kosher food because of my religious denomination."

b) Indirect Reference Groups

  • Aspirational Groups: Group a person hopes to join. Eg: IIT, Brand Ambassadors
  • Dissociative Groups: Group a person rejects. Eg: Drug addicts
  • Opinion Leaders: Person who offers informal advice or information about a specific product. Eg: Dentist for dental hygiene, IT professional for a PC, Painter for paints, Mason for cement/sanitary ware
Slide Example

UltraTech Cement: "Strong enough to uphold an Engineer's promise" — "The Engineer's Choice." This ad targets masons and engineers as opinion leaders who recommend cement brands to end consumers (home builders).

8 Cliques

Cliques are small groups whose members interact frequently, playing into the law of few:

  • Professionals: Doctors in a small city
  • Mavens: People who are knowledgeable
  • Connectors: Connect with many people (press)
  • Salesmen: Persuasive skills (insurance salesmen)
  • Stickiness of Ideas: An idea that would motivate people to act on it

10 Family Role Cannot Be Ignored

Family of Orientation

Parents and siblings. From parents a person acquires an orientation toward religion, politics, economics and a sense of personal ambition, self-worth, and love.

Family of Procreation

Spouse and children — more direct influence on everyday buying behavior.

  • Housewife: Household goods, garments
  • Children as influencers: Mobile phones, PCs, movies, vacations
Slide Example

Dalda Vanaspati: Classic Indian ad — "Mothers who care use Dalda." This ad directly targets the family of procreation, positioning the housewife as the key decision maker for cooking products, appealing to the mother's care for family health.

Session 2 Class Notes — Key Takeaways

  • Customer (purchaser) vs Consumer (user) — critical distinction
  • Consumer behaviour = how individuals/groups/organizations select, buy, use, dispose of goods/services
  • Influenced by Cultural (culture, sub-culture, social class), Social (reference groups, cliques, family, roles/status), and Personal factors
  • SEC Classification: IMRB 1988; Urban (12 groups based on education + consumer durables), Rural (4 groups based on education + dwelling type)
  • HPI measures purchasing power concentration by SEC segment
  • Reference Groups: Direct (Primary = family/friends; Secondary = religious/professional) and Indirect (Aspirational, Dissociative, Opinion Leaders)
  • Cliques: Professionals, Mavens, Connectors, Salesmen, Stickiness of Ideas
  • UltraTech Cement targets engineers/masons as opinion leaders
  • Family: Orientation (parents, religion, values) vs Procreation (spouse, children — direct buying influence)
  • Dalda ad: targeting housewife as key decision maker in family of procreation
