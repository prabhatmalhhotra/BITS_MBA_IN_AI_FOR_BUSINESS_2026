Segmentation & Targeting

You cannot serve everyone equally. The art of marketing is choosing WHO to serve and HOW to divide the market into meaningful groups. This is where strategy begins.

Real-World Problem

How does Maruti Suzuki sell to EVERY income group in India?

Maruti holds ~43% of India's car market. How? By segmenting ruthlessly: Alto/S-Presso for first-time budget buyers, Swift/Baleno for young professionals, Dzire for families wanting a sedan, Brezza/Grand Vitara for SUV aspirers, and Invicto for premium buyers. Same company, completely different products for different segments.

Maruti does not sell "one car for all." It identifies distinct customer segments, targets each with a specific product, and positions each uniquely. That is the STP framework — Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning.

1 The STP Framework Beginner

STP is the most important strategic framework in marketing. Everything else (4Ps, communications, pricing) follows from STP decisions.

S - Segmentation
Divide the market into distinct groups
→
T - Targeting
Choose which segments to serve
→
P - Positioning
Create a distinct image in target minds

This session covers S and T. Positioning comes in Sessions 5-6.

2 Bases of Segmentation Intermediate

How do you divide millions of consumers into meaningful groups? Kotler identifies 4 major bases:

BaseVariablesExample
GeographicCountry, region, city size, density, climateAmul Kool sells more in hot regions; Winterwear brands focus on North India
DemographicAge, gender, income, family size, occupation, education, religion, generationDove targets women 25-45; Royal Enfield targets men 25-40 with Rs 8L+ income
PsychographicLifestyle (AIO), values (VALS), personalityHarley-Davidson = rebellious individualists; Fabindia = conscious, traditional-modern
BehaviouralOccasion, benefits sought, usage rate, loyalty, buyer-readinessCadbury segments by occasion: Celebrations (gifting), Silk (self-indulgence), 5 Star (snacking)
Daily Life Example

Think about how Netflix segments you: Demographic (age, language preference), Behavioural (watch history, binge patterns), and Psychographic (thriller lover vs comedy lover). That is why your homepage looks completely different from your parents' — different segments, different recommendations.

3 Requirements for Effective Segmentation: MADAS Intermediate

Not every way of dividing a market is useful. Effective segments must pass the MADAS test:

M - Measurable
Can you measure size and purchasing power?
A - Accessible
Can you reach and serve this segment?
D - Differentiable
Do segments respond differently to marketing?
A - Actionable
Can you design programs for them?
S - Substantial
Is it large/profitable enough?
Exam Tip: MADAS is a very common exam question. You may be given a segmentation scenario and asked to evaluate whether segments are effective. Apply each criterion with reasoning.

4 Targeting Strategies Intermediate

Once you have identified segments, you must choose which to target. There are 4 approaches:

StrategyWhat It MeansExampleWhen to Use
Undifferentiated (Mass)One product for entire marketCoca-Cola (historically), Tata SaltProduct has universal appeal, economies of scale
DifferentiatedDifferent offerings for multiple segmentsMaruti (Alto to Invicto), HUL (Lux to Lifebuoy)Multiple attractive segments, resources to serve each
Concentrated (Niche)Focus all resources on one segmentRoyal Enfield (premium cruisers), Forest Essentials (luxury Ayurveda)Limited resources, one highly profitable segment
Customised (Micro)Tailored for individual customersNike By You (custom shoes), Bombay Shaving Co. subscription boxesHigh-value customers willing to pay premium
Trending Example (2024-25)

D2C brands and hyper-segmentation: Brands from Shark Tank India like Perfora (premium oral care), Atomberg (energy-efficient fans), and Anveshan (pure farm products) use concentrated targeting via Instagram/Facebook ads. They pick one specific psychographic segment (health-conscious urban millennials) and serve them deeply, rather than trying to be everything to everyone.

5 Case Study: Mahindra XYLO Advanced

R1 Case 9: Who will buy XYLO? — This is the mandatory case for this session.

Key Questions to Prepare

  • What segments was Mahindra targeting with XYLO? (Large families needing space + small commercial operators)
  • Why was the segmentation challenging? (Overlap between personal and commercial use blurred the targeting)
  • What bases of segmentation apply? (Demographic: family size, income; Behavioural: usage occasion; Geographic: semi-urban/rural)
  • Was XYLO's targeting differentiated or concentrated?
  • What went wrong with XYLO's positioning? (Straddled between MUV and SUV, confusing consumers)
Daily Life Example

XYLO's problem is like a restaurant trying to be both a fine-dining place and a dhaba. You confuse both segments. The learning: clear targeting prevents positioning confusion. Compare with Tata Nano — positioned as "cheapest car" alienated everyone who did not want to be seen in the cheapest car.

Session 4 — Key Takeaways Checklist

  • STP = Segmentation → Targeting → Positioning (most important marketing framework)
  • 4 bases of segmentation: Geographic, Demographic, Psychographic, Behavioural
  • MADAS criteria: Measurable, Accessible, Differentiable, Actionable, Substantial
  • 4 targeting strategies: Undifferentiated, Differentiated, Concentrated, Customised
  • Maruti = differentiated targeting; Royal Enfield = concentrated; Tata Salt = undifferentiated
  • Mahindra XYLO case: blurred segmentation between personal and commercial use caused positioning confusion
  • B2B segmentation uses industry, operating variables, purchasing approach, situational factors
