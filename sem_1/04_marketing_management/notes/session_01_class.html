1 Course Outline & Value of Marketing

The course covers: Introduction to Marketing, Marketing Strategies and Plans, Consumer Behaviour, Segmentation Targeting and Positioning, Brand Positioning, Product Management, Pricing, Distribution and Logistics, and Promotion.

What is the Value of Marketing?

Marketing works in conjunction with other departments like finance, operations, accounts and other business activities to generate demand for products and services so that the firm can earn a profit.

Profit = Revenue - Cost
Valuation = Shares * Price of shares
Eg: 1m shares * 1000 = Rs. 100 crore
Slide Example

Unilever (HUL): Builds its bottom line on the back of its brands — Lifebuoy, Dove, TRESemme, Lakme, Pepsodent, Rexona, Axe, Pond's, Lux, Fair & Lovely, Toni&Guy, Closeup, Elle 18, Hamam, Pears, Vaseline, Sunsilk, Clinic Plus, Sure, Indulekha, Ayush, Breeze, Liril, Brylcreem.

2 What Should Marketing Do For You?

The goal of marketing is to build brands and a loyal customer base for the company; intangible assets which contribute heavily to the value of a firm.

What is a Brand? (AMA Definition)

"A brand is a name, term, design, symbol, or any other feature that identifies one seller's good or service as distinct from those of other sellers" — American Marketing Association
Prof. Sagar's Example

WhatsApp vs Signal: Both are messaging apps with similar features, but WhatsApp's brand recognition gives it a massive advantage. Why having a strong brand matters — it creates differentiation even when the product is similar.

3 What is Marketing at its Core?

Marketing is about identifying and meeting human and social needs in a profitable manner for the firm.

Examples from Slides:

  • P&G: Identifies and meets needs across hundreds of consumer categories
  • Zepto: Quick commerce meeting the need for instant grocery delivery
  • Edu-tech firms: Meeting the need for accessible education
  • Go Zero Ice creams, Minimalist skincare: FMCG meeting new consumer preferences
  • Electrical Vehicles: Human need (affordable, noise-free transport) + Social need (less pollution)

Principles of marketing don't change — skilled marketing is a never-ending pursuit involving the art of adaptability through changing customer needs, technology and environmental factors.

4 Scope of Marketing (AMA Definition)

Marketing is the activity, set of institutions, and processes for creating, communicating, delivering and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners and society at large.
  • Activity: Advertising, Selling, Product Management (product/package design), Pricing etc.
  • Set of Institutions: Marketing departments, Ad Agencies, Modelling Agencies, Market Research Firms, Event Management Companies, Transport Companies, Retailers
  • Processes: Research Inputs → Product Design → Product Launch → Response Measurement etc.

Two Definitions of Marketing

Managerial Definition

Marketing is the art and science of choosing target markets and getting, keeping and growing customers through creating, delivering and communicating superior customer value.

Societal Definition

Marketing is a societal process by which individuals and groups obtain what they need and want through creating, offering and freely exchanging products and services of value with others.

5 Marketing is NOT Selling

A common management misconception. Selling at best is an activity associated with marketing. Marketing is far broader.

9 What is a Market Actually?

Economics Perspective

A collection of buyers and sellers who transact over a particular product or product class.

Marketing Perspective

Used to describe various groupings of customers. The automobile market = set of people interested in buying an automobile.

Structure of Flows in a Modern Exchange Economy

Five key entities: Resource Markets (Land, Labour, Capital) → Manufacturer → Government → Consumer → Intermediary. Money, goods, services, taxes flow between them.

10 Key Customer Markets

  • Consumer: Buyer is the end customer
  • B2B: Business 1 sells to Business 2 which uses the product to meet its customers' demands
  • Global: Challenges of customisation, localization (cars in India need high ground clearance; no market for beef burgers in India)
  • Non-profit and Govt Markets: NGOs (CRY, Helpage), tenders

12 STP Introduction & Product Pyramid

  • Segmentation: Grouping of customers — demographic, psychographic etc.
  • Target: Choosing which segment to serve
  • Positioning: Volvo = safe cars; BMW = driving pleasure
Product Pyramid

HUL Beauty (differentiable product): Skin Cleansing has Lifebuoy, Lux, Pears, Dove, Hamam, Rexona, Liril, VWash. Hair Care has Clinic Plus, Dove, Sunsilk, TRESemme, Indulekha.

Tata Salt (non-differentiable product): Super Premium (>4.0X), Premium (1.0-3.0X), Popular (1.0X — Tata Salt, Shuddh), Mass (0.6X — Tata Salt Crystal, Shakti). Even for a commodity like salt, brand tiers create value.

13 Core Marketing Concepts (Slides 26-43)

Offerings & Brands

  • Value Proposition: Set of benefits satisfying customer needs (quality medical service; innovative electronics with after-sales)
  • Offering: Combination of products, services, information, experiences — gives physical shape to the value proposition (Apollo Hospitals; SONY)
  • Brand: An offering from a known (identified) source

Marketing Channels

  • Distribution: Dealers, Distributors, Retailers
  • Service: Warehouses, Transport companies, Banks, Insurance
  • Communication: Print, electronic, digital media, stores

Types of Media

  • Paid: Advertisements
  • Owned: Company brochures, website
  • Earned: Word of Mouth

Impressions & Engagement

Impression = consumer views a communication. Engagement = customer's attention and action involvement (Facebook "likes").

Value and Satisfaction

Value = combination of Features, Quality, Service, Price (FQSP) — the customer value triad. Satisfaction = perceived performance vs expectation.

Supply Chain

Raw material → component → finished product → final buyer. Example: Steel — Coal/Iron ore/Limestone → Steel Plant → Stock Yard → Dealer → Consumer.

Competition

All actual and potential rival offerings or substitutes. Toyota vs Honda (rival), Tesla vs Honda in India (potential rival), Metro Rail (substitute), Internet (potential substitute).
