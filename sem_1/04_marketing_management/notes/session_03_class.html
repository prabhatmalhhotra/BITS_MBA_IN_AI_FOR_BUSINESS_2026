Consumer Behaviour (Part 2)

Personal factors, key psychological processes (Motivation, Perception, Learning, Emotions, Memory), the buying decision process, and behavioral economics.

Lecture 3 (L3) Mid-Sem Syllabus

1 Personal Factors Influencing Consumer Behaviour

Age & Life Cycle

  • Childhood — Cakes, Chocolates, Books, Cartoons
  • Young adults — Jeans, Bikes, T-shirts, Movies
  • Middle Ages — Diet, Gym, Sugar-free products
  • Old Age — Health Care

Lifecycle Stages

  • First Job — Insurance, Bike/Car, House
  • Marriage — Household items
  • Child Birth — Infant items (diapers, disinfectants)

Occupation

  • Self Improvement Products (Coursera, Udemy)
  • Tools related to occupation (Laptops, Stethoscopes, Calculators)

Economic Circumstances

Times of boom and recession — recreational expenses go up during "booms".

Personality & Brand Personality

A set of distinguishing human psychological characteristics that lead to relatively consistent and enduring responses to environmental stimuli. People choose brands consistent with their actual or ideal self-concept.

Lifestyle Influence

A person's pattern of living as expressed in activities, interests and opinions:

  • Achievers: Personal computers
  • Money constrained: Affordable products (Garib Rath)
  • Time constrained: Packaged food industry, Zomato
  • Core Values: Simple living and high thinking. "Dress is Address" (Santoor)

2 Model of Consumer Behaviour

The complete model shows: Marketing Stimuli (Products & services, Price, Distribution, Communications) + Other Stimuli (Economic, Technological, Political, Cultural) → Consumer Psychology (Motivation, Perception, Learning, Memory) + Consumer Characteristics (Cultural, Social, Personal) → Buying Decision Process (Problem Recognition, Information Search, Evaluation, Purchase Decision, Post-purchase Behaviour) → Purchase Decision (Product choice, Brand choice, Dealer choice, Purchase amount, Purchase timing, Payment method).

Slide Example: Delhi Family Buys SUV

Marketing Stimuli: Cars & SUVs, Rs. 5-15L, Dealerships, TV/Print/Digital Ads. Other Stimuli: Low EMI (economy), Connected Car (tech), Ban 10-yr vehicles in Delhi (political), Old style machismo (cultural). Consumer Characteristics: Delhi culture, Peers/Neighbours as ref group, Adventure seeking (personal). Buying Process: Ageing car (problem), Internet/Car Owners/Ads (search), Evaluation, Purchase (Navratri timing), Cognitive Dissonance/Resonance (post-purchase). Decision: 1500cc diesel Maruti Vitara, Arora Motors dealer, One car, 2nd week Oct, EMI from SBI.

3 Key Psychological Processes

Five processes: Motivation (first impression) → Perception → Learning → Emotions → Memory (making impressions last).

a. Motivation

A need becomes a motive when it is aroused to a sufficient level of intensity to drive us to act.

Three theories of motivation:

TheoryCore IdeaTechniques
Freud's TheoryBehavior guided by the SubconsciousProjective techniques (Sentence Completion, Picture Interpretation, Role Playing), Laddering
Maslow's HierarchyBehavior driven by lowest unmet needPhysiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-actualization
Herzberg's Two-FactorBehavior guided by Dissatisfiers and SatisfiersAbsence of dissatisfier + presence of satisfier
Laddering Example: Coaching Centre

Why costly coaching? "I want him to clear IIT and become an engineer." What's good about engineering? "Coaching is costly, too many engineers, son plays guitar..." Why important? "Want son well settled, respected. Music is risky, drugs in that industry." — Laddering reveals the subconscious motive: social status and safety, not just career.

Herzberg's Theory Applied

Workplace: Dissatisfiers = Salary, Physical Workplace, Colleague relationships, Supervisor relationships. Satisfiers = Performance, Recognition, Job Status, Responsibility.
Hospital: Dissatisfiers = Physical Workplace, Waiting time, Staff Behaviour. Satisfiers = Quality of Treatment, Affordability.

b. Perception

The process by which we select, organize, and interpret information inputs to create a meaningful picture of the world.

  • Selective Attention: Relevance (people notice ads for products they want; Google Ads tagged to searches), Anticipate (chocolate not noticeable in a chemist shop), High Deviation (40% off more pronounced than 20%)
  • Selective Distortion: Consumers distort information to fit prior brand experiences; "loser" brands struggle with this
  • Selective Retention: Smart one-liners, catchy jingles, memorable imagery
  • Subliminal Perception: Subconscious messages (soft drinks between movie frames); Colors (light blue/white = peace)

c. Learning

Induces changes in behavior arising from experience. (Eg: Tata Motors needs to change customer perception to gain market share.)

  • Drive and Cues: Drive = strong internal impulse (Domino's "Hungry Kya?"); Cue = minor external stimulus. Marketers use drive/cue followed by positive reinforcement.
  • Generalization: Putting things in broad categories (all car manufacturers offer warranty)
  • Discrimination: Recognizing differences to induce switch (only Hyundai offers 10 years of mental peace)

d. Emotions

Many different kinds of emotions can be linked to brands:

  • Axe: created male body wash category by boosting confidence of young men (18-24)
  • Hamara Bajaj: nostalgia and national pride
  • IIT JEE results: emotion of achievement/aspiration

e. Memory

  • Short-term vs Long-term memory: Visual, Verbal, Abstract, Contextual
  • Associative Network Memory Model: Brand Associations = all thoughts, feelings, perceptions, images associated with a brand (Ford Fiesta = Small Car, Good Gas Mileage, Fun to Drive, Small Back Seat)
  • Memory Encoding: How consumers process and retain info — encoding through understanding is more efficient (Nirma "Shuddh Shakti", Oreo, Royal Enfield)
  • Memory Retrieval: External/internal stimuli that help access stored memories. Time leads to decay. Accessibility at right time matters (near checkout/Point of Sale ads)
Slide Example: Apollo Hospital Noida

Brand association network: Noida Apollo Hospital → Friendly, Clean, Responsive, Costly, Small, Under-equipped. The hospital positions itself as "Friendly Neighbourhood Hospital." Marketers manage these brand associations.

4 The Buying Decision Process (5 Stages)

1. Problem Recognition
Need triggered by internal/external stimuli
▼
2. Information Search
Personal, Commercial, Public, Experiential sources
▼
3. Evaluation of Alternatives
Total Set → Awareness → Consideration → Choice → Decision
▼
4. Purchase Decision
Intervening factors: attitudes of others, unanticipated situations
▼
5. Post-Purchase Behaviour
Satisfaction, Actions (repeat/abandon), Uses & Disposal

Information Search Sources

  • Personal: Friends, Relatives, Colleagues (ask a car user)
  • Commercial: Advertising, Websites, Salespersons
  • Public: Social Media, Press, Consumer Rating Orgs (Reddit, Quora, Auto Car magazine, JD Power Rating)
  • Experiential: Handling, Examining, Using the product (Test Drive)

Purchase Decision Models

Compensatory Model (Expectancy-Value): Weighted scores across attributes. Eg: Laptop with Memory(0.4), Graphics(0.3), Size(0.2), Price(0.1) — Brand A scores 8.0, Brand B 7.0.

Non-Compensatory Models:

  • Conjunctive: Minimum cutoff for all attributes (Brand B if cutoff is 7)
  • Lexicographic: Best on most important attribute (Brand C with Memory=10)
  • Elimination-by-aspects: Compress shortlists by aspects in order of importance (Brand A)

Risk TypeDescription

Post-Purchase Behaviour

  • Satisfaction: Depends on Expectation vs Performance. Be careful about expectations you raise.
  • Actions: Repeat purchase or abandonment, good or bad Word of Mouth (Michelin Star restaurants)
  • Uses & Disposal: Keep, Get rid temporarily (rent/lend), Get rid permanently (give away/trade/sell). Platforms: OLX, refurbished laptops/mobile phones.

5 Behavioral Economics: Heuristics

Methods of taking decisions based on personal experiences:

  • Availability Heuristic: How easily customers remember an outcome (auto company with poor image offers extended warranty)
  • Representativeness Heuristic: How representative is the product (all 5-star hotels offer discounts; all mall products are expensive)
  • Anchoring & Adjustment: First impression then adjustments (service industry; first impression of a coaching class)
  • Mental Accounting: "A meal should cost Rs. 500"; lost movie ticket Rs. 200 vs lost Rs. 200 cash — different psychological impact

Consumer Behaviour Patterns

  • Consumers tend to segregate gains (focus on each product feature separately)
  • Consumers tend to integrate losses (car accessories sell best at time of new purchase)
  • Consumers tend to integrate large gains with smaller losses (EMIs deducted at salary time)
  • Consumers tend to segregate small gains from large losses (look for silver lining)

6 Low Involvement & Variety-Seeking

  • Low-involvement: Low priced, frequent purchases — decisions based on peripheral cues (catchy jingle, pretty model). High involvement = more rational.
  • Variety-Seeking: Consumers switch brands easily for low involvement products. Leader defends by dominating shelf space.

  • 5-Stage Buying Process: Problem Recognition → Information Search → Evaluation → Purchase Decision → Post-Purchase
  • Decision Models: Compensatory (expectancy-value) vs Non-Compensatory (conjunctive, lexicographic, elimination-by-aspects)
  • 6 types of perceived risk: Functional, Physical, Time, Financial, Psychological, Social
  • Behavioral Economics: Availability, Representativeness, Anchoring, Mental Accounting heuristics
  • Consumers segregate gains, integrate losses
